Module 1 — How To Think About Prompts
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SLIDES
Slide 1 — Fact — What A Prompt Actually Is
A prompt is not a search query. It's not a command. It's the entire context a language model has when generating a response — everything it knows about what you want, who you are, what format you need, and what constraints apply. Most people treat prompts like Google searches — a few words and hope for the best. The people who get consistently good results treat prompts like instructions to a capable but literal assistant who knows nothing about you except what you tell them.
Slide 2 — Example — The Difference Context Makes
"Write a summary" produces something generic. "Write a three-sentence summary of the following text for a 10th-grade reading level, focusing on the main argument and ignoring examples" produces something useful. The second prompt isn't longer for the sake of it; every added word narrows the space of possible outputs toward what you actually want. Specificity isn't overhead you add before the real work begins — it's how the work gets done well.
Slide 3 — Hot take — The Model Is Not Psychic
The most common prompting mistake is assuming the model knows things it doesn't. It doesn't know your audience, your tone preferences, what you've already tried, or what "good" means in your context. Everything the model needs to know has to be in the prompt. If the output is wrong, the most likely explanation isn't that the model is bad — it's that the prompt didn't give it enough to work with..
Slide 4 — Fact — The Mental Model
Think of prompting as hiring a contractor. You tell them what you want, what constraints apply, what the deliverable looks like, and what success means. A good contractor with a bad brief produces bad work. A good contractor with a clear brief produces good work. The model is the contractor. The prompt is the brief. Your job is not to hope the model figures out what you meant. Your job is to make what you mean unambiguous.
Slide 5 — Example — System Prompts vs User Prompts
Most AI APIs separate the prompt into parts. The system prompt sets context that applies to the entire conversation — who the AI is, what it knows, what rules apply, what format to use. The user prompt is the specific request for this turn. A well-designed system prompt means you don't have to re-explain context every time. Building real applications means almost always using a system prompt to establish the baseline behavior you want.
Slide 6 — Hot take — Iteration Is The Skill
The first prompt rarely produces the best output. The skill is not writing perfect prompts on the first try — it's knowing how to iterate. You run the prompt, look at what was wrong, identify what context was missing or what instruction was ambiguous, update the prompt, and run it again. Prompt engineering is a debugging process. The output is your feedback on whether the prompt was clear. Treating iteration as failure misunderstands what the skill actually is.
Slide 7 — Scenario — When Prompting Fails
Some tasks are hard to get right with prompting alone. If you need consistent formatting across hundreds of outputs, prompting is fragile — the model will sometimes deviate. If you need the model to know specific facts it wasn't trained on, prompting can't fix that — you need RAG. If you need a specific style or persona maintained perfectly across many interactions, prompting is inconsistent — you need fine-tuning. Understanding when prompting is the right tool and when you need something else is as important as knowing how to prompt well.
Slide 8 — Big idea — Language Is The Interface
For the first time in the history of computing, the primary interface to the most powerful software tools is natural language. You don't need to learn syntax. You don't need to understand data structures. You need to be able to communicate precisely what you want in plain language. This sounds easy. It's actually a skill — the skill of making implicit knowledge explicit, of specifying constraints you usually leave unstated, of describing success clearly enough that a system with no common sense can achieve it. That skill is new and it matters.

Module 2 — Prompt Engineering
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SLIDES
Slide 1 — Fact — Techniques That Work
Prompt engineering is not mystical. It's a small set of techniques that reliably improve outputs for specific types of tasks. Few-shot prompting. Chain of thought. Role assignment. Output formatting. Each works for different reasons and different situations. Learning them takes less than an hour. Using them well takes practice.
Slide 2 — Example — Few-Shot Prompting
Instead of explaining what you want in words, show the model examples of what you want. "Here are three examples of the kind of summary I need: [example 1] [example 2] [example 3]. Now summarize this: [text]." The model infers the pattern from the examples rather than from a description. Few-shot prompting works especially well when what you want is easier to demonstrate than to describe — formatting, tone, structure, style.
Slide 3 — Example — Chain Of Thought
For complex reasoning tasks, tell the model to think step by step before giving an answer. "Think through this step by step before giving your final answer." This dramatically improves performance on math problems, logic puzzles, and multi-step reasoning tasks. The model produces better answers when it generates intermediate reasoning steps. Why this works is not fully understood — but empirically it does, reliably, across many task types.
Slide 4 — Example — Role Assignment
"You are an expert tax attorney with 20 years of experience. Answer the following question as you would advise a small business client." Role assignment changes the register, depth, and framing of responses. The model draws on patterns from the relevant domain. It's not magic — the model doesn't become a tax attorney. But it shifts toward the kind of language, caveats, and structure that appear in that domain's training data.
Slide 5 — Fact — Output Formatting
If you need structured output — JSON, a table, a numbered list, a specific schema — specify it explicitly. "Return your answer as a JSON object with fields: title, summary, key_points (array), confidence (0-1)." Models follow format instructions reliably when they're clear. Structured output is essential when AI is part of a larger system — you need to parse the output programmatically. Hoping the model produces parseable output without specifying format is a common failure mode.
Slide 6 — Hot take — Temperature And Randomness
Temperature controls how random the model's outputs are. Low temperature (near 0) produces consistent, deterministic outputs — good for factual tasks, code generation, structured data. High temperature (near 1) produces more varied, creative outputs — good for brainstorming, creative writing, generating diverse options. Most applications use low temperature for reliability. Creative applications use higher temperature for variety. Understanding this parameter is the difference between predictable and unpredictable behavior in your application.
Slide 7 — Scenario — Combining Techniques
The most effective prompts often combine multiple techniques. System prompt establishing role and format. Few-shot examples demonstrating the desired output. Chain of thought instruction for complex reasoning. Explicit output format specification. This sounds like a lot but it becomes natural quickly. A well-engineered prompt for a production application might be 500 words — most of which the user never sees. The work of crafting it is done once and pays off across every interaction.
Slide 8 — Big idea — Prompts As Code
In traditional software, you write code that tells a computer exactly what to do. In AI-powered software, you write prompts that tell a model how to behave. Prompts are code in this sense — they are the instructions that determine system behavior. They need to be versioned, tested, and maintained like code. A change to a prompt can change the behavior of an entire application. The discipline of prompt engineering is new but the underlying principle — write clear instructions, test them, iterate — is as old as programming.

Module 3 — Working With APIs
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SLIDES
Slide 1 — Fact — What An API Is
An API — Application Programming Interface — is a way for your code to talk to someone else's software. AI APIs let you send a prompt and get a response back, programmatically, without using a chat interface. Instead of typing into ChatGPT, you write code that sends text to an endpoint and receives text back. This is how every AI-powered product is built — Notion's AI, GitHub Copilot, Duolingo's AI features, all of them call AI APIs.
Slide 2 — Example — A Basic API Call
The simplest possible call to an AI API: send a list of messages with roles — system, user, assistant — and receive a completion. In Python using the Anthropic SDK: client.messages.create(model="claude-opus-4-6", max_tokens=1000, messages=[{"role": "user", "content": "What is backpropagation?"}]). The response comes back as an object. You extract the text and use it however you need. This is the foundation of every AI application.
Slide 3 — Fact — Tokens And Cost
AI models don't process words — they process tokens, roughly three quarters of a word on average. API pricing is per token — input tokens cost one rate, output tokens another. A 1000-word prompt is roughly 1300 tokens. Generating a 500-word response is roughly 650 tokens. For a production application handling thousands of requests per day, token costs add up. Optimizing prompts for token efficiency — removing unnecessary words, using concise instructions — is part of building real applications.
Slide 4 — Fact — The Context Window
Everything you send in one API call — system prompt, conversation history, user message, any documents — must fit in the context window. If you're building a chatbot you need to include previous messages so the model has conversation history. As conversations get longer they approach the context limit. At that point you need to summarize earlier conversation, truncate old messages, or use retrieval to surface only relevant history. Managing context is one of the core engineering challenges of building AI applications.
Slide 5 — Example — Streaming
For applications where users see the response as it's generated — like a chat interface — streaming lets you receive tokens as they're produced rather than waiting for the complete response. The user sees text appearing word by word rather than waiting for the full response. Streaming dramatically improves perceived performance. Most chat applications use streaming. It requires handling a stream of events rather than a single response object — slightly more complex to implement but standard in production applications.
Slide 6 — Hot take — Rate Limits And Reliability
APIs have rate limits — caps on how many requests you can make per minute or per day. At scale, hitting rate limits breaks your application. Production applications need retry logic — if a request fails, wait and try again. They need fallback behavior — what happens when the API is unavailable. They need timeout handling — what happens when a response takes too long. Building with AI APIs is not just about the happy path. It's about making your application robust when things go wrong.
Slide 7 — Scenario — Choosing A Model
Different models have different capabilities, speeds, and costs. Larger models produce better outputs but cost more and respond more slowly. Smaller models are faster and cheaper but less capable. For many applications the right choice is not the most powerful model — it's the least powerful model that meets your quality threshold. A model that costs ten times less and responds three times faster might be perfectly adequate for your use case. Choosing the right model for each task is an engineering decision, not just a quality decision.
Slide 8 — Big idea — AI As Infrastructure
When you use an AI API you're not using a product — you're using infrastructure. Like calling a database or a payment processor, you're integrating a service into your application that handles a specific capability. The model is not your application. Your application is the logic, interface, and experience you build around the model. This framing matters because it clarifies where the complexity lives — in the prompts, the data pipeline, the user experience, the error handling — not in the model itself.

Module 4 — RAG — Retrieval Augmented Generation
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SLIDES
Slide 1 — Fact — The Hallucination Problem
Language models are trained on data with a cutoff date. They don't know about events after training. They don't know about your company's internal documents. They don't know about the specific customer whose question you're answering. Ask them about any of these things and they'll either say they don't know — or worse, confidently make something up. RAG — Retrieval Augmented Generation — is the standard solution to this problem.
Slide 2 — Example — What RAG Does
Instead of relying on the model's training data, RAG retrieves relevant information from an external source and includes it in the prompt. User asks a question. Your system searches a database of relevant documents. The most relevant chunks are included in the prompt. The model answers based on the retrieved content rather than training data. The model's job shifts from remembering facts to reasoning over provided text. This dramatically reduces hallucination on factual questions.
Slide 3 — Fact — Embeddings
RAG depends on a key technology: embeddings. An embedding is a numerical representation of text — a vector of hundreds of numbers that captures semantic meaning. Similar texts produce similar embeddings. "The dog ran fast" and "the canine moved quickly" produce embeddings that are close together in vector space. Embedding models convert text to vectors. Vector databases store and search these vectors efficiently. When a user asks a question, you embed the question and search for document chunks with similar embeddings.
Slide 4 — Example — The RAG Pipeline
Step by step: index your documents by splitting them into chunks, embedding each chunk, storing chunks and embeddings in a vector database. At query time: embed the user's question, search the vector database for the most similar chunks, retrieve the top results, include them in the prompt with instructions to answer based on this context, generate a response. The entire pipeline can be built with a few hundred lines of code using standard libraries. It's the foundation of most enterprise AI applications.
Slide 5 — Hot take — RAG vs Fine-Tuning
A common question: should you use RAG or fine-tune the model on your data? For most use cases RAG is better. RAG keeps knowledge current because you update the database rather than the model, and it lets you cite sources since you know which documents informed the answer. Fine-tuning makes more sense when you need to change how the model behaves — its style, its persona, its reasoning patterns — rather than what it knows. If the problem is knowledge, reach for RAG. If the problem is behavior, reach for fine-tuning.
Slide 6 — Fact — Chunking Matters
How you split documents into chunks significantly affects RAG quality. Chunks too small lose context. Chunks too large dilute relevance — you retrieve a lot of irrelevant content along with the relevant part. Chunk boundaries matter — splitting a paragraph mid-sentence loses meaning. Overlapping chunks — where each chunk shares some content with adjacent chunks — helps maintain context across boundaries. Chunking strategy is one of the most important and least discussed aspects of RAG system design.
Slide 7 — Scenario — When RAG Fails
RAG fails in predictable ways. The retrieval step fails — the relevant document isn't in the database, or the similarity search doesn't find it. The context window fills up — too many retrieved chunks leave no room for a useful response. The model ignores the retrieved context — it falls back on training data rather than the provided documents. Each failure has a different fix: better indexing, smarter retrieval, more explicit prompting. Debugging RAG systems requires understanding which part of the pipeline failed.
Slide 8 — Big idea — Grounding
The core value of RAG is grounding — connecting the model's outputs to verifiable, specific sources. A grounded answer can be checked. The sources can be cited. The reasoning can be traced. Ungrounded answers from pure language models are fluent and plausible but not verifiable. For applications where accuracy matters — healthcare information, legal research, financial analysis, enterprise knowledge management — grounding is not optional. RAG is currently the most practical way to achieve it.

Module 5 — Fine-Tuning
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SLIDES
Slide 1 — Fact — What Fine-Tuning Is
Fine-tuning trains an existing model on new data to change its behavior. You start with a pretrained model that already understands language and has broad capabilities. You continue training on your specific dataset. The result is a model that behaves differently from the base model in ways shaped by your training data — different tone, different domain knowledge emphasis, different response patterns. Fine-tuning modifies how the model behaves. RAG modifies what it knows.
Slide 2 — Example — When Fine-Tuning Makes Sense
A customer service AI that needs to consistently match your company's exact tone and terminology. A coding assistant that needs to follow your team's specific conventions. A medical documentation tool that needs to reliably use specific clinical terminology in specific ways. A model that needs to output a very specific format that prompting alone can't enforce consistently. These are fine-tuning use cases — situations where you need behavior change, not knowledge addition.
Slide 3 — Fact — What Fine-Tuning Requires
Fine-tuning requires training data — examples of the inputs and outputs you want. Typically hundreds to thousands of examples for meaningful behavior change. The data needs to be high quality — garbage training data produces garbage fine-tuned behavior. It requires compute — either your own or a cloud provider's. And it requires evaluation — a way to measure whether the fine-tuned model actually behaves better than the base model on your task. Data quality is the most important factor.
Slide 4 — Hot take — Fine-Tuning Is Often Overkill
Most teams reach for fine-tuning too early. Before fine-tuning, try: better prompts, few-shot examples in the system prompt, RAG for knowledge. Fine-tuning is expensive in time, compute, and data collection. It produces a model that requires ongoing maintenance — when the base model improves, your fine-tuned version doesn't automatically improve with it. Fine-tuning makes sense when you've exhausted prompting and RAG and still can't get consistent enough behavior. That's less often than people think.
Slide 5 — Example — RLHF As Fine-Tuning
The fine-tuning you've already learned about — RLHF — is a specific kind. Base language models predict text. RLHF fine-tunes them on human preference data to be helpful, harmless, and honest. ChatGPT and Claude are base models fine-tuned with RLHF. The same technique is available to developers — collect examples of preferred and non-preferred model outputs, train a reward model, fine-tune using that reward signal. This is how you train a model to optimize for human judgment rather than just text prediction.
Slide 6 — Fact — Catastrophic Forgetting
Fine-tuning on a narrow dataset can cause the model to forget general capabilities — a phenomenon called catastrophic forgetting. A model fine-tuned heavily on medical text might become worse at general language tasks. The more you fine-tune and the more different your fine-tuning data is from the original training data, the more general capability you risk losing. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning techniques like LoRA — Low Rank Adaptation — address this by updating only a small subset of the model's parameters, preserving most original capabilities.
Slide 7 — Scenario — Evaluating A Fine-Tuned Model
How do you know if fine-tuning helped? You need an evaluation set — examples held out from training that represent the task you care about. You measure the fine-tuned model's performance on this set and compare to the base model. The metrics depend on the task — accuracy for classification, human preference ratings for open-ended generation, exact match for structured output. Without proper evaluation you can't tell if fine-tuning helped or if it just changed behavior in ways that feel different but aren't actually better.
Slide 8 — Big idea — The Stack
Building real AI applications means choosing the right tool for each layer of the problem. Prompting shapes behavior turn by turn. RAG grounds responses in current, specific knowledge. Fine-tuning changes how the model behaves at scale. These aren't competing approaches; a production application might use all three simultaneously — a fine-tuned model with a carefully engineered system prompt that retrieves relevant context before generating a response. Understanding when to use each layer, and how they interact, is what separates an AI engineer from someone who knows how to write prompts.

Q1 — Easy — Fact
"Fine-tuning starts from scratch — you train a new model on your specific dataset."
False · Wrong. Fine-tuning starts from a pretrained model that already understands language and has broad capabilities. You continue training on your specific dataset. The result is a model that behaves differently in ways shaped by your training data. Starting from scratch would require enormous compute and data. Fine-tuning is efficient because it builds on existing capabilities.
Q2 — Easy — Fact
"Fine-tuning changes how a model behaves, while RAG changes what it knows."
True · Correct. This distinction is the most important thing to understand about when to use each. Fine-tuning is for behavior change — tone, style, response patterns, domain-specific conventions. RAG is for knowledge addition — current information, internal documents, specific facts. Most teams need both for serious applications. Using fine-tuning when you need RAG, or RAG when you need fine-tuning, produces poor results.
Q3 — Easy — Fact
"Fine-tuning requires high-quality training examples of the inputs and outputs you want."
True · Correct. Training data quality is the most important factor in fine-tuning success. Garbage training data produces garbage fine-tuned behavior. You typically need hundreds to thousands of high-quality examples for meaningful behavior change. Data collection and curation is often the most time-consuming part of a fine-tuning project.
Q4 — Medium — Fact
"Fine-tuning is usually the first thing teams should try when prompting alone doesn't produce good results."
False · Wrong. Most teams reach for fine-tuning too early. Before fine-tuning, try better prompts, few-shot examples in the system prompt, and RAG for knowledge. Fine-tuning is expensive in time, compute, and data collection. It produces a model that requires ongoing maintenance. Fine-tuning makes sense when you've exhausted simpler approaches — which is less often than people think.
Q5 — Medium — Fact
"Catastrophic forgetting occurs when fine-tuning on a narrow dataset causes the model to lose general capabilities."
True · Correct. A model fine-tuned heavily on medical text might become worse at general language tasks. The more different your fine-tuning data is from the original training data, the more general capability you risk losing. LoRA — Low Rank Adaptation — addresses this by updating only a small subset of parameters, preserving most original capabilities while still adapting behavior.
Q6 — Medium — Fact
"RLHF is a type of fine-tuning that trains models on human preference data to be helpful, harmless, and honest."
True · Correct. ChatGPT and Claude are base models fine-tuned with RLHF. The same technique is available to developers — collect examples of preferred and non-preferred outputs, train a reward model, fine-tune using that reward signal. This is how you train a model to optimize for human judgment rather than just text prediction. It's the most powerful alignment tool currently available at scale.
Q7 — Hard — Fact
"Without proper evaluation on a held-out test set, you can't tell if fine-tuning actually improved performance."
True · Correct. You need an evaluation set — examples held out from training — and metrics appropriate for your task. Without this, you can only observe that behavior changed, not whether it changed for the better. A fine-tuned model that feels different might be worse on the actual task you care about. Evaluation is not optional for fine-tuning — it's how you know whether the investment paid off.
Q8 — Hard — Fact
"A production AI application typically uses only one of prompting, RAG, or fine-tuning — combining them creates complexity without benefit."
False · Wrong. These are complementary layers, not competing approaches. A production application might use all three: a fine-tuned model with a carefully engineered system prompt that retrieves relevant context from a RAG pipeline. Each layer handles a different aspect — behavior, knowledge, guidance. Understanding when to use each and how they interact is what distinguishes an AI engineer from someone who only knows prompting.

Module 6 — AI Agents
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SLIDES
Slide 1 — Fact — What An Agent Is
An AI agent is a system where a language model doesn't just respond to a single prompt — it takes actions, observes the results, and takes further actions to accomplish a goal. Instead of one input and one output, the model operates in a loop: think, act, observe, think again. Agents can search the web, run code, call APIs, read and write files, and interact with external systems. The model is no longer just generating text — it's doing things in the world.
Slide 2 — Example — Tool Use
The foundation of agents is tool use — giving the model access to functions it can call. You define a set of tools: search the web, run Python code, query a database, send an email. You describe each tool to the model. The model decides which tools to use and when, generates the arguments, and your system executes the actual function call and returns the result. The model sees the result and decides what to do next. This loop continues until the task is complete or the model gives up.
Slide 3 — Example — ReAct
ReAct — Reasoning and Acting — is a prompting pattern for agents. The model alternates between Thought — reasoning about what to do next — and Action — calling a tool. Then Observation — the result of the action. Then Thought again. This explicit structure makes the model's reasoning visible and often improves performance on complex multi-step tasks. It also makes debugging easier — you can see exactly where the reasoning went wrong.
Slide 4 — Hot take — Agents Are Hard
Agents fail in ways that single-turn models don't. They get stuck in loops — repeating the same action because they don't recognize they're going in circles. They make wrong assumptions early that compound through subsequent steps. They run up large costs by taking many actions before failing. They're hard to test — the space of possible execution paths is enormous. Building reliable agents requires careful task decomposition, robust error handling, and often human checkpoints at critical decision points.
Slide 5 — Fact — Context Management In Agents
As an agent takes more actions, the context window fills with the history of thoughts and observations. For long-running tasks this becomes a problem — important early context gets pushed out. Agent architectures address this through summarization — compressing earlier steps into summaries. External memory — storing important information outside the context window and retrieving it when relevant. Memory management is one of the core engineering challenges of building reliable agents.
Slide 6 — Example — Multi-Agent Systems
Complex tasks can be broken into subtasks handled by specialized agents. An orchestrator agent breaks down the overall task and delegates to specialist agents — a research agent that searches the web, a coding agent that writes and runs code, a writing agent that produces documents. Each specialist focuses on what it does best. The orchestrator coordinates. This architecture can handle tasks too complex for a single agent but introduces new challenges — agents misunderstanding each other, coordination failures, error propagation between agents.
Slide 7 — Scenario — When Agents Make Sense
Agents are appropriate when: the task requires multiple steps that depend on each other. The steps require external information or capabilities. The exact sequence of steps can't be predetermined — it depends on what you find along the way. The task takes long enough that human intervention at every step isn't practical. Research synthesis, code debugging, data analysis workflows — these fit the agent pattern. Simple question answering, text generation, classification — these don't need agents and are better handled with simpler approaches.
Slide 8 — Big idea — Autonomy And Oversight
Agents represent a shift toward AI systems that act autonomously in the world rather than just responding to queries. The more autonomy an agent has, the more important it becomes to think carefully about what happens when it goes wrong — because an agent that can send emails, make purchases, or modify files can cause real harm through errors or misunderstanding. Building agents responsibly means defining clear scope boundaries for what the agent can and can't do, requiring human approval for irreversible actions, logging everything the agent does, and preserving the ability to stop and roll back. Autonomy and oversight aren't in tension; they're both design requirements that have to be satisfied together.

Module 7 — Evaluating AI Systems
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SLIDES
Slide 1 — Fact — Evaluation Is Not Optional
You can't improve what you don't measure. AI systems behave differently across different inputs in ways that are hard to predict. A system that works well on the examples you tested might fail on examples you didn't. Building AI applications without evaluation is building on an unknown foundation. Evaluation tells you if your system works, where it fails, whether changes made it better or worse, and whether it's ready to deploy.
Slide 2 — Example — Building An Eval Set
An evaluation set is a collection of inputs with expected outputs — test cases for your AI system. For a customer service AI: sample customer questions with ideal responses. For a document summarizer: documents with human-written summaries to compare against. For a classifier: labeled examples in each category. The eval set should represent the real distribution of inputs your system will encounter — including hard cases, edge cases, and the examples most likely to cause failures.
Slide 3 — Fact — Types Of Evaluation
Different tasks need different evaluation approaches. Exact match — the output must exactly equal the expected output. Used for structured data extraction, classification. BLEU or ROUGE scores — measuring overlap between generated text and reference text. Used for summarization, translation. Human evaluation — people rate outputs on quality dimensions. Used for open-ended generation where there's no single right answer. LLM-as-judge — using a language model to evaluate outputs. Increasingly common as a scalable alternative to human evaluation.
Slide 4 — Hot take — Benchmarks Are Not Your Task
Public benchmarks — MMLU, HumanEval, BIG-bench — measure performance on standardized tasks. They're useful for comparing models. They're not useful for evaluating whether a model works for your specific application. A model that scores highest on MMLU might still fail at your specific document summarization task. Building real applications requires building task-specific evals that measure what you actually care about. Using public benchmarks as a proxy for production performance is a common mistake.
Slide 5 — Example — Regression Testing
Every time you change your prompt, your model, or your RAG pipeline, you need to know whether the change helped or hurt. Regression testing runs your eval set before and after a change and compares results. Did accuracy improve? Did latency change? Did cost change? Did new failure modes appear? Without regression testing, improvements are guesswork. A change that helped on one example might have hurt on fifty others. Regression testing catches these regressions before they reach users.
Slide 6 — Fact — The Goodhart Problem In Evaluation
Goodhart's Law applies to AI evaluation: once you optimize directly for a metric, the metric ceases to be a good measure. A model optimized for a specific benchmark will game the benchmark rather than improve on the underlying capability. An AI system optimized for human approval ratings will learn to sound good rather than be correct. Evaluation metrics need to be diverse — measuring multiple dimensions — and periodically refreshed to prevent gaming. The metric is not the goal. It's a proxy for the goal.
Slide 7 — Scenario — Evaluating For Safety
For AI systems deployed to real users, evaluation must include safety dimensions — not just capability. Does the system refuse to help with harmful requests? Does it handle sensitive topics appropriately? Does it behave differently for different demographic groups? Does it maintain appropriate uncertainty rather than confidently answering when it shouldn't? Safety evaluation requires adversarial test cases — deliberately trying to make the system fail in harmful ways. You need to find these failures before users do.
Slide 8 — Big idea — Evaluation As Product Development
Evaluation isn't a final gate before deployment; it's a continuous practice throughout development. You build evaluation sets before you build the system, so you know what success looks like. You run them throughout development to guide decisions about prompts, models, and architecture. You run them after deployment to catch performance degradation and emerging failure modes. The teams that build the best AI products treat evaluation as a first-class engineering discipline rather than an afterthought, because measurement is the only thing that separates deliberate improvement from guessing.

Module 8 — Responsible Building
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SLIDES
Slide 1 — Example — Failure Mode Analysis
Before shipping an AI system, map its failure modes. What happens when the model is wrong? What happens when it's confidently wrong? What happens when a user tries to misuse it? What happens when it encounters input very different from what it was designed for? What happens when the underlying model changes? Systematic failure mode analysis — imagining all the ways the system could go wrong — is the foundation of responsible deployment. It's easier to prevent failures you've anticipated than to respond to ones you haven't.
Slide 2 — Hot take — The Worst Case Is Not The Unlikely Case
In responsible AI development, the question is not just "what usually happens?" but "what happens to the people most harmed when it goes wrong?" A hiring AI that works well for most applicants but systematically disadvantages a specific group is not "mostly good." The worst-case outcome for the most affected group matters as much as average performance. Building responsibly means deliberately testing for and caring about the experience of users who are most vulnerable to the system's failures.
Slide 3 — Fact — Safety Testing
Before deployment, AI systems should be tested against adversarial inputs — attempts to make the system behave badly. Red teaming: having people try to make the system produce harmful outputs. Prompt injection testing: checking whether the system can be manipulated through user inputs. Bias testing: checking whether the system performs differently across demographic groups. These tests will find failures. Finding them before deployment is much better than finding them after.
Slide 4 — Example — Content Policies
Any AI system deployed to users needs explicit content policies — decisions about what the system will and won't do. These are not just technical decisions. They are ethical decisions about what kinds of assistance are appropriate to provide and to whom. A general-purpose AI assistant, a children's educational tool, and a medical information system need very different content policies. Being explicit about what your system does and doesn't do — and enforcing it technically — is part of responsible deployment.
Slide 5 — Fact — Transparency With Users
Users interacting with AI systems deserve to know they're interacting with AI. They deserve to know what data is being collected and how it's used. They deserve meaningful information about the system's capabilities and limitations. They deserve to know when the system is uncertain. Transparency is not just an ethical obligation — it's practical. Users who understand a system's limitations use it more appropriately, catch errors more readily, and trust it more sustainably than users who are deceived about what it is.
Slide 6 — Scenario — The Deployment Decision
You've built an AI system. It performs well on your evals. But it fails on some cases — all AI systems do. The question is whether the failures are acceptable. Is the failure rate low enough? Are the failures distributed fairly across different user groups? Are failures recoverable — or do they cause permanent harm? Is there human oversight for high-stakes decisions? Is there a feedback mechanism to catch failures you didn't anticipate? These questions don't have universal answers. They require judgment about the specific system and the specific deployment context.
Slide 7 — Big idea — You Are Responsible
When you build and deploy an AI system, you are responsible for what it does. Not the model provider. Not the training data. Not the users who misuse it — though they bear responsibility too. You, the builder, made the decisions about what to build, how to constrain it, who to deploy it to, and what safeguards to put in place. The accountability gap we discussed in W4 — where AI harm diffuses across many actors with no one clearly responsible — is partly closed by builders who take that responsibility seriously. Building AI is powerful. Power and responsibility are not separable.

Module 1 — Multimodal AI
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SLIDES
Slide 1 — Fact — Beyond Text Early AI
systems processed one type of data at a time. Multimodal AI processes multiple types simultaneously and reasons across them rather than just switching between them. Show a multimodal model a photo of a broken machine and ask what's wrong and it reads the image and answers in text. GPT-4V can see images and read text in the same context; Gemini was trained on text, images, audio, and video together. This is qualitatively different from systems that handle one modality at a time, not just more capable but able to do things single-modality systems structurally cannot.
Slide 2 — Example — Vision Language Models Vision
language models combine image understanding with language generation. You can point them at a chart and ask for analysis. Show them a screenshot and ask for code that recreates it. Show them a medical scan and ask what they observe. Show them a handwritten equation and ask for the solution. The combination of seeing and reasoning about what is seen opens applications that pure text models can't touch. Most frontier models released since 2023 are vision language models.
Slide 3 — Example — Audio And Speech AI
speech recognition has become remarkably capable — OpenAI's Whisper transcribes audio across dozens of languages with near-human accuracy. Text-to-speech systems produce voices indistinguishable from humans with a few seconds of sample audio. Multimodal models that process audio can understand tone, emotion, and context beyond just the words. Real-time voice AI — where you speak naturally and the model responds conversationally with low latency — is becoming standard. The phone tree of the future sounds like a person.
Slide 4 — Hot take — Video Is The Hard One Video
is the most data-intensive modality and the hardest to reason about. A one-minute video at standard quality contains thousands of frames — enormous amounts of data. Understanding video requires tracking objects over time, inferring causality from sequences, and reasoning about motion. Models that can watch a video and answer questions about it, generate video from text descriptions, or edit video based on instructions exist but are significantly less capable than text or image models. Video is where the next major capability jumps are likely.
Slide 5 — Fact — Embodied Multimodal AI The
combination of vision, language, and motor control is enabling robots that can follow natural language instructions. "Pick up the blue cup and put it next to the coffee maker." Previous robots needed explicit programming for every task. Multimodal AI enables robots to generalize — using visual understanding and language comprehension to figure out novel tasks they weren't specifically trained for. This is still early and fragile in real-world environments. The trajectory is clear.
Slide 6 — Example — Code And Images Together GitHub Copilot
and similar tools show one direction of multimodal AI for developers — AI that understands both code and natural language simultaneously. More recently, tools that can look at a UI screenshot and generate the code to implement it. Or look at a diagram and generate the system it describes. Or look at a bug report with screenshots and suggest fixes. The combination of visual and code understanding is transforming how software gets built.
Slide 7 — Scenario — The Unified Model The
trajectory of multimodal AI points toward unified models — single systems trained across all modalities simultaneously rather than separate models for each. GPT-4 with vision is closer to this than separate image and text models. The hypothesis is that training across modalities produces richer representations — the model understands concepts more deeply when it has seen them described in text, depicted in images, and heard discussed in audio. Whether this hypothesis is correct at scale is one of the active research questions.
Slide 8 — Big idea — The World As Input The
endpoint of multimodal AI is systems that can perceive the world the way humans do — through multiple simultaneous sensory channels — and reason about what they perceive. This is not just a capability improvement. It's a qualitative shift in what AI can be applied to. Most of the world's information is not text. Medical knowledge is in scans and images. Engineering knowledge is in diagrams and physical systems. Cultural knowledge is in art, music, and film. Multimodal AI makes all of this accessible to AI reasoning for the first time.

Module 2 — AI Agents At Scale
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SLIDES
Slide 1 — Fact — From Chatbots To Agents
The first generation of deployed AI was chatbots: answer a question, generate some text, done. The second generation is agents, AI systems that take sequences of actions over time to accomplish goals. Where a chatbot helps you draft an email, an agent researches the topic, drafts it, checks your calendar, finds the right send time, and sends it. The difference between answering a question and getting something done turns out to be the difference between a tool and a collaborator.
Slide 2 — Example — Software Engineering Agents
AI coding agents can take a GitHub issue, read the codebase, write code to fix the problem, run tests, iterate based on test failures, and submit a pull request — with minimal human involvement. Tools like Devin, Claude Code, and Cursor represent early versions of this. They're not reliable enough to be fully autonomous yet — they need human review and often go wrong in subtle ways. But the trajectory is clear. Software engineering is one of the domains where AI agency is advancing fastest.
Slide 3 — Example — Research Agents AI
research agents can take a question, search the web, read papers, synthesize findings, identify gaps, generate hypotheses, and produce research reports. Early versions exist — Perplexity for web research, experimental systems in scientific domains. The combination of retrieval, reasoning, and synthesis that human researchers do over weeks can be approximated in hours. The quality is not human researcher quality yet. The speed differential is enormous. This is changing how research-intensive work gets done.
Slide 4 — Hot take — The Reliability Problem The
fundamental challenge of agents at scale is reliability. For a single task, an agent that succeeds 80% of the time might be acceptable. For an agent running thousands of tasks per day, 20% failure is catastrophic. Building reliable agents requires not just capability but robustness — consistent performance across diverse inputs, graceful failure when things go wrong, and clear escalation paths when the agent is out of its depth. Reliability engineering for agents is one of the most important and least glamorous frontiers in AI.
Slide 5 — Fact — Long-Horizon Tasks Current AI
excels at short tasks — answer a question, write a paragraph, fix a bug. The frontier is long-horizon tasks — projects that unfold over hours, days, or weeks, requiring planning, memory, adaptation, and coordination. Research projects. Software development projects. Business analysis. These require capabilities current agents don't reliably have: maintaining coherent goals over long time horizons, learning from errors mid-task, and knowing when to ask for help. Long-horizon task completion is the capability that would make AI a genuine collaborator rather than a powerful tool.
Slide 6 — Example — Agent Economies Some
researchers envision economies of AI agents — large numbers of specialized agents working together, contracting with each other for services, producing economic output with minimal human direction. Early versions of multi-agent collaboration exist in research settings. The implications if this scales are significant — economic productivity that grows faster than human population, labor markets that change faster than humans can adapt, and coordination challenges that existing institutions weren't designed for.
Slide 7 — Scenario — Human-Agent Collaboration The
most productive near-term model is probably not fully autonomous agents or pure human work — it's human-agent collaboration. Humans set goals, review outputs at key checkpoints, make judgment calls the agent can't, and provide feedback that improves agent performance. Agents handle research, drafting, data processing, and execution. The human contribution shifts from doing to directing and reviewing. This requires new workflows, new skills, and new ways of thinking about work.
Slide 8 — Big idea — What Agents Change Agents
change the fundamental relationship between AI and work. Tools augment what humans do; agents do the work while humans direct and review. If this transition is as significant as it appears, the implications for employment, productivity, economic distribution, and human purpose are substantial. We're early in it and the outcome isn't determined, but the direction is clear: AI is moving from answering questions to getting things done. What that means for human work is the defining question of the next decade.

Module 3 — Frontier Models
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SLIDES
Slide 1 — Fact — What Frontier Means Frontier
models are the most capable AI systems that exist at any given time. They define the boundary of what AI can currently do. They are trained at enormous scale — hundreds of billions of parameters, trillions of tokens of data, months of compute on thousands of specialized processors. The organizations building them are a small handful. The capabilities they develop cascade into every other application of AI. Understanding what's happening at the frontier is understanding what AI will be able to do in two to five years.
Slide 2 — Example — GPT-4 To GPT-5 Each
generation of frontier models adds capabilities that surprised people who thought the previous generation was near a ceiling. GPT-3 was astonishing. GPT-4 was significantly more capable at reasoning, instruction following, and multimodal understanding. The jump between generations has remained large. Whether the scaling curve continues to produce significant jumps with each generation — or whether diminishing returns set in — is one of the most important open questions in AI.
Slide 3 — Hot take — The Capability Overhang There
is likely a capability overhang — capabilities that exist in current frontier models that haven't been fully discovered or exploited yet. Models are often used for the first application that comes to mind, not the full range of what they can do. As prompting techniques improve, as fine-tuning matures, as researchers probe model capabilities systematically, previously unknown capabilities emerge. This means the impact of current models on society has not yet been fully realized even before the next generation arrives.
Slide 4 — Fact — Model Size Is Not Everything The
relationship between model size and capability is real but not simple. Smaller models trained on more data and for longer can outperform larger models trained less efficiently. The Chinchilla paper showed that most large models at the time were undertrained. Architectural innovations can produce capability jumps without scale increases. Inference-time compute — having the model think longer about hard problems — can improve performance significantly. The frontier is not just about making bigger models.
Slide 5 — Example — Mixture Of Experts Mixture
of Experts — MoE — is an architecture that activates only a subset of the model's parameters for any given input. A 1 trillion parameter MoE model might activate only 100 billion parameters per forward pass — equivalent to the compute of a 100 billion parameter dense model but with the knowledge capacity of a much larger one. GPT-4 is believed to use MoE architecture. It allows scaling model capacity without proportionally scaling compute cost. MoE is one of the key architectural innovations enabling current frontier models.
Slide 6 — Fact — The Inference Revolution Training
frontier models is expensive but happens once. Inference — using the model to generate responses — happens billions of times per day across all deployed applications. The cost and speed of inference determines what AI applications are economically viable. Techniques like quantization — reducing the precision of model weights — and distillation — training smaller models to mimic larger ones — make inference faster and cheaper. The frontier is not just about training better models but about deploying them efficiently.
Slide 7 — Scenario — Reasoning Models A
new category of frontier models — reasoning models — explicitly allocate more computation to thinking through hard problems before responding. OpenAI's o1 and o3, Anthropic's extended thinking models, and similar systems spend compute on internal chain-of-thought reasoning invisible to the user. This trades latency for accuracy on hard problems. Reasoning models significantly outperform standard models on mathematics, science, and complex reasoning tasks. They represent a different paradigm from generating the best immediate response — they generate the best considered response.
Slide 8 — Big idea — The Capability Trajectory The
trajectory of frontier model capabilities over the last five years has been steep and surprisingly consistent, with systems that seemed to require human intelligence now performed routinely by AI. Most people underestimate AI because they extrapolate from past experience rather than from the current rate of change, while simultaneously overestimating specific short-term capabilities. Both errors come from the same source: the rate of change is faster than intuition built on prior decades can accommodate. The honest position is that the trajectory is steep, the endpoint is unclear, and anyone claiming confidence about the timeline in either direction is getting ahead of the evidence.

Module 4 — AI and Science
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SLIDES
Slide 1 — Fact — A New Scientific Instrument AI
is becoming a scientific instrument as important as the microscope or the telescope. Not by replacing scientists — by dramatically expanding what scientists can observe, analyze, and hypothesize. The microscope let us see things too small for human eyes. The telescope let us see things too far away. AI lets us find patterns in datasets too large for human analysis and generate hypotheses across combinations of variables too numerous for human intuition.
Slide 2 — Example — AlphaFold And Biology AlphaFold 2's
solution to protein folding in 2020 was one of the most significant scientific breakthroughs of the decade. Predicting the 3D structure of proteins from their amino acid sequences — after 50 years of failure — transformed structural biology overnight. The AlphaFold database now contains predicted structures for virtually every known protein. This is accelerating drug discovery, enabling the design of novel enzymes, and opening biological research directions that were previously impossible. One AI system changed an entire field.
Slide 3 — Example — Materials Discovery AI
systems are dramatically accelerating materials science — the discovery of new materials with specific properties. Google DeepMind's GNoME discovered 2.2 million new stable crystal structures — more than were discovered in all previous scientific history combined. New materials enable better batteries, more efficient solar panels, stronger and lighter construction materials, and advances in semiconductors. The bottleneck in materials science has historically been the enormous search space of possible compounds. AI navigates that search space far faster than human intuition.
Slide 4 — Hot take — AI Hypotheses The
most interesting frontier in AI and science is not AI analyzing data — it's AI generating hypotheses. Systems that can read the scientific literature and suggest experiments that haven't been tried. That identify patterns across disparate fields that human researchers wouldn't connect. That propose mechanisms for observed phenomena. Early examples exist in drug discovery and genomics. Whether AI can be a genuine creative force in science — not just a fast pattern matcher — is one of the most important open questions.
Slide 5 — Fact — Climate And Earth Science AI
is transforming climate science and earth observation. GraphCast and similar systems outperform traditional numerical weather prediction. AI analysis of satellite imagery tracks deforestation, ice extent, and crop health at global scale in near real time. Climate models are being improved by AI components that better represent physical processes. AI is helping identify locations for renewable energy, optimize grid management, and predict extreme weather events earlier. Climate change is one of the problems most in need of better tools. AI is providing them.
Slide 6 — Example — Drug Discovery At Scale The
traditional drug discovery pipeline takes 10-15 years and costs over a billion dollars per approved drug. AI is compressing this at multiple stages. Identifying promising drug targets. Generating and screening candidate molecules. Predicting toxicity before clinical trials. Designing clinical trials to be more efficient. Repurposing existing drugs for new indications. Multiple AI-designed drug candidates are in clinical trials. The rate of new drug discovery is accelerating. The implications for global health — particularly for diseases that historically receive less research investment — are significant.
Slide 7 — Scenario — The Reproducibility Challenge Scientific
progress depends on reproducibility — results that can be verified independently. AI-assisted science creates new reproducibility challenges. If a discovery depends on a proprietary model and proprietary training data, independent verification is difficult. If an AI identifies a pattern in a dataset, understanding why — what mechanism underlies the pattern — is often unclear. The scientific community is developing new norms around transparency for AI-assisted research. Getting this right matters because unreproducible AI science could poison fields with false discoveries at unprecedented scale.
Slide 8 — Big idea — Compressing Time The
most important effect of AI on science is compression of time. Hypotheses that would take years to test can be simulated and filtered computationally. Patterns that would take decades of data collection to emerge can be identified in existing datasets. Literature that would take a career to read can be synthesized in hours. If AI compresses the effective timeline of scientific progress — allowing discoveries that would have taken 50 years to happen in 10 — the implications for medicine, energy, climate, and human knowledge are profound. We may be approaching a period where the rate of scientific discovery is limited not by human cognitive capacity but by our ability to test and implement what AI helps us discover.

Module 5 — Robotics and Embodied AI
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SLIDES
Slide 1 — Fact — The Missing Body AI
has transformed language and vision. It has been slower to transform physical interaction with the world. Robots exist and have for decades — but they are typically programmed for specific tasks in controlled environments. They don't generalize. A robot programmed to pick up a specific part on a specific factory line can't pick up a different part, let alone navigate an unfamiliar environment. The frontier of embodied AI is building robots that generalize — that can figure out novel physical tasks the way multimodal AI figures out novel language tasks.
Slide 2 — Example — Foundation Models For Robotics The
same approach that produced powerful language models is being applied to robotics. Train a large model on enormous amounts of robot experience data — videos of humans performing tasks, robot demonstrations, simulated experience. The resulting model has broad physical intuitions that can be fine-tuned for specific tasks. Google's RT-2 and similar systems demonstrate robots that can follow novel natural language instructions for tasks they weren't specifically trained on. "Pick up the green block and put it on top of the yellow block" — even if those specific blocks and that specific instruction weren't in the training data.
Slide 3 — Hot take — The Real World Is Hard Robots
in controlled environments are impressive. Robots in the real world — homes, warehouses, hospitals, construction sites — encounter enormous variability. Lighting changes. Objects are in unexpected positions. Surfaces are irregular. Humans are unpredictable. Physical interaction requires force feedback and fine motor control that current robotic hardware handles poorly. The gap between what embodied AI can do in a lab and what it can reliably do in a messy real-world environment is still large. This is why the factory floor and the warehouse are ahead of the home.
Slide 4 — Fact — Simulation To Reality Training
robots in the real world is expensive and slow — real experience takes real time. Simulation allows training robots on millions of scenarios in virtual environments faster than real time. The challenge: simulations don't perfectly replicate the real world. Robots trained in simulation often fail when deployed in reality because of the "sim-to-real gap" — subtle differences between simulated and real physics. Closing this gap through more accurate simulation and domain randomization — training across many varied simulated environments so the robot learns to generalize — is a major research focus.
Slide 5 — Example — Manipulation Robotic
manipulation — picking up, moving, and placing objects — is one of the hardest robotics problems. Humans do it effortlessly because we have exquisite tactile feedback, years of physical experience, and sophisticated motor control. Robots lack this. Recent progress using learning-based approaches has produced systems that can manipulate a wide range of objects in varied conditions — folding laundry, loading dishwashers, assembling components. These systems still fail frequently and require significant training. But the trajectory from "only works on specific objects in specific positions" to "generalizes reasonably across object types" has been fast.
Slide 6 — Scenario — Humanoid Robots Multiple
companies are developing humanoid robots — machines with human-like bodies intended to operate in human environments. Figure, Optimus, Atlas, Digit — the space is active and well-funded. The argument for humanoid form: the world is built for humans, so a human-shaped robot can use human tools and navigate human spaces without modification. The argument against: specialization beats generalization for most tasks, and human form is not optimal for most physical work. Whether humanoid robots become practical general-purpose workers or remain specialized tools is an open question.
Slide 7 — Fact — The Labor Implications The
combination of capable manipulation, reliable navigation, and natural language instruction following — none individually solved but all advancing — points toward robots that could perform a significant fraction of physical labor across warehousing, food preparation, elder care, construction, and agriculture, industries that employ hundreds of millions of people globally. The timeline for this displacement is more uncertain than for knowledge work, but the direction is less so. What's most notable is that the policy challenge of managing this transition has barely begun even as the technical trajectory becomes clearer.
Slide 8 — Big idea — Closing The Loop Most AI
exists in the digital world — it reads and writes text, processes images, generates content. Embodied AI closes the loop between the digital and physical worlds. An embodied AI can perceive the physical world through sensors, reason about it using AI, and act on it through actuators. This closes the loop that keeps most current AI as a tool rather than an agent in the fullest sense. An AI that can perceive, reason, and act in the physical world is not just more capable — it's a different kind of system. The implications for what AI can do, where it can be deployed, and what oversight it requires are significantly different from AI that only processes text.

Module 6 — AI Safety Research
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SLIDES
Slide 1 — Fact — A New Field AI
safety research is a relatively new field — serious as a research discipline for about a decade — that focuses on ensuring AI systems behave as intended, remain under human control, and don't cause catastrophic harm as they become more capable. It spans technical work — alignment, interpretability, robustness — and governance work — how to structure institutions and incentives to make AI development go well. It operates on the assumption that the questions it's working on are important to answer before capabilities outpace our ability to address them.
Slide 2 — Example — Interpretability Research Mechanistic
interpretability tries to understand what's happening inside neural networks at a mechanistic level — identifying specific circuits that perform identifiable computations. Anthropic's research has identified circuits that detect emotions, perform arithmetic, implement algorithms, and represent space. This is still early — these discoveries cover tiny fractions of large models. But the approach is working. The goal is eventually to be able to verify that a model is aligned — not just behaviorally but at the level of what it's actually computing. We're far from that. The progress is real.
Slide 3 — Example — Robustness Research AI
systems fail unexpectedly when inputs are slightly outside their training distribution — on adversarial examples designed to fool them, on rare edge cases, or simply on inputs that look normal to humans but are unusual for the model. Robustness research works on making AI systems that fail gracefully — that know when they're uncertain, that don't produce confident wrong answers on out-of-distribution inputs, that behave consistently across different phrasings of the same question. Robust systems are safer systems. This research is directly applicable to deployed products today.
Slide 4 — Hot take — The Capabilities-Safety Race AI
capabilities research is growing faster than AI safety research, which means the gap between what AI systems can do and our ability to ensure they do it safely is arguably widening rather than narrowing. Most AI investment goes to capabilities; safety research is a small fraction of total spending. Whether safety research can scale fast enough to stay relevant as capabilities increase is one of the most important questions in the field, and the answer depends partly on how many resources and how much talent flow into it.
Slide 5 — Fact — Scalable Oversight One
of the core safety challenges: as AI systems become more capable than humans at specific tasks, how do humans oversee whether those systems are behaving correctly? You can't evaluate a system's mathematical proofs if you can't do the math. Scalable oversight research develops methods that remain valid even as AI capabilities exceed human ability to directly verify outputs. Debate — having AI systems argue for and against positions so humans can evaluate the arguments. Amplification — using AI to help humans evaluate AI. These are early approaches to a problem that will become more important as capabilities increase.
Slide 6 — Scenario — The Evaluation Problem A
safe AI system is one that behaves as intended across the full range of situations it will encounter. Evaluating this comprehensively is extraordinarily difficult. You can test on examples you've thought of. You can't test on examples you haven't thought of. Adversarial red-teaming finds some failures. It doesn't find all of them. As AI systems are deployed in more contexts with more autonomy, the gap between what was tested and what the system encounters grows. The evaluation problem is the core technical challenge of AI safety deployment. It doesn't have a complete solution.
Slide 7 — Fact — Constitutional AI And RLAIF Constitutional AI —
developed at Anthropic — trains AI systems using explicit principles and has the AI evaluate its own outputs against those principles. Rather than relying solely on human feedback — which is expensive and inconsistent — the model generates critiques of its own outputs and revises them. RLAIF — Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback — uses AI to generate the preference data that trains alignment. These approaches help scale alignment techniques beyond what pure human feedback can support. They're promising. They don't solve alignment — the principles still need to be right, and AI self-evaluation can be wrong.
Slide 8 — Big idea — The Window The
most important insight of AI safety research is that there may be a limited window — a period before AI systems become powerful enough that misalignment becomes catastrophic — during which we can develop the tools, techniques, and institutions needed to ensure AI remains beneficial. The window may be long or short. Its length depends on how fast capabilities advance and how hard the safety problems turn out to be. What's clear is that the work done during the window matters more than work done after capable systems are already deployed. Safety research is not insurance against a future risk. It's infrastructure for a future that's arriving faster than most people realize.

Module 7 — The Geopolitics Of AI
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SLIDES
Slide 1 — Fact — AI As Strategic Technology Governments
now treat AI as a strategic national interest — comparable to nuclear technology or semiconductor manufacturing in its geopolitical significance. The United States, China, European Union, and United Kingdom have all produced major national AI strategies. Export controls on advanced AI chips are reshaping global technology supply chains. State investment in AI research and infrastructure is growing. The development of the most powerful AI systems is no longer just a private sector story — it's a story about national power.
Slide 2 — Example — The US-China Competition The US-China AI
competition is the defining geopolitical AI story. China has announced ambitions to be the world leader in AI by 2030. US export controls on advanced semiconductors — the hardware that trains frontier models — are intended to limit Chinese AI capabilities. Chinese AI companies — Baidu, Alibaba, ByteDance, Huawei — are developing frontier models with varying access to the most advanced hardware. Whether these controls are effective, whether they slow Chinese AI development, and whether they damage US technology companies is actively debated.
Slide 3 — Hot take — The Compute Concentration Problem The
ability to train frontier AI models depends on access to enormous amounts of specialized compute — currently NVIDIA's H100 and A100 GPUs. This compute is concentrated in the hands of a small number of organizations. US export controls try to keep the most advanced chips out of Chinese hands. But compute concentration also means that AI capability is concentrated globally in a few US and UK-based labs. The question of whether frontier AI should be developed by a small number of private companies with minimal democratic accountability is a political question as much as a technical one.
Slide 4 — Fact — AI In Defense Every
major military power is integrating AI into defense applications. Autonomous weapons systems — drones that select and engage targets without human control. AI-assisted cyber operations. Intelligence analysis and synthesis. Logistics optimization. The US Department of Defense has invested billions in AI. China's military is explicitly integrating AI into its doctrine. The development of lethal autonomous weapons — systems that can kill without human decision — is proceeding faster than international norms governing their use.
Slide 5 — Example — Autonomous Weapons Lethal
autonomous weapons systems — sometimes called killer robots — are AI systems capable of selecting and engaging targets without real-time human control. They exist. Armed drones with varying levels of autonomy have been used in conflicts in Libya, Ukraine, and elsewhere. International negotiations on governing autonomous weapons have been ongoing at the UN since 2014 without producing binding agreements. The countries most advanced in autonomous weapons development — primarily the US, China, and Israel — have resisted binding restrictions. The international norm against autonomous killing has not been established.
Slide 6 — Scenario — The AI Governance Gap National AI
strategies and export controls are national solutions to a global problem. AI systems trained in one country affect people in every country. Disinformation generated by AI doesn't respect borders. Autonomous weapons developed by one state create pressure on others to develop them. The governance frameworks most needed for AI — international agreements on safety standards, arms control for autonomous weapons, norms for AI surveillance — require international cooperation among parties with competing interests. The gap between the governance needed and the governance achieved is large.
Slide 7 — Fact — AI And Democratic Backsliding AI
surveillance, AI-generated disinformation, and AI-powered persuasion are being used to undermine democratic governance in multiple countries. AI facial recognition enables authoritarian surveillance of populations. AI-generated content floods information environments faster than fact-checkers can respond. AI-powered microtargeting allows political manipulation at scale. These tools are available to authoritarian and democratic governments alike. Whether AI strengthens or weakens democratic institutions depends on how it's governed — and that governance is contested.
Slide 8 — Big idea — The Stakes Are Geopolitical AI
development is no longer just a technology story or an economic story. It's a story about power — who has it, who doesn't, and how the most transformative technology since the industrial revolution gets distributed globally. The decisions being made now — about export controls, about military AI, about international governance, about democratic oversight of AI companies — will shape the geopolitical order for decades. These decisions are being made by a small number of governments and companies with enormous stakes in the outcomes. The public — which will live with the consequences — has limited visibility and limited voice. That's a governance problem. It's also a democratic one.

Module 8 — What Comes Next
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SLIDES
Slide 1 — Fact — The Honest Uncertainty Nobody
knows what AI will look like in ten years — not the people building it, not the people governing it, not the people studying it. Anyone claiming certainty about the trajectory, whether confidently optimistic or confidently catastrophic, is wrong not because their conclusion is necessarily wrong but because the certainty is unjustified. What's actually possible is reasoning carefully about trajectories, identifying the decisions that matter most, and acting wisely under genuine uncertainty. That's less satisfying than a confident prediction, but it's the honest position.
Slide 2 — Example — What The Last Five
Years Should Teach In 2019 GPT-2 was considered potentially dangerous for producing realistic text. In 2024 GPT-2 is routinely outperformed by models available for free on phones. In 2019 protein folding was an unsolved 50-year-old problem. In 2024 AlphaFold has solved it for virtually every known protein. In 2019 generating photorealistic images from text was science fiction. In 2024 it's a consumer product. The lesson is not that AI will solve every problem — it's that the pace of capability improvement should update your expectations significantly.
Slide 3 — Hot take — The Predictions That Were Wrong Most AI
predictions have been wrong in a specific direction: underestimating how fast certain capabilities would develop, overestimating how broadly those capabilities would generalize. Chess fell faster than expected. Go fell faster than expected. Image recognition fell faster than expected. Language generation fell faster than expected. But AI still can't reliably navigate an unfamiliar physical environment, build genuine long-term relationships, or develop genuinely novel scientific theories from first principles. The capability curve is steep and uneven. Both the progress and the remaining gaps are real.
Slide 4 — Fact — The Three Possible Futures Researchers
who think carefully about AI outcomes tend to cluster around three broad scenarios. Flourishing — AI dramatically accelerates human progress, distributes benefits broadly, and humanity navigates the transition successfully. Stagnation — AI progress slows or plateaus, current harms persist without catastrophic new ones, AI becomes infrastructure rather than transformation. Catastrophe — AI misalignment, power concentration, or other failure modes produce outcomes that permanently curtail human potential. All three are possible. None is inevitable. The outcome depends heavily on choices being made now.
Slide 5 — Example — The Economic Transformation The
economic case for AI investment rests on productivity gains — AI making workers more productive or replacing workers entirely in some tasks. The magnitude of these gains is uncertain. McKinsey estimates AI could automate 60-70% of work activities across industries. Goldman Sachs estimates 300 million jobs globally could be partially or fully automated. These are estimates with enormous uncertainty ranges. What's more certain is that the economic transition, whatever its magnitude, will be uneven — some sectors, regions, and skill sets will be affected much faster than others.
Slide 6 — Scenario — If AGI Arrives If
a genuinely general AI system arrives — one that can do any intellectual task a human can do — the implications are difficult to overstate. Scientific progress that currently takes decades could happen in years. Economic productivity could increase by orders of magnitude. The balance of military and economic power could shift dramatically and rapidly. Whether the transition would be manageable depends almost entirely on two things: whether the system is aligned with human values, and whether its benefits are distributed rather than concentrated. Neither is guaranteed. Both are the most important policy problems in the world.
Slide 7 — Fact — What You Can Do The
future of AI is not determined. It's being shaped by choices — made by researchers, engineers, policymakers, investors, educators, and citizens. More people who understand AI means better decisions about AI. Better decisions about AI means better outcomes. This is not just abstract. Understanding AI well enough to ask the right questions about it — what is it optimizing for, who controls it, who benefits, who bears the risks — is the foundation for holding AI developers, deployers, and governments accountable. That accountability is how good outcomes get produced.
Slide 8 — Big idea — The Conversation Continues AI
isn't a problem that gets solved; it's a phenomenon that continues developing, and the questions it raises about intelligence, consciousness, power, fairness, and human identity will be active questions for as long as AI exists. This course gave you a foundation for engaging with those questions seriously. The specific facts will get outdated; the capacity to ask the right questions, evaluate claims skeptically, and participate in the decisions that shape where this goes will not. That's what this was actually for.

WORLD 1 — WHAT IS AI?

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Module 1 — History of AI
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Myth bust — Before Computers
  Aristotle spent his life trying to formalize logic: a set of rules where if you follow
  them correctly you always reach the right answer. He wasn't building a machine. He was
  asking whether reasoning itself could be mechanical. That question is 2,500 years old.
  AI didn't start with computers. It started with that question.

  Slide 2 — Myth bust — Ada Lovelace
  Ada Lovelace was writing notes on a machine that hadn't been built yet. Charles
  Babbage designed the Analytical Engine: a mechanical calculator. Everyone else saw a
  calculator. Ada saw something else entirely. She wrote that the machine could
  potentially compose music, produce graphics, and solve problems of any complexity: if
  you gave it the right instructions. She wrote those instructions. The first algorithm
  ever published was hers. In 1843.

  Slide 3 — Hot take — The Turing Test
  Alan Turing publishes a paper asking: can machines think? He doesn't answer it, he
  sidesteps it entirely. He invents a test instead. If a machine can hold a conversation
  well enough that a human can't tell it's a machine, does it even matter whether it's
  really thinking? That question was radical in 1950. ChatGPT passes versions of the
  Turing Test today. Which means the question still isn't answered.

  Slide 4 — Scenario — The Birth of AI
  It's 1956. A group of researchers gather at Dartmouth College for a summer workshop.
  John McCarthy proposes a name for the field they're building: Artificial Intelligence.
  They actually believe they'll make serious progress in one summer. Some think they'll
  basically solve it. They don't. But the name sticks, the field is born, and the most
  ambitious research project in human history officially begins.

  Slide 5 — Myth bust — The AI Winters
  Twice, the money runs out. Governments pour funding into AI, get excited, watch it
  underdeliver, and pull back completely. Labs shut down. Researchers leave the field.
  The first winter hits in the 1970s. The second in the late 1980s. The field nearly
  dies both times. This matters because it's still happening: every few years AI gets
  declared the next revolution. Knowing it has nearly collapsed twice makes you a much
  sharper reader of that hype.

  Slide 6 — Hot take — Deep Blue
  1997. Deep Blue defeats Garry Kasparov: the reigning world chess champion. The
  headlines declare that machines are now smarter than humans. But Deep Blue couldn't
  play checkers. It couldn't recognize a cat, hold a conversation, or understand what
  chess even was. It calculated millions of possible moves per second and picked the
  best one. That's not intelligence. That's a very fast calculator with a very specific
  purpose. The world missed that distinction completely.

  Slide 7 — Turning point — Everything Changes
  2012. A neural network called AlexNet enters an image recognition competition and wins
  by a margin that shocks the entire field. The technique it uses, deep learning, takes
  over everything. Before 2012 AI is mostly clever rule-based tricks. After 2012 AI
  starts actually learning from data at scale. Every tool you use today: image
  generators, voice assistants, ChatGPT, recommendation systems: traces directly back to
  what happened that year. Most people missed it entirely.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — You're In It
  2022. ChatGPT launches and reaches 100 million users in two months: faster than any
  product in history. AI-generated images, music, code, and video go from experimental
  to everywhere almost overnight. For the first time AI is something anyone can use, not
  just researchers. Nobody fully agrees on what it means yet. That's not a cop-out. It's
  actually where we are. You're not learning about something that happened. You're
  learning about something that's still happening.

QUIZ

  Q1 — Easy — Myth bust
  "The idea behind AI predates computers by thousands of years."
  True · Correct.
    Aristotle was formalizing mechanical reasoning 2,500 years ago. Ada Lovelace wrote
    the first algorithm in 1843. The computers are new. The obsession with making
    machines think is not.

  Q2 — Easy — Myth bust
  "Ada Lovelace was the first person to write an algorithm."
  True · Correct.
    She wrote it in 1843 for a machine that hadn't been built yet. She also predicted
    machines could compose music, produce graphics, and solve complex problems. She was
    right about all of it. A century early.

  Q3 — Easy — Hot take
  "The Turing Test proves a machine is intelligent if it passes."
  False · Wrong.
    Turing never said that. He asked whether the question even matters. Passing means a
    machine can imitate human conversation, not that it understands anything. ChatGPT
    passes versions of it today and has no idea what a word means.

  Q4 — Medium — Scenario
  "The researchers at Dartmouth in 1956 were naive to think they could solve AI quickly."
  False · Not really.
    They weren't naive. They had no way of knowing how hard the problem was. Nobody did.
    The most brilliant researchers in the world were completely wrong about something
    they invented. That's not naivety. That's the nature of working at the edge of what
    anyone has ever tried.

  Q5 — Medium — Myth bust
  "AI research has been steadily progressing since the 1950s."
  False · Wrong.
    It nearly died twice. The AI Winters of the 1970s and 1980s saw funding collapse,
    labs shut down, and researchers abandon the field entirely. Understanding this
    separates people who think clearly about AI from people who believe every headline.

  Q6 — Medium — Hot take
  "Deep Blue was a narrow AI. It could only play chess and nothing else."
  True · Correct.
    Deep Blue was extraordinarily powerful at one specific task and completely useless
    at everything else. It couldn't play checkers, recognize a face, or understand a
    sentence. Beating Kasparov was not evidence of general intelligence. It was evidence
    of very fast, very specific calculation.

  Q7 — Hard — Turning point
  "The most important moment in modern AI history was the launch of ChatGPT in 2022."
  False · Wrong.
    It was 2012. When AlexNet won the image recognition competition by a shocking
    margin, deep learning took over the entire field overnight. ChatGPT is a product of
    that moment. Without 2012 there is no 2022. Most people get this backwards because
    ChatGPT is what they noticed.

  Q8 — Hard — Big idea
  "The question of what AI fundamentally is remains unresolved."
  True · Correct.
    Nobody fully agrees on what AI is, whether it can think, what rights it might
    deserve, or what it will do to the economy and human identity. You just finished a
    lesson on 2,500 years of people trying to answer these questions. They're still
    open. That's not a failure: that's what makes this the most interesting field in the
    world right now.


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Module 2 — What AI Does
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Myth bust — The Magic Trick
  Everyone thinks AI is magic. Scientists, journalists, even people who build it use
  words like 'the AI decided' or 'the AI understands.' None of that is accurate. AI
  finds patterns in data. That's the whole secret. The magic is just math running very
  fast on very large amounts of information.

  Slide 2 — Scenario — How You Learned
  When you were a baby you learned what a dog is by seeing dogs. Nobody gave you a
  definition. You just saw enough of them, big ones, small ones, fluffy ones, and your
  brain built a pattern. AI does the exact same thing. The difference is it can process
  millions of examples in hours instead of years, and it never gets tired or distracted.

  Slide 3 — Example — Show It Enough Cats
  Show an AI 10 million photos labeled 'cat.' It finds what they all have in common:
  pointy ears, whiskers, a certain eye shape. Now show it a photo it has never seen. It
  checks the pixels against the pattern and decides: cat or not cat. That is image
  recognition. It is also exactly how Face ID works when your phone unlocks with your
  face.

  Slide 4 — Hot take — Garbage In
  The pattern is only as good as the examples. Bad examples produce a bad pattern.
  Biased examples produce a biased pattern. In the 1990s a hiring algorithm trained on
  historical resumes started rejecting women, not because someone programmed it to, but
  because most past hires were men. It learned exactly what it was shown. The data was
  the problem. The AI worked perfectly.

  Slide 5 — Myth bust — No One's Home
  The AI matching your face has no idea what a face is. It has no idea what anything is.
  It just knows this specific arrangement of pixels matches the pattern it learned.
  There is no understanding happening. No recognition. No awareness. Just a number that
  says: match. This is the most important thing to understand about AI and almost nobody
  gets it right.

  Slide 6 — Hot take — Why It Feels Like Magic
  Pattern matching at scale looks exactly like intelligence. It recommends the show you
  wanted to watch. It finishes your sentence better than you could. It translates a
  language you don't speak in real time. None of that is thinking. All of it is patterns
  found across billions of examples. The scale is so large that the result is
  indistinguishable from something that actually understands you.

  Slide 7 — Myth bust — Where It Breaks
  Ask it something slightly outside its training and it falls apart: confidently. It has
  no way to know it doesn't know something. It can't reason its way to a new answer. It
  can only match against what it has seen before. When there is no good match it either
  fails silently or invents an answer that sounds completely correct and is completely
  wrong.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — The Mirror
  AI is a mirror. It reflects back the patterns in whatever data it was trained on. It
  is only as smart, fair, creative, or accurate as the examples it learned from. That
  means every AI system carries the values, biases, and blind spots of whoever collected
  its training data. That is not a bug. That is the whole design. And it means the most
  important question about any AI is not how smart it is. It is who built it and what
  they fed it.

QUIZ

  Q1 — Easy — Myth bust
  "AI finds patterns in data rather than thinking through problems logically."
  True · Correct.
    AI doesn't think, reason, or understand anything. It finds patterns. Show it enough
    dogs and it learns dog. Show it enough spam and it learns spam. That's the whole
    mechanism. Everything else is details.

  Q2 — Easy — Example
  "Face ID on your phone is an example of AI pattern matching."
  True · Correct.
    Your phone trained on your face, built a model of the pattern, and checks every
    unlock attempt against it. It has no idea who you are. It just knows the pixels
    match. That's also how every image recognition system in the world works.

  Q3 — Easy — Scenario
  "An AI trained on millions of cat photos will recognize every cat it's ever shown perfectly."
  False · Wrong.
    It will still fail on unusual cases: a hairless cat, a cartoon, a cat in costume.
    The pattern is powerful but it has edges. Every AI has a boundary where its training
    ends and its confidence continues. The dangerous moment is when it crosses that
    boundary without anyone noticing.

  Q4 — Medium — Hot take
  "If an AI produces biased results, someone must have programmed the bias in deliberately."
  False · Wrong.
    Nobody has to program bias in. It comes from the data. In the 1990s a hiring
    algorithm started rejecting women's resumes, not because anyone told it to, but
    because most historical hires were men. It learned exactly what it was shown. The
    bias was in the data. The AI worked perfectly.

  Q5 — Medium — Myth bust
  "When AI translates a language it understands what the words mean."
  False · Wrong.
    It has no idea what any word means. It learned that certain patterns in one language
    correspond to certain patterns in another. Meaning never enters the picture. That's
    why AI translation falls apart on idioms, jokes, and cultural references: there's no
    understanding to fall back on.

  Q6 — Medium — Hot take
  "The scale of AI pattern matching is what makes it seem like intelligence."
  True · Correct.
    Pattern matching at scale looks exactly like intelligence. It recommends the show
    you wanted. It finishes your sentence perfectly. It translates in real time. None of
    that is thinking. All of it is patterns found across billions of examples. The scale
    is so large that the result is indistinguishable from something that actually
    understands you.

  Q7 — Hard — Myth bust
  "Because AI is so good at pattern matching at scale, it's basically the same as thinking."
  False · Wrong.
    It looks like thinking. It produces results that feel like thinking. But there is no
    reasoning happening. No understanding. No awareness. A calculator doing a million
    additions per second isn't thinking either. It's just fast. Scale changes the
    output. It doesn't change what's actually happening underneath.

  Q8 — Hard — Big idea
  "AI is a neutral tool. It doesn't carry anyone's values or perspective."
  False · Wrong.
    AI is a mirror. It reflects back the patterns in whatever data it was trained on.
    Whoever decides what goes into that data shapes everything the AI learns, gets
    right, gets wrong, and leaves out entirely. There is no neutral. There is only whose
    data got included and whose didn't.


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Module 3 — AI In Your Life
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Myth bust — You Already Use It
  You don't need to go looking for AI. It found you years ago. Every app you opened
  today, every song that played next, every search result you clicked, AI was involved.
  You're not a future user of this technology. You're a current one. You have been for
  years.

  Slide 2 — Example — The Feed
  TikTok's algorithm watches everything. How long you pause. Whether you replay. What
  you skip. It builds a model of you, not your name, not your face, just your patterns.
  Then it finds content that matches those patterns. Within 30 minutes of a new account
  it knows more about what you'll watch than your closest friends do.

  Slide 3 — Example — The Recommendation
  Netflix doesn't show everyone the same homepage. It shows you a homepage built for
  your pattern. The thumbnails it picks, the order shows appear, even which shows get
  promoted: all of it is personalized to what its model predicts you'll click. You've
  never seen Netflix the way anyone else sees it.

  Slide 4 — Hot take — Your Phone Knows You
  Autocomplete on your keyboard is AI. Spam filter in your email is AI. The camera
  deciding what's in focus is AI. Face ID is AI. Google Maps rerouting around traffic is
  AI. Most people interact with AI dozens of times before noon without ever thinking of
  it as AI.

  Slide 5 — Myth bust — It's Not Trying To Help You
  Recommendation algorithms are not designed to make you happy. They're designed to
  maximize engagement: time spent, clicks, shares. Sometimes that means showing you
  things that make you angry or anxious because those emotions keep you scrolling. The
  algorithm doesn't know the difference. It just knows what gets clicks.

  Slide 6 — Hot take — The Echo Chamber
  When AI only shows you things that match your existing patterns it gets harder to
  encounter ideas you disagree with. Your feed becomes a mirror of what you already
  believe. This isn't a conspiracy. It's just pattern matching doing its job. The
  algorithm gives you more of what you engage with. If you only engage with one
  viewpoint, that's all it shows you.

  Slide 7 — Example — Spotify Knows First
  Spotify has predicted breakups before the people involved announced them. Listening
  patterns shift: more sad songs, less shared playlists, different genres at night. The
  algorithm notices the pattern change before most friends would. It's not magic. It's
  just that your music choices are data, and data tells a story.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — You Are The Product
  Every free app that uses AI to personalize your experience is doing so because your
  attention is valuable. The more accurately the algorithm models you, the better it can
  sell that model to advertisers. You are not the customer. You are the data that makes
  the product work. Understanding that doesn't mean you have to stop using these apps.
  It means you should use them knowing what they actually are.

QUIZ

  Q1 — Easy — Example
  "TikTok's algorithm watches your behavior, not just what you like but how long you pause and what you replay."
  True · Correct.
    It doesn't need your name. It watches behavior: pause time, replays, skips. It
    builds a behavioral pattern that is often more accurate than anything you'd tell it
    voluntarily. Within 30 minutes of a new account it knows more about what you'll
    watch than your closest friends do.

  Q2 — Easy — Myth bust
  "Every Netflix user sees the same homepage."
  False · Wrong.
    Netflix builds a different homepage for every user based on watch history and
    patterns. The thumbnails, the order, which shows get promoted: all personalized. You
    have never seen Netflix the way anyone else sees it.

  Q3 — Easy — Example
  "Spam filters in your email are a form of AI."
  True · Correct.
    Spam filters learned what spam looks like by training on millions of examples. They
    pattern-match every incoming email against what they learned. That's AI. It's been
    in your inbox for decades without most people ever thinking of it that way.

  Q4 — Medium — Hot take
  "Recommendation algorithms are designed to make you happy."
  False · Wrong.
    They're designed to maximize engagement: time spent, clicks, shares. Happiness and
    engagement often overlap but not always. Content that makes you angry or anxious
    keeps you scrolling just as well as content that makes you happy. The algorithm
    doesn't know the difference and doesn't care.

  Q5 — Medium — Myth bust
  "Echo chambers on social media happen because algorithms show you more of what you engage with."
  True · Correct.
    It's not just that people seek out opinions they agree with. Recommendation
    algorithms amplify it automatically. If you engage with one viewpoint that's what
    fills your feed, not because you chose it but because the algorithm optimized for
    your clicks. The design of the system does most of the work.

  Q6 — Medium — Hot take
  "Using personalized apps means AI has an accurate model of who you are as a person."
  False · Wrong.
    AI has an accurate model of your behavioral patterns. That's different from knowing
    who you are. It knows what you click not why. It knows what you watch not what you
    value. The model is powerful and shallow at the same time.

  Q7 — Hard — Hot take
  "The fact that apps like TikTok and Spotify are free means AI personalization costs users nothing."
  False · Wrong.
    The price is your attention and your data. The more accurately the algorithm models
    your behavior the more valuable that model is to advertisers. You are not the
    customer of these platforms. You are the product being sold. Free means someone else
    is paying, and that someone is paying for access to you.

  Q8 — Hard — Big idea
  "Understanding how recommendation algorithms work can change your relationship to them."
  True · Correct.
    Knowing the algorithm optimizes for engagement rather than your wellbeing doesn't
    make it powerless but it changes how you interact with it. You can ask why you're
    being shown something instead of just reacting to it. That gap, between stimulus and
    response, is where critical thinking lives. It's also exactly what the algorithm is
    designed to eliminate.

Module 4 — Narrow vs General AI
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Myth bust — AI Is Not One Thing
  When people say AI they might mean the algorithm that recommends your next video, the
  system that detects cancer in an X-ray, the chatbot that writes your essay, or the
  robot that builds cars. These are completely different technologies. Calling them all
  AI is like calling a bicycle, a helicopter, and a submarine all vehicles. Technically
  true. Practically useless.

  Slide 2 — Example — Narrow AI
  Every AI you have ever used is narrow. It does one thing. Face ID recognizes faces, it
  cannot drive a car. Spotify recommends music, it cannot diagnose a disease. AlphaGo
  plays Go better than any human. It cannot play chess. Each one is extraordinarily
  powerful within its specific task and completely useless outside of it.

  Slide 3 — Hot take — ChatGPT Is Still Narrow
  ChatGPT feels different. It can write, code, translate, explain, and debate. But it is
  still narrow: its task is generating text that matches patterns in its training data.
  It cannot walk into a room, pick up a glass of water, or understand what thirst feels
  like. The breadth of what it does with text is impressive. It is still one thing.

  Slide 4 — Myth bust — General AI Doesn't Exist
  General AI, a system that can do anything a human can do, learn anything, reason about
  anything: does not exist. It has never existed outside of science fiction. Every AI
  system ever built has been narrow. Some researchers think general AI is decades away.
  Some think it's impossible. Nobody thinks it exists right now.

  Slide 5 — Scenario — Why This Matters
  In 1997 Deep Blue beat Kasparov. Headlines declared machines smarter than humans. But
  Deep Blue couldn't buy groceries, understand a joke, or learn a new game without being
  completely rebuilt. Narrow AI beating humans at specific tasks is not the same as AI
  being smarter than humans. Confusing the two is one of the most common and
  consequential mistakes people make about this technology.

  Slide 6 — Hot take — The Danger Of Narrow AI
  Narrow AI can still cause enormous damage. A hiring algorithm that discriminates. A
  loan algorithm that denies credit by zip code. A content algorithm that radicalizes
  teenagers. None of these require general intelligence. They just require a narrow
  system optimizing the wrong thing at scale. You don't need AI to be smart to be
  dangerous. You need it to be fast and widely deployed.

  Slide 7 — Example — Getting Closer
  Recent AI systems are getting broader. GPT-4 can see images and write code and reason
  about math. DeepMind's Gemini was trained across text, images, audio, and video
  simultaneously. These are still narrow in the technical sense. They don't have goals,
  desires, or self-awareness. But the gap between narrow and general is getting harder
  to define clearly.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — The Real Question
  The question isn't whether AI is smart. It's what it's optimizing for and who decided
  that. A narrow AI optimizing for engagement can reshape democracy. A narrow AI
  optimizing for efficiency can eliminate millions of jobs. The intelligence level
  doesn't matter as much as the objective. And in almost every case, a human chose that
  objective. That means a human can choose a different one.

QUIZ

  Q1 — Easy — Myth bust
  "All AI systems work the same way."
  False · Wrong.
    Face ID, ChatGPT, AlphaGo, and Spotify's recommendation engine are all called AI and
    work completely differently. They were built differently, trained differently,
    deployed for completely different purposes. Treating them as one thing leads to
    confused thinking about all of them.

  Q2 — Easy — Fact
  "Every AI currently in existence is narrow: designed for specific tasks."
  True · Correct.
    Every AI currently in existence is narrow. Face ID recognizes faces. Spotify
    recommends music. ChatGPT generates text. None of them can do each other's jobs.
    General AI, a system that can do anything a human can do, does not exist.

  Q3 — Easy — Myth bust
  "General AI exists but most people don't have access to it."
  False · Wrong.
    General AI does not exist at all. Not in a lab, not classified, not in development.
    It has never existed outside of science fiction. Every AI system ever built has been
    narrow. This is not a secret. It's just not how AI gets reported in the news.

  Q4 — Medium — Hot take
  "ChatGPT is general AI because it can do so many different things."
  False · Wrong.
    ChatGPT is narrow. Its task is generating text that fits patterns in its training
    data. It does that task across many domains: writing, coding, explaining, debating.
    But it cannot walk, feel, learn a new skill from scratch, or understand what any of
    the words it produces actually mean.

  Q5 — Medium — Hot take
  "Narrow AI can still cause enormous harm even without general intelligence."
  True · Correct.
    Discriminatory hiring algorithms, biased lending systems, radicalizing content
    algorithms: all narrow, all damaging at scale. You don't need general intelligence
    to cause large-scale harm. You need a narrow system optimizing the wrong objective
    deployed to millions of people.

  Q6 — Medium — Scenario
  "Deep Blue beating Kasparov at chess in 1997 meant AI was becoming smarter than humans overall."
  False · Wrong.
    Deep Blue couldn't play checkers. It couldn't recognize a face or understand a
    sentence. Superhuman performance at a specific task says nothing about general
    intelligence. A calculator beats every human at arithmetic. That doesn't make it
    smart.

  Q7 — Hard — Fact
  "The boundary between narrow and general AI is becoming harder to define as systems become more capable."
  True · Correct.
    Systems like GPT-4 handle text, images, code, and reasoning across domains. They're
    still technically narrow but the boundary is harder to draw. This is one reason AI
    researchers disagree so much about timelines. They can't fully agree on what general
    AI would even mean.

  Q8 — Hard — Big idea
  "The most important question about any AI system is how intelligent it is."
  False · Wrong.
    The most important question is what it's optimizing for and who decided that. A
    narrow AI optimizing for engagement can reshape political beliefs. A narrow AI
    optimizing for efficiency can displace millions of workers. The objective matters
    more than the intelligence level. And in almost every case a human chose that
    objective.

Module 5 — How AI Learns
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Myth bust — It Wasn't Programmed
  Traditional software is programmed. A human writes rules: if this then that. AI is
  different. Nobody programmed it to recognize your face or recommend your next song. It
  learned those things by being shown millions of examples. The difference sounds
  subtle. It isn't. It means nobody fully knows why an AI makes the decisions it makes,
  including the people who built it.

  Slide 2 — Example — Supervised Learning
  Show an AI 10 million photos. Half are labeled 'cat.' Half are labeled 'not cat.' It
  adjusts itself millions of times until it gets the labels right. Then show it a photo
  it's never seen. It applies what it learned. This is supervised learning: learning
  from labeled examples. It's how most AI you've encountered works. Someone had to label
  all those photos first.

  Slide 3 — Example — Unsupervised Learning
  Sometimes AI learns without labels. Show it a million photos with no labels at all. It
  finds its own patterns: clusters things that look similar, separates things that look
  different. It doesn't know what a cat is. It just notices that some images share
  features. This is how Spotify groups musical styles together without anyone telling it
  what genres exist.

  Slide 4 — Hot take — The Training Data Problem
  The quality of what an AI learns is entirely determined by what it was trained on. An
  AI trained on 1950s medical textbooks will give 1950s medical advice. An AI trained
  mostly on text written by men will reflect male perspectives as default. An AI trained
  on internet data will absorb every bias, conspiracy, and falsehood on the internet.
  Training data is the single most important and least discussed part of AI development.

  Slide 5 — Example — Reinforcement Learning
  A third way AI learns: trial and error with feedback. Give it a goal and let it try
  things. Reward it when it gets closer. Punish it when it doesn't. Over millions of
  attempts it figures out strategies no human programmed. This is how AlphaGo learned to
  play Go. It played itself millions of times and discovered moves that human players
  had never considered in centuries of playing the game.

  Slide 6 — Myth bust — It Never Stops Being Wrong
  AI doesn't learn until it's perfect then stop. It learns until it's good enough then
  gets deployed. Every AI system in use right now is making mistakes constantly. Face ID
  misidentifies. Autocomplete suggests the wrong word. Medical AI misses diagnoses. The
  goal is never perfection. It's being right often enough to be useful. That gap between
  often enough and always is where real harm happens.

  Slide 7 — Hot take — Who Does The Real Work
  Somewhere in the world, right now, thousands of people are labeling data so AI can
  learn. Clicking which photos contain traffic lights. Transcribing audio. Rating
  whether AI responses were helpful. Much of this work is done by low-paid contractors
  in developing countries. The glamorous AI products you use exist because of enormous
  amounts of invisible human labor that almost nobody talks about.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Learning Without Understanding
  Here is the most unsettling thing about how AI learns. It can become extraordinarily
  good at a task without developing any understanding of what that task means. An AI can
  learn to write convincing essays about grief without having any concept of loss. It
  can learn to diagnose cancer without understanding what it feels like to be sick. It
  gets better and better at the surface of things while the depth remains completely
  empty. Whether that matters, whether understanding is even necessary, is one of the
  open questions of our time.

QUIZ

  Q1 — Easy — Myth bust
  "AI is programmed with specific rules for every decision it makes."
  False · Wrong.
    Traditional software is programmed with rules. AI learns from examples. Nobody
    programmed Face ID with rules about what faces look like. It was shown millions of
    faces and learned on its own. That means nobody fully knows why an AI makes any
    specific decision, including the people who built it.

  Q2 — Easy — Fact
  "Supervised learning requires someone to label the training data first."
  True · Correct.
    Every example needs a label. This is a cat, this is spam, this loan defaulted.
    Someone has to do that labeling. Millions of examples means millions of labels. Much
    of that work is done by low-paid contractors around the world whose labor makes the
    AI products you use possible.

  Q3 — Easy — Myth bust
  "AlphaGo was programmed with Go strategies by expert players."
  False · Wrong.
    AlphaGo used reinforcement learning. It played itself millions of times and figured
    out its own strategies through trial and error. It discovered moves that human
    players had never considered in centuries of playing the game. Nobody programmed
    those moves. AlphaGo invented them.

  Q4 — Medium — Hot take
  "Training AI on more data always makes it more accurate."
  False · Wrong.
    More data makes AI more powerful. It doesn't make it more right. Training on more
    biased data produces more confidently biased AI. More of the wrong thing is still
    the wrong thing. The quality and composition of data matters far more than the
    quantity.

  Q5 — Medium — Fact
  "The training data is the single most important factor in determining what an AI learns."
  True · Correct.
    An AI trained on 1950s medical textbooks gives 1950s medical advice. An AI trained
    mostly on text written by men reflects male perspectives as default. An AI trained
    on internet data absorbs every bias and falsehood on the internet. Training data is
    the most important and least discussed part of AI development.

  Q6 — Medium — Myth bust
  "The goal of AI training is to make the system perfectly accurate."
  False · Wrong.
    The goal is accurate enough to be useful. No AI system is deployed at perfect
    accuracy. The acceptable error rate is a human decision and it varies enormously by
    context. An error rate acceptable for music recommendation is catastrophic for
    cancer diagnosis. Who decides what counts as good enough matters as much as the
    training itself.

  Q7 — Hard — Fact
  "AI can become extraordinarily good at a task without developing any understanding of what that task means."
  True · Correct.
    This is the most unsettling thing about how AI learns. It can write convincingly
    about grief without any concept of loss. It can diagnose cancer without
    understanding what illness feels like. It gets better and better at the surface of
    things while the depth remains completely empty. Whether understanding is even
    necessary is one of the open questions of our time.

  Q8 — Hard — Myth bust
  "Because AI learns from human-produced data it will naturally reflect human values accurately."
  False · Wrong.
    It reflects the values present in the data: which means the values of whoever
    produced that data, which is not all humans equally. Data from the internet
    overrepresents wealthy English-speaking Western perspectives. Historical data
    overrepresents whoever had power in the past. The AI doesn't know this. It just
    learned from what it was given.


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Module 6 — What AI Gets Wrong
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Myth bust — Confidence Is Not Accuracy
  AI systems are often most confident when they are most wrong. They have no internal
  sense of uncertainty. They produce answers the same way whether they are completely
  right or completely fabricating. This is one of the most dangerous things about
  deploying AI in high-stakes situations: the output looks the same regardless of
  whether it should be trusted.

  Slide 2 — Example — Hallucinations
  Ask an AI to cite its sources and it will often invent citations that don't exist:
  real-sounding journal names, real-sounding authors, plausible-looking dates. It isn't
  lying. It doesn't know what lying is. It's pattern-matching what a citation looks like
  and producing one. The result is indistinguishable from a real citation unless you
  check. Lawyers have submitted fake AI-generated citations in court. Judges were not
  amused.

  Slide 3 — Hot take — Bias Is Baked In
  In 2015 Google Photos automatically labeled Black people as gorillas. In 2018 Amazon
  scrapped a hiring AI because it penalized resumes that included the word "women's." In
  2019 a healthcare algorithm gave Black patients lower risk scores than equally sick
  white patients, reducing their access to care. None of these were glitches. They were
  the algorithms working correctly on biased data. Fixing the output without fixing the
  data fixes nothing.

  Slide 4 — Example — The Edge Case Problem
  AI fails at edge cases: situations just outside its training. A self-driving car
  trained on American roads struggles in Vietnam. A medical AI trained on adult X-rays
  misreads pediatric ones. A translation AI handles formal English well and falls apart
  on slang. Every AI has a boundary where its training ends and its confidence
  continues. The dangerous moment is when it crosses that boundary without anyone
  noticing.

  Slide 5 — Myth bust — More Data Doesn't Fix Everything
  The common response to AI failures is: train it on more data. Sometimes that helps.
  Sometimes it just scales the problem. An AI trained on more biased data is more
  confidently biased. An AI trained on more internet text absorbs more misinformation at
  higher volume. More data makes AI more powerful. It doesn't make AI more right. Power
  and correctness are not the same thing.

  Slide 6 — Hot take — The Automation Bias
  When a human makes a decision people question it. When an AI makes the same decision
  people often accept it without question, because it feels objective, scientific,
  unchallengeable. This is called automation bias and it is extremely well documented.
  Judges give harsher sentences when an algorithm recommends it. Doctors defer to
  diagnostic AI even when their own instinct says otherwise. The appearance of
  objectivity is its own kind of manipulation.

  Slide 7 — Example — When It Really Goes Wrong
  In 2020 the Dutch government used an AI fraud detection system to flag welfare
  recipients for investigation. The algorithm disproportionately targeted people with
  dual nationalities and low incomes. 26,000 families were wrongly accused. Some lost
  their homes. The government eventually fell over the scandal. The AI didn't intend any
  of this. It just optimized the metric it was given. The metric was the wrong one.
  Nobody caught it for years.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — The Accountability Gap
  When a human makes a mistake there is someone to hold responsible. When an AI makes a
  mistake the responsibility dissolves. The company says it's the data. The data
  collectors say it's the model. The model builders say it's how it was deployed. The
  deployers say it's how it was used. Nobody is accountable and the person harmed has no
  clear path to justice. Figuring out how to close this gap is one of the most important
  unsolved problems of our time.

QUIZ

  Q1 — Easy — Myth bust
  "When an AI gives a confident answer it's more likely to be correct."
  False · Wrong.
    AI systems produce answers with the same apparent confidence regardless of whether
    they are right or completely fabricating. They have no internal sense of
    uncertainty. This is one of the most dangerous properties of deployed AI, the output
    looks identical whether it should be trusted or not.

  Q2 — Easy — Fact
  "AI hallucinations happen because AI has no way to check whether what it produces is real."
  True · Correct.
    AI has no concept of truth or lying. When it invents citations or fabricates
    statistics it's doing exactly what it was trained to do, produce text that matches
    patterns. It doesn't know the difference between real and invented. That's not a
    moral failure. It's a structural one.

  Q3 — Easy — Myth bust
  "AI bias is always the result of someone deliberately programming discrimination into the system."
  False · Wrong.
    Bias almost never comes from deliberate programming. It comes from biased training
    data. Amazon's hiring AI penalized women because most historical hires were men. The
    engineers didn't program that. The history did.

  Q4 — Medium — Fact
  "Automation bias means people tend to trust AI decisions more than equivalent human decisions."
  True · Correct.
    This is well documented across medicine, law, and finance. AI decisions feel
    objective, scientific, unchallengeable. Judges give harsher sentences when an
    algorithm recommends it. Doctors defer to diagnostic AI against their own judgment.
    The appearance of objectivity is itself a form of influence.

  Q5 — Medium — Myth bust
  "Training AI on more diverse data completely eliminates bias."
  False · Wrong.
    More diverse data reduces some biases. It doesn't eliminate bias. It just changes
    which biases remain. Deciding what counts as diverse, who gets to decide, and how to
    source it are unsolved problems. More diverse training is better. It is not a
    complete solution.

  Q6 — Medium — Hot take
  "AI systems fail most dangerously when they are obviously wrong."
  False · Wrong.
    They fail most dangerously when they are subtly wrong in ways that are hard to
    detect at scale. An AI that is slightly wrong in a consistent direction, deployed to
    millions of people making consequential decisions. That is where serious harm
    accumulates. The Dutch welfare scandal, discriminatory lending, biased healthcare:
    none were obvious failures. They were systematic ones.

  Q7 — Hard — Fact
  "When an AI system causes harm it is often unclear who is legally responsible."
  True · Correct.
    This is the accountability gap. The company blames the data. The data collectors
    blame the model. The model builders blame the deployment. Nobody is clearly
    responsible and the person harmed has no clear legal path. Laws have not kept up
    with the technology.

  Q8 — Hard — Hot take
  "The solution to AI getting things wrong is to slow down deployment until the technology is more reliable."
  False · Genuinely complicated.
    Slower deployment would reduce certain harms. It would also delay benefits: earlier
    cancer detection, more accessible education, better translation. The question of how
    to balance speed against reliability is one of the central unresolved debates in AI
    policy. There is no clean answer. Anyone who gives you one isn't taking the
    tradeoffs seriously.

Module 7 — What Is AI?
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Big idea — The Question We Started With
  Aristotle asked whether reasoning could be mechanical. Turing asked whether machines
  could think. McCarthy named the field Artificial Intelligence before anyone agreed on
  what intelligence meant. You have now spent six lessons looking at what AI actually
  is, what it does, how it learns, where it lives, what it gets wrong. Now the question
  is yours. What is it?

  Slide 2 — Big idea — What We Know It Is
  AI is pattern matching at scale. It is trained on human-produced data. It reflects the
  values and biases of whoever built it and whatever they fed it. It has no
  understanding, no awareness, no desires. It is extraordinarily powerful at specific
  tasks. It fails unpredictably at edges. It is deployed faster than our ability to
  understand its consequences. These are facts. They are not in dispute.

  Slide 3 — Big idea — What We Don't Know
  Whether AI will ever be conscious. Whether understanding is necessary for
  intelligence. Whether current AI systems experience anything at all. Whether general
  AI is possible or what it would mean if it arrived. Whether the economic disruption
  will be manageable or catastrophic. Whether the benefits will be distributed or
  concentrated. None of these questions have agreed answers. Anyone who tells you
  otherwise is either lying or selling something.

  Slide 4 — Hot take — The Tool Argument
  Some people say AI is just a tool. Like a hammer or a calculator: powerful, useful,
  shaped entirely by whoever wields it. On this view the question isn't what AI is but
  what humans choose to do with it. The technology is neutral. The choices aren't. This
  argument has a lot of truth in it. It also conveniently removes responsibility from
  the people building the tools.

  Slide 5 — Hot take — The Mind Argument
  Some people say AI is something new, not a tool but a kind of mind. Not human, not the
  robots of science fiction, but a third thing we don't have good language for yet. On
  this view treating AI as just a tool is a category error that will lead us to make
  serious mistakes. This argument also has truth in it. It also risks anthropomorphizing
  systems that may warrant no such consideration.

  Slide 6 — Scenario — The Turing Question Revisited
  Turing asked: if a machine can convince you it's thinking, does it matter whether it
  really is? You've now seen what's inside the machine. You know it's pattern matching.
  You know there's no understanding. Does that change your answer? Should it? Or is the
  output, the conversation, the art, the diagnosis, the recommendation, what actually
  matters, regardless of what produced it?

  Slide 7 — Big idea — Why Your Answer Matters
  How you answer the question what is AI determines how you vote on regulations, what
  jobs you think are safe, how you treat AI-generated content, whether you trust AI
  systems in medicine and law and education, and what kind of future you think is
  possible. This isn't abstract philosophy. It's the most practical question of your
  generation. And unlike most of the questions in this course, nobody can answer it for
  you.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — The End Of The Beginning
  You started this world not knowing what AI was. You end it knowing what it does, how
  it learns, where it lives, what it gets wrong, and why none of the easy answers about
  what it is hold up under pressure. That is not a small thing. Most people who use AI
  every day have never thought this carefully about it. You have. That makes you
  something the world actually needs right now, someone who can think clearly about the
  most consequential technology of your lifetime.

  Q2 — Easy — Myth bust
  "AI has understanding, awareness, and desires. It just doesn't show them."
  False · Wrong.
    There is no evidence of understanding, awareness, or desires in any current AI
    system. It produces outputs that feel like these things because it was trained on

  Q3 — Easy — Fact
  "The people who built ChatGPT cannot fully explain why it gives every answer it gives."
  True · Correct.
    This is the interpretability problem: one of the most active areas of AI research.
    The systems are so complex their builders cannot explain individual outputs. You are
    using systems that their creators don't fully understand. That is not a reason to

  Q4 — Medium — Hot take
  "Calling AI 'just a tool' is a complete and accurate description of what it is."
  False · Incomplete.
    There is truth in the tool argument, AI is shaped by human choices and its impact
    depends on how it's used. But it also removes responsibility from builders, suggests

  Q5 — Medium — Fact
  "How you answer the question 'what is AI?' affects real decisions you will make throughout your life."
  True · Correct.

  Q6 — Medium — Myth bust
  "Because AI is trained on human data it will always reflect human values."
  False · Wrong.
    It reflects the values present in its training data: which represents some humans

  Q7 — Hard — Hot take
  "As AI becomes more capable the question of what it is becomes less important."
  False · Wrong. It becomes more important.
    The more capable AI systems become the more consequential our answers are. If we

  Q8 — Hard — Big idea
  "Understanding how AI works technically is the most important thing about it."
  False · Wrong.
    The most important thing is what it's being used for, who it's affecting, who

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Module 1 — What Is A Neural Network
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Myth bust — Not Magic, Just Math
  A neural network sounds like science fiction. It isn't. It's a mathematical system
  loosely inspired by how your brain works: billions of simple calculations happening
  simultaneously, organized in layers, producing an output. The word "neural" makes it
  sound alive. It isn't. It's a very large, very organized system of math that learned
  something. Every AI product you use today runs on some version of this.

  Slide 2 — Fact — Your Brain First
  Your brain has about 86 billion neurons. Each one receives signals from thousands of
  others, processes them, and either fires or doesn't. When you learned to read, certain
  neurons fired together so often that the connection strengthened permanently. That's
  literally what learning is in your brain: connections getting stronger or weaker based
  on use. AI borrowed this idea. Just the idea.

  Slide 3 — Example — The Artificial Neuron
  An artificial neuron does one thing: takes a bunch of numbers in, multiplies each by a
  weight, adds them up, and outputs a number. That's it. One neuron is trivial and
  useless. Stack millions of them in layers, connect them, and train them on enough
  data: now patterns emerge that nobody programmed and nobody fully understands. The
  intelligence isn't in any single neuron. It's in the connections.

  Slide 4 — Example — The Layers
  Neural networks are organized in layers. The first layer receives raw input: pixels,
  words, numbers. Each subsequent layer finds more abstract patterns in the previous
  layer's output. In an image recognition network, early layers detect edges. Middle
  layers detect shapes. Later layers detect objects. Nobody told it to organize this
  way. It figured it out during training. That emergence is what makes neural networks
  so powerful and so hard to interpret.

  Slide 5 — Hot take — Inspired By But Not Like Your Brain
  Neural networks are often described as working like the human brain. This is
  misleading. Your brain has 86 billion neurons with 100 trillion connections, runs on
  20 watts of power, learns from a handful of examples, and generalizes across
  completely different domains instantly. Current neural networks need millions of
  examples, thousands of watts, and fall apart outside their training. The inspiration
  was real. The similarity ends there.

  Slide 6 — Fact — The Weights Are Everything
  Every connection between neurons has a weight: a number that determines how much
  influence one neuron has on the next. A freshly created network has random weights and
  produces random outputs. Training is the process of adjusting those weights millions
  of times until the network produces correct outputs. The entire knowledge of a trained
  neural network lives in its weights. Change the weights and you change what it knows.
  Delete the weights and everything it learned is gone.

  Slide 7 — Scenario — What Actually Happens When You Use ChatGPT
  When ChatGPT answers your question, billions of weights are applied across hundreds of
  layers in milliseconds. Each layer transforms the input slightly. From raw text to
  something more abstract, then more abstract again. By the final layer the network has
  gone from your words to a probability distribution over every possible next word. It
  picks the most likely one. Then does it again. That's the whole thing. Billions of
  multiplications, running very fast.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Simple Units, Emergent Complexity
  The most important thing about neural networks: none of the individual pieces are
  intelligent. Each neuron does trivial math. Each layer does slightly more complex
  math. But somewhere in the combination of millions of trivial operations, behavior
  emerges that looks like understanding, creativity, and reasoning. Whether that
  appearance reflects anything real is a question nobody has answered. That gap, between
  what neural networks do and what they seem to do, is where most of the important
  debates about AI live.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Module 2 — How Networks Train
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Myth bust — It Wasn't Programmed
  When a neural network learns to recognize your face, nobody wrote rules about faces.
  Nobody described cheekbones or eye spacing. The network was shown millions of labeled
  examples and adjusted itself until it got them right. This is the fundamental shift
  from traditional software to AI. Traditional software does what it's told. Neural
  networks figure it out. The difference sounds subtle. It changes everything about how
  they work and why they fail.

  Slide 2 — Example — The Forward Pass
  Training starts with a forward pass. You feed the network an input: say, a photo of a
  cat. The data travels through every layer, each neuron applying its weights. At the
  end the network produces an output. In this case, its guess about what's in the photo.
  On the first attempt with random weights, that guess is basically random. Maybe it
  says "truck." That's fine. That's where training begins.

  Slide 3 — Example — The Error Signal
  After the forward pass the network compares its output to the correct answer. It said
  "truck." The correct answer was "cat." The difference between those two things is the
  error. The bigger the error, the more the network needs to adjust. This error signal
  is the entire engine of learning. Without knowing how wrong it is, the network has no
  direction to improve. The error is not a failure. It's information.

  Slide 4 — Example — Backpropagation
  Once the error is calculated, the network works backwards, layer by layer, adjusting
  each weight slightly in the direction that would have produced a smaller error. This
  is called backpropagation. It's the algorithm that makes neural networks trainable. It
  was described mathematically in the 1970s but nobody thought it would work at scale.
  In 2012 it turned out to work spectacularly at scale. Most of modern AI runs on it.

  Slide 5 — Hot take — Millions Of Times
  One forward pass and one backpropagation step adjusts the network a tiny amount. To
  actually learn something useful the network needs to run this cycle millions or
  billions of times across millions of examples. Training a large language model like
  GPT-4 takes months on thousands of specialized processors running continuously. The
  electricity cost alone is millions of dollars. The environmental cost is significant
  and rarely discussed. Learning at this scale is not cheap.

  Slide 6 — Fact — Overfitting
  A network that trains too long on the same data memorizes it instead of learning from
  it. It gets perfect scores on training data and falls apart on anything new. This is
  called overfitting. It's one of the most common problems in AI development. The fix is
  testing the network on data it has never seen during training: if performance drops
  dramatically, it overfit. Avoiding overfitting requires careful judgment about when to
  stop training.

  Slide 7 — Scenario — What The Network Actually Learns
  After millions of training cycles on labeled images, a network has adjusted billions
  of weights into a configuration that reliably distinguishes cats from trucks. Nobody
  knows exactly why those specific weights work. Nobody can look at a weight and say
  "this one is responsible for detecting ears." The knowledge is distributed across
  billions of numbers in a way that works but cannot be read. The network learned
  something. We can confirm it learned something. We just can't see what.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Learning Without A Teacher
  Backpropagation requires labeled data: someone has to say this is a cat, this is spam,
  this loan defaulted. For every large AI system someone had to label enormous amounts
  of data first. That labor is invisible in the final product. But researchers have also
  developed ways for networks to learn from unlabeled data: finding patterns without
  being told what they mean. The boundary between supervised and unsupervised learning
  is where some of the most interesting AI research is happening right now.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Module 3 — Deep Learning
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — Depth Changes Everything
  A shallow neural network has one or two layers between input and output. A deep neural
  network has many: sometimes hundreds. The difference isn't just size. Each layer
  learns to represent the world at a different level of abstraction. Shallow networks
  can learn simple patterns. Deep networks can learn concepts. The word "deep" in deep
  learning refers to this depth of layers. It's the thing that changed AI from a
  promising research area into something that actually works.

  Slide 2 — Example — What Each Layer Sees
  In a deep image recognition network, layer 1 detects raw edges and gradients. Layer 2
  combines edges into corners and curves. Layer 3 combines curves into shapes. Layer 4
  combines shapes into object parts. Layer 5 combines parts into objects. Nobody
  designed this hierarchy. It emerged from training. The network built its own
  understanding of visual structure, layer by layer, from raw pixels up to meaning.

  Slide 3 — Hot take — The 2012 Moment
  Before 2012 the best image recognition systems used hand-crafted features: researchers
  manually designed what the system should look for. AlexNet, the deep learning system
  that won the 2012 ImageNet competition, used none of that. It learned its own features
  from scratch. And it won by such a large margin that the entire field switched
  approaches almost overnight. A decade of hand-crafted feature engineering was
  abandoned in a year.

  Slide 4 — Fact — Why It Needed GPUs
  Deep learning requires enormous amounts of matrix multiplication: the same operation,
  over and over, on large grids of numbers. CPUs are designed for complex varied tasks.
  GPUs were designed for graphics: repeating the same simple calculation millions of
  times simultaneously. It turned out that GPUs were perfect for deep learning. The
  availability of cheap, powerful GPUs in the 2010s is one of the main reasons deep
  learning exploded when it did. Gaming hardware accidentally unlocked modern AI.

  Slide 5 — Example — Transfer Learning
  Training a deep network from scratch takes enormous data and compute. Transfer
  learning is a shortcut: take a network already trained on a large general dataset,
  then fine-tune it on your specific smaller dataset. The network already knows edges,
  shapes, and objects. You just teach it your particular task on top of that foundation.
  Most practical AI applications today use transfer learning. Training from scratch is
  something mostly large research labs bother with.

  Slide 6 — Myth bust — More Layers Isn't Always Better
  Adding more layers doesn't always improve performance. Too many layers and the error
  signal from backpropagation fades before it reaches the early layers. They stop
  learning. This is the vanishing gradient problem and it stumped researchers for years.
  The solution, residual connections that let the error signal skip layers, was
  discovered in 2015 and enabled networks hundreds of layers deep. Modern networks can
  have over a thousand layers. Without residual connections they would be untrainable.

  Slide 7 — Scenario — What Deep Learning Can Do That Shallow Learning Can't
  A shallow network can learn to detect spam email. A deep network can learn to
  understand context. That the same sentence means something different depending on what
  came before it. A shallow network can classify images into categories. A deep network
  can describe what's happening in an image in natural language. The qualitative leap
  from shallow to deep isn't just better performance on the same tasks. It's the ability
  to do tasks that were previously impossible.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Representations All The Way Down
  The key insight of deep learning is that useful representations of the world can be
  learned from data rather than designed by hand. The network doesn't need to be told
  what a face looks like or what makes a sentence grammatical. Given enough data and
  enough layers it will discover representations that capture these things. This is why
  deep learning generalized across so many domains: vision, language, audio, games,
  science. The same basic approach works everywhere because representations are
  everywhere.

Module 4 — How ChatGPT Works
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Myth bust — It's Not Thinking
  When ChatGPT writes an essay, answers a question, or holds a conversation, it is not
  thinking. It is predicting. At every step it looks at everything written so far and
  predicts what word should come next, based on patterns learned from hundreds of
  billions of words of text. It does this one word at a time, each prediction feeding
  the next. The result can look like thought. It is very fast, very sophisticated
  pattern completion.

  Slide 2 — Fact — The Training Data
  ChatGPT was trained on an enormous amount of text: books, websites, code, articles,
  conversations. The exact composition is not fully disclosed but it's estimated at
  hundreds of billions of words. The network read all of it, in the sense that it
  adjusted its weights based on all of it, and learned to predict what comes next in any
  kind of text. That training is what gives it the appearance of knowing things. It
  learned the patterns. The patterns contain the knowledge.

  Slide 3 — Example — The Transformer
  ChatGPT is built on an architecture called the transformer, introduced by Google
  researchers in 2017. The key innovation is attention: the ability for every word in a
  sequence to directly influence every other word, regardless of distance. Before
  transformers, networks processed text sequentially and struggled with long-range
  dependencies. Transformers process everything at once. That's what allows ChatGPT to
  maintain coherence across a long conversation. Attention is the mechanism that makes
  it work.

  Slide 4 — Example — Tokens Not Words
  ChatGPT doesn't process words. It processes tokens. A token is roughly a word or part
  of a word. "Unbelievable" might be two tokens. "AI" is one. The model converts text to
  tokens, processes the sequence of tokens through its layers, and produces
  probabilities over the next token. This tokenization is why ChatGPT sometimes
  struggles with letter counting or wordplay. It never sees individual letters, only
  chunks.

  Slide 5 — Hot take — It Has No Memory
  Every time you start a new conversation with ChatGPT, it starts from zero. It has no
  memory of previous conversations. Within a single conversation it can refer back to
  what was said: that's in its context window. But close the window and it's gone. The
  model has no persistent memory of you, your preferences, or your history. What feels
  like a relationship is reconstructed fresh each time from the patterns in its weights.
  You remember the conversation. It doesn't.

  Slide 6 — Fact — The Context Window
  ChatGPT can only consider a limited amount of text at once: its context window.
  Everything outside that window is invisible to it. Early versions had small context
  windows and would lose track of the beginning of long conversations. Recent versions
  have much larger windows: some can hold entire books. The context window is one of the
  fundamental constraints of transformer models. Everything the model knows about your
  conversation has to fit inside it.

  Slide 7 — Scenario — Why It Hallucinates
  ChatGPT hallucinates, confidently producing false information, because it has no
  mechanism to check whether what it's predicting is true. It predicts the most
  plausible next token given everything before it. A plausible-sounding citation is a
  plausible next token. A confident but wrong fact is a plausible next token. Truth and
  plausibility are not the same thing. The model optimizes for plausibility. It has no
  access to truth. That gap is where hallucinations live.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — The Imitation Game At Scale
  What ChatGPT does is, at some level, an extreme version of Turing's imitation game. It
  was trained to produce text that looks like human text. It does this so well that the
  output is often indistinguishable from what a human would write. Whether that means it
  understands anything: whether producing human-like output requires or produces
  anything like human-like cognition: is the question that makes AI researchers,
  philosophers, and neuroscientists argue. The imitation is real. What lies behind it is
  not agreed upon.

Module 5 — How Recommendation Algorithms Decide
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — You Have A Profile
  Every major platform, YouTube, TikTok, Spotify, Netflix, Instagram, builds a model of
  you. Not a file with your name in it. A mathematical representation of your behavior:
  what you watch, how long, what you skip, what you share, what time of day, what
  device. This representation is updated continuously. Every interaction you have with
  the platform adjusts your model. The algorithm uses that model to predict what you'll
  engage with next.

  Slide 2 — Example — Collaborative Filtering
  The simplest recommendation approach: find people who behaved like you in the past and
  show you what they liked next. You watched three specific films: other users who
  watched those same three films also watched this fourth one. So you get recommended
  the fourth. This is collaborative filtering. It doesn't need to understand what any
  film is about. It just finds behavioral patterns across millions of users. Most early
  recommendation systems were built this way.

  Slide 3 — Example — Content-Based Filtering
  A second approach: analyze the content itself rather than user behavior. A song has
  tempo, key, instrumentation, vocal style. A user who likes songs with these
  characteristics probably likes other songs with these characteristics. Spotify's early
  recommendation system was partly built this way. It analyzed the audio of songs to
  find musical similarity. The advantage is it can recommend things nobody else has
  listened to yet. The disadvantage is it can't surprise you.

  Slide 4 — Hot take — The Engagement Trap
  Modern recommendation algorithms don't optimize for your satisfaction or happiness.
  They optimize for engagement: the metric that predicts revenue. Engagement means time
  spent, clicks, shares, comments. Research has repeatedly shown that outrage, anxiety,
  and conflict drive more engagement than calm, informative content. The algorithm
  doesn't know this. It just learned that certain content gets more clicks. The result
  is systems that systematically amplify the most emotionally activating content
  regardless of its accuracy or effect on wellbeing.

  Slide 5 — Fact — The Cold Start Problem
  When you're a new user a recommendation algorithm knows nothing about you. This is the
  cold start problem. It has to guess. Most platforms solve this by asking you to pick
  some initial preferences, then rapidly updating based on your first few interactions.
  TikTok is particularly aggressive at this. It can build a detailed behavioral model
  within 30 minutes of a new account's first use. The algorithm moves from knowing
  nothing to knowing a lot very fast.

  Slide 6 — Scenario — The Filter Bubble
  As the algorithm learns your preferences it shows you more of what you already like.
  This feels good: the feed gets more relevant. But it also means you see less of what
  you don't already know you like. New ideas, opposing viewpoints, and unfamiliar
  content get filtered out. Not by conspiracy. Just by optimization. The algorithm is
  doing exactly what it was designed to do, maximize engagement by showing you what
  keeps you engaged. The filter bubble is a side effect of success.

  Slide 7 — Myth bust — You Can't Fully Opt Out
  Even if you actively try to confuse the algorithm, watching things you don't like,
  clicking randomly, it adapts. It builds a model of you confusing it. It finds the
  patterns in your confusion. The only real opt-out is not using the platform. The
  algorithm is designed to model any behavioral pattern, including attempts to hide your
  preferences. Your data is always being collected. Your model is always being updated.
  There is no neutral use.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — The Attention Economy
  Recommendation algorithms exist because human attention is scarce and valuable.
  Advertisers pay for access to attention. Platforms sell that access. The algorithm's
  job is to maximize the amount of attention each user gives to the platform. Everything
  else, the entertainment, the connection, the information, is in service of that goal.
  Understanding this doesn't make the content less enjoyable. But it changes what you
  think the product actually is. The content is the bait. Your attention is what's being
  sold.

Module 6 — Computer Vision
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Myth bust — Seeing Is Not Understanding
  When an AI identifies a cat in a photo it is not seeing the cat the way you see it. It
  is detecting patterns in pixel values that correlate with what humans have labeled
  "cat" in training data. It has no concept of what a cat is, what it feels like, how it
  moves, what it means to be one. It detects the visual signature. That's not nothing.
  But it's fundamentally different from visual understanding, and confusing the two
  leads to serious mistakes in how we deploy these systems.

  Slide 2 — Example — How Images Become Data
  A digital image is a grid of pixels. Each pixel is three numbers: red, green, and blue
  values from 0 to 255. A 1000x1000 pixel image is 3 million numbers. Computer vision is
  the problem of taking those 3 million numbers and producing meaningful output: what's
  in this image, where are the objects, what are people doing. The raw input is just
  numbers. The meaning has to be learned.

  Slide 3 — Example — Convolutional Networks
  The neural network architecture that made modern computer vision possible is the
  convolutional neural network. It processes images by applying small filters that slide
  across the image, detecting features at each location. Early filters detect edges.
  Later filters detect increasingly complex patterns. The same filter is applied
  everywhere in the image: if a filter detects eyes it detects them wherever they
  appear. This weight sharing is what makes the network efficient enough to work on real
  images.

  Slide 4 — Hot take — What It Gets Wrong
  Computer vision fails in predictable ways. Change the lighting dramatically and
  recognition drops. Rotate an object to an unusual angle and the network may not
  recognize it. Add carefully designed noise invisible to humans, adversarial examples,
  and a network will confidently identify a cat as a toaster. These failures reveal that
  the network learned to recognize visual signatures, not objects. A human who sees a
  cat upside down still knows it's a cat. The network may not.

  Slide 5 — Fact — Where It Works Remarkably Well
  Despite its limitations, computer vision now matches or exceeds human performance on
  many specific tasks. Detecting tumors in medical scans. Identifying defects in
  manufacturing. Reading license plates. Recognizing faces. Describing the contents of
  images for visually impaired users. In conditions similar to its training, consistent
  lighting, typical angles, high image quality, modern computer vision is
  extraordinarily reliable. The key word is conditions.

  Slide 6 — Scenario — Facial Recognition
  Facial recognition is computer vision applied to faces. It powers phone unlocking,
  airport security, and law enforcement tools. It also has well-documented accuracy
  disparities: multiple studies have shown it performs worse on darker-skinned faces,
  women, and older people. These disparities come from training data that overrepresents
  lighter-skinned men. The network learned the patterns it was shown. The deployment
  consequences, wrongful arrests, surveillance, are serious and ongoing.

  Slide 7 — Example — Beyond Classification
  Modern computer vision does more than classify images. Object detection identifies
  multiple objects and their locations. Semantic segmentation labels every pixel in an
  image with a category. Pose estimation tracks body position. Video understanding
  tracks objects across frames. Depth estimation infers 3D structure from 2D images.
  These capabilities power self-driving cars, medical imaging, robotics, and augmented
  reality. The same basic deep learning approach, applied to increasingly complex visual
  tasks.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — The World As Data
  Computer vision is turning the physical world into data. Every camera is potentially a
  sensor feeding AI systems. Traffic cameras, security cameras, satellite imagery,
  medical scans: all of it processable by systems that can detect patterns humans would
  miss or couldn't process at scale. This creates enormous potential benefits in
  medicine, science, and safety. It also creates surveillance infrastructure that can be
  used to monitor populations at scale. The technology doesn't determine which outcome
  happens. The people deploying it do.

Module 7 — The Black Box Problem
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — Nobody Fully Understands Them
  The people who build the most powerful neural networks in the world cannot fully
  explain how they work. They can describe the architecture. They can describe the
  training process. They cannot tell you why the network produces any specific output.
  This is not false modesty. It's a structural property of systems with billions of
  parameters whose knowledge is distributed across all of them simultaneously. The black
  box problem is real and it has serious consequences.

  Slide 2 — Example — What Interpretability Means
  Interpretability research tries to open the black box, to understand what's happening
  inside a neural network. Some progress has been made. Researchers can identify neurons
  that activate for specific concepts. They can find circuits: groups of neurons that
  together perform identifiable operations. But these are partial explanations of small
  pieces of large systems. Nobody has a complete, faithful explanation of what a large
  model is doing when it produces any given output. The field is active. The problem is
  genuinely hard.

  Slide 3 — Hot take — This Is Unusual In Engineering
  In most engineering, you understand what you build. A bridge engineer can calculate
  exactly why the bridge holds. A software engineer can read the code and trace exactly
  why the program behaves as it does. Neural networks are different. The behavior
  emerges from training in ways that can't be read back. We have systems making
  consequential decisions, in medicine, law, finance, that their builders cannot fully
  explain. This is a new situation in the history of technology. We haven't fully
  reckoned with it.

  Slide 4 — Scenario — When It Matters
  A medical AI recommends against a treatment. A loan algorithm denies a mortgage. A
  hiring system rejects a candidate. In each case the person affected asks: why? The
  answer is almost always: the model assigned a lower score based on patterns in
  training data. Which patterns? We don't know exactly. What should change to get a
  different result? We can't say with certainty. The black box problem isn't abstract
  when you're the person who got denied and nobody can explain why.

  Slide 5 — Fact — The EU Has Tried To Address This
  The European Union's GDPR includes a right to explanation: people affected by
  automated decisions are entitled to an explanation of how the decision was made.
  Implementing this right turns out to be technically very difficult. Current AI systems
  often can't produce explanations that are both faithful to their actual reasoning and
  comprehensible to a human. Researchers are working on this. The gap between the legal
  requirement and the technical capability is wide.

  Slide 6 — Example — Saliency Maps
  One partial solution: saliency maps. Take an image classification network and
  highlight which pixels most influenced the output. If the network says "dog" the
  saliency map shows which parts of the image drove that decision. Sometimes it's
  reassuring, the highlighted area is the dog. Sometimes it's alarming, the highlighted
  area is the background, or a watermark, or something unrelated. Saliency maps give a
  window into the network's attention but not a full explanation of its reasoning.

  Slide 7 — Myth bust — Explainable AI Solves The Problem
  There's a field called Explainable AI (XAI) devoted to making neural networks more
  interpretable. It has produced useful tools. It has not solved the problem. Most XAI
  methods produce approximations: simplified explanations that roughly capture what the
  network does without being fully faithful to the actual computation. Using an
  approximate explanation to make a high-stakes decision is better than nothing. It's
  not the same as actually understanding the system. That gap is real.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Power Without Understanding
  We have built systems more powerful than anything in history whose internal workings
  we don't fully understand, and we are deploying them to make decisions that affect
  people's lives. This is not an argument against neural networks. It's an argument for
  humility, for ongoing research into interpretability, for regulatory frameworks that
  require meaningful explanations, and for keeping humans in the loop on high-stakes
  decisions. The black box is not inevitable. It's a current limitation that we should
  be actively working to reduce.

QUIZ

  Q1 — Easy — Fact
  "The people who build the most powerful neural networks can fully explain how they work."
  False · Wrong.
    They can describe the architecture and training process. They cannot tell you why
    the network produces any specific output. This is not false modesty. It's a
    structural property of systems with billions of parameters whose knowledge is
    distributed across all of them simultaneously. The black box problem is real.

  Q2 — Easy — Example
  "Interpretability research tries to understand what is happening inside neural networks."
  True · Correct.

  Q3 — Easy — Hot take
  "In most engineering disciplines, builders fully understand why their systems behave as they do."
  True · Correct.
    A bridge engineer can calculate exactly why the bridge holds. A software engineer
    can trace exactly why the program behaves as it does. Neural networks are different:
    their behavior emerges from training in ways that can't be read back. We have a new
    kind of engineering where the builder doesn't fully understand what was built.

  Q4 — Medium — Fact
  "The EU's GDPR gives people the right to a full technical explanation of any automated decision that affects them."
  False · Wrong. It's more complicated.

    faithful to their actual reasoning and comprehensible to a human. The gap between
    the legal requirement and the technical capability is wide and unresolved.

  Q5 — Medium — Example
  "Saliency maps show which parts of an input most influenced a neural network's output."
  True · Correct.
    Take an image classification network and highlight which pixels most influenced the
    output. Sometimes the highlighted area is reassuringly the dog. Sometimes it's
    alarmingly the background or a watermark. Saliency maps give a partial window into
    the network's attention, not a full explanation of its reasoning.

  Q6 — Medium — Myth bust
  "Explainable AI tools produce explanations that are completely faithful to the actual computation inside the network."
  False · Wrong.

  Q7 — Hard — Scenario
  "The black box problem is only a concern for AI researchers. It doesn't affect ordinary people."
  False · Wrong.
    A medical AI recommends against a treatment. A loan algorithm denies a mortgage. A
    hiring system rejects a candidate. The person affected asks why. The answer is
    almost always: the model assigned a lower score based on patterns in training data:
    which patterns, we can't say exactly. The black box problem is most consequential
    for ordinary people making high-stakes decisions.

  Q8 — Hard — Big idea
  "The fact that we can't fully explain neural networks is an argument for keeping humans in the loop on high-stakes decisions."
  True · Correct.

Module 8 — When Decisions Go Wrong
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — Scale Changes Everything
  A human making a bad decision affects the people immediately around them. An AI system
  making a bad decision, deployed to millions of people, affects millions of people:
  simultaneously, automatically, without anyone reviewing each case. Scale is what makes
  AI failures categorically different from human failures. A biased human hiring manager
  might affect dozens of candidates. A biased hiring algorithm deployed across an
  industry affects hundreds of thousands. The same error rate produces completely
  different consequences at different scales.

  Slide 2 — Example — The Dutch Welfare Scandal
  Between 2013 and 2019 the Dutch government used an AI system to detect fraud in
  welfare payments. The algorithm flagged 26,000 families for investigation based on
  factors including nationality, postal code, and income level. The system
  disproportionately targeted families with dual nationalities. Many were wrongly
  accused. Some lost their benefits. Some lost their homes. The government eventually
  fell over the scandal. The algorithm wasn't broken. It optimized the metric it was
  given. The metric was the wrong one. Nobody caught it for years.

  Slide 3 — Example — COMPAS
  COMPAS is a risk assessment tool used in US courts to predict the likelihood of
  reoffending. Judges use its scores to inform sentencing. An investigation by
  ProPublica found the tool was twice as likely to incorrectly flag Black defendants as
  high risk compared to white defendants. The company disputed the methodology.
  Researchers disagreed about the right statistical framework. Meanwhile the tool
  continued to be used in sentencing decisions. The technical dispute doesn't change the
  human consequence.

  Slide 4 — Hot take — Automation At The Speed Of Mistakes
  Human decision-making is slow. That's partly a bug, slow decisions miss opportunities.
  It's also partly a feature: slow decisions create opportunities for review,
  correction, and accountability. AI decision-making is fast. Loan applications
  processed in milliseconds. Content moderation at a billion posts per day. Benefits
  eligibility determined automatically. The speed removes the natural checkpoints where
  errors get caught. By the time a systematic mistake is discovered it may have affected
  millions of people.

  Slide 5 — Fact — Feedback Loops
  AI systems can create feedback loops that amplify their own errors. A predictive
  policing algorithm sends more police to certain neighborhoods. More police means more
  arrests in those neighborhoods. More arrests means more data confirming those
  neighborhoods as high-crime. The algorithm recommends more policing. The loop
  tightens. The original bias gets baked in more deeply with each cycle. Breaking a
  feedback loop requires recognizing it exists, which requires someone to be looking for
  it.

  Slide 6 — Myth bust — Fixing The Algorithm Fixes The Problem
  When a biased AI system is discovered, the instinct is to fix the algorithm. Retrain
  it on better data. Adjust the objective function. Add fairness constraints. This
  helps. It doesn't fix everything. The decisions already made under the biased system
  stand. The people harmed by those decisions are rarely compensated. The structural
  conditions that produced the biased training data, historical discrimination, unequal
  data collection, still exist. Fixing the algorithm is necessary. It's not sufficient.

  Slide 7 — Scenario — Who Is Responsible
  A benefits algorithm wrongly denies someone housing assistance. They appeal. The
  agency says the algorithm made the decision. The algorithm's developer says it
  performed as specified. The specification was written by a contractor. The contractor
  says they built what they were asked to build. Nobody is clearly responsible. Nobody
  is held accountable. The person denied housing has no clear path to remedy. This is
  not hypothetical. It's the pattern in virtually every documented AI harm case. The
  accountability gap is the central unsolved problem of AI governance.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — The Question Of Control
  Every AI failure we've discussed, bias, hallucination, feedback loops, accountability
  gaps, has a common thread: humans built systems and then lost meaningful control over
  their consequences. Not because AI became sentient. Because the systems scaled faster
  than our ability to understand, monitor, and correct them. The question for your
  generation is not whether AI will be powerful. It will be. The question is whether the
  people and institutions with power over AI systems will choose to maintain meaningful
  human oversight over decisions that affect human lives. That choice is political,
  legal, and ethical. It is not technical. It is yours to make.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Module 1 — AI and Jobs
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — The Automation Wave
  AI is automating tasks faster than any previous technology in history. Not just
  physical labor: cognitive work too. Writing, coding, designing, analyzing,
  translating, diagnosing. The jobs most affected are not the ones most people expected.
  Physical jobs that require dexterity and judgment in unpredictable environments are
  harder to automate than knowledge work that follows patterns. The pattern is what AI
  replaces.

  Slide 2 — Example — What's Already Gone
  Some jobs have already largely disappeared or shrunk dramatically. Travel agents. Bank
  tellers. Toll booth operators. Data entry clerks. Film photo developers. These didn't
  vanish overnight. They declined over decades as automation improved. AI is
  accelerating that process. The World Economic Forum estimates AI will displace 85
  million jobs by 2025 while creating 97 million new ones. The net number is positive.
  The transition is not painless.

  Slide 3 — Example — What's Being Automated Now
  Tasks currently being automated at scale: writing first drafts, generating code,
  screening job applications, answering customer service questions, producing legal
  documents, analyzing medical images, creating marketing content, translating
  languages, summarizing documents. These aren't fringe tasks: they're significant parts
  of what lawyers, doctors, writers, programmers, and analysts do every day.

  Slide 4 — Hot take — White Collar Jobs Are More Vulnerable Than Expected
  The conventional wisdom was that AI would automate blue collar work first: factory
  jobs, truck driving, physical labor. The reality is different. AI is currently better
  at pattern-based knowledge work than at physical tasks requiring dexterity and
  judgment in unpredictable environments. A robot that can fold laundry reliably is
  harder to build than a system that can write a legal brief. The disruption is hitting
  knowledge workers first.

  Slide 5 — Fact — What AI Can't Automate
  Tasks requiring genuine human connection, physical presence, unpredictable judgment,
  and creative originality are harder to automate. Nursing. Teaching in person.
  Plumbing. Therapy. Electrical work. Parenting. These require being in a body, in a
  specific place, responding to an unpredictable human situation in real time. AI can
  assist with all of these. It cannot replace the human at the center of them.

  Slide 6 — Example — New Jobs AI Creates
  Every wave of automation creates new jobs. AI is creating demand for: prompt
  engineers, AI trainers, model evaluators, AI ethicists, synthetic data generators, AI
  policy specialists, machine learning engineers, and people who can work effectively
  alongside AI tools. Many of these jobs didn't exist five years ago. Many of the most
  important ones five years from now don't exist yet.

  Slide 7 — Scenario — The Transition Problem
  The economic argument for AI automation is that it creates more jobs than it destroys
  overall. That's probably true at the macro level. It doesn't help the 55-year-old data
  entry clerk in Ohio whose job disappeared and who doesn't have the resources, time, or
  opportunity to retrain as a machine learning engineer. The aggregate numbers can be
  positive and the individual experience can still be devastating. They can both be true
  at once.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Productivity Without Prosperity
  AI can increase productivity dramatically without that productivity being distributed
  equally. A company that automates half its workforce and doubles its revenue has
  achieved real economic gains. Those gains flow to shareholders. The displaced workers
  are not automatically beneficiaries of the productivity they helped create. How the
  gains from AI automation get distributed. Through wages, taxation, retraining
  programs, or new social contracts: is one of the defining political questions of the
  next decade.

Module 2 — AI and Creativity
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — Machines Are Making Things
  AI systems are now producing images, music, writing, video, code, and design at scale.
  Some of it is indistinguishable from human work. Some of it is better by measurable
  standards. Some of it is winning awards in competitions: sometimes without the judges
  knowing. The question of what counts as creativity, and whether machines can have it,
  is no longer hypothetical. It's being answered in real time by the market, by courts,
  and by artists trying to figure out what their work is worth.

  Slide 2 — Example — AI Art
  Text-to-image systems like Midjourney and DALL-E can generate photorealistic images,
  paintings in any style, illustrations, and graphic design from plain English
  descriptions in seconds. In 2022 an AI-generated image won first place at the Colorado
  State Fair art competition. The artist entered it knowingly. The judges didn't know.
  The result sparked a debate about what art is, what competitions are for, and whether
  disclosure should be required.

  Slide 3 — Example — AI Music
  AI systems can generate original music in any genre, in the style of any artist, at
  any tempo, for any duration. They can clone voices with a few minutes of audio. In
  2023 a fake Drake and The Weeknd song generated using AI went viral before being taken
  down. It had millions of streams. Labels are now building AI tools to generate music
  for advertising, film, and games: faster and cheaper than hiring human composers.

  Slide 4 — Example — AI Writing
  Large language models can write news articles, novels, screenplays, poetry, marketing
  copy, academic papers, and legal documents. The Writers Guild of America went on
  strike in 2023 partly over AI, specifically over whether studios could use AI to
  generate scripts or require writers to edit AI-generated content. The strike resulted
  in some AI protections for writers. The underlying tension between AI capabilities and
  human creative work remains unresolved.

  Slide 5 — Hot take — The Training Data Question
  Every AI creative system learned from human work. Midjourney trained on millions of
  images made by human artists. Language models trained on books written by human
  authors. The artists and authors whose work was used for training were not compensated
  and in most cases did not consent. Multiple lawsuits are ongoing. The question of
  whether training on copyrighted work constitutes infringement has not been resolved in
  court.

  Slide 6 — Fact — What AI Changes About Creative Work
  AI doesn't eliminate creative work. It changes what the work is. A graphic designer
  using AI generates dozens of options in minutes instead of one in hours. The skill
  shifts from execution to curation, direction, and judgment. A writer using AI produces
  more content faster. The skill shifts from drafting to editing, shaping, and deciding
  what's worth keeping. These are real skills. They're different skills. The market for
  them is different.

  Slide 7 — Scenario — The Attribution Problem
  When an AI generates an image, who owns it? The person who wrote the prompt? The
  company that built the model? The artists whose work trained the model? Courts in
  different countries are reaching different conclusions. The US Copyright Office has
  ruled that purely AI-generated work with no human authorship cannot be copyrighted.
  Work with significant human creative input can be. The line between those two things
  is being litigated in real time.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — A New Kind Of Creative Landscape
  Human creative history has always involved tools: brushes, cameras, synthesizers, word
  processors. Each new tool changed what was possible, who could create, and what
  counted as skill. AI is another tool in that sequence, but one that can perform many
  of the tasks that previously required years of training. What that means for the value
  of creative work, for creative education, and for what human creativity is actually
  for is one of the most open questions of this moment.

Module 3 — AI and Privacy
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — You Are Being Watched
  AI-powered surveillance is more pervasive than most people realize. Facial recognition
  cameras in cities. License plate readers on roads. Behavioral tracking on every
  website. Voice data from smart speakers. Location data from phones. Purchase history
  from credit cards. These streams of data are increasingly processed by AI systems that
  can identify patterns, predict behavior, and make inferences that individuals never
  consented to share.

  Slide 2 — Example — Facial Recognition In Public Spaces
  China has deployed one of the most extensive facial recognition surveillance systems
  in the world: hundreds of millions of cameras connected to AI systems that can
  identify individuals in public spaces in real time. It's used for law enforcement,
  traffic management, and social credit scoring. Several US cities including San
  Francisco and Boston have banned government use of facial recognition. Others use it
  routinely. The technology is the same. The policy choices are different.

  Slide 3 — Example — What Your Phone Knows
  Your phone knows where you are at all times. It knows who you call, what you search,
  what you buy, what you read, how fast you walk, how well you sleep, and what your face
  looks like from multiple angles. This data is collected by apps, sold to data brokers,
  and purchased by advertisers, employers, insurance companies, and governments. Most of
  it is disclosed in terms of service that almost nobody reads. The data economy is
  built on this exchange.

  Slide 4 — Hot take — Inferred Data Is Often More Revealing Than Disclosed Data
  The most sensitive information AI systems have about you is often not what you told
  them. It's what they inferred. Your political views inferred from your browsing
  history. Your health conditions inferred from your purchases. Your sexual orientation
  inferred from your social connections. Your mental health inferred from your typing
  patterns. You never disclosed these things. The AI figured them out. This inference
  capability is what makes modern data collection qualitatively different from previous
  eras.

  Slide 5 — Fact — The Data Broker Industry
  Data brokers are companies that collect, aggregate, and sell personal data. There are
  thousands of them. They collect data from public records, social media, app tracking,
  purchase history, and other sources. They sell profiles containing hundreds of data
  points on hundreds of millions of individuals. Their customers include marketers,
  employers, insurers, landlords, and law enforcement. In the US this industry is
  largely unregulated. Most people have no idea it exists.

  Slide 6 — Example — Predictive Policing
  Several US cities have used AI systems to predict where crimes are likely to occur and
  deploy police accordingly. These systems use historical crime data to generate
  predictions. Because historical crime data reflects where police have historically
  focused, which reflects existing biases, the predictions often direct police to the
  same neighborhoods. The system reinforces the pattern it was trained on. Critics argue
  it's not prediction. It's automation of existing bias.

  Slide 7 — Scenario — The Aggregation Problem
  Any single piece of data about you is relatively harmless. Your name. Your employer.
  Your neighborhood. Your physical description. Your daily routine. But combine all of
  them and you have a profile that enables stalking, manipulation, discrimination, and
  harassment. AI makes aggregation cheap and fast. Data brokers do it automatically. The
  aggregation problem is why privacy advocates argue that data minimization, collecting
  less data in the first place, is the only real solution.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Privacy As Infrastructure
  Privacy is often framed as a personal preference: some people care about it, others
  don't. A different framing: privacy is infrastructure for other rights. Without
  privacy, free speech is chilled: people self-censor when they know they're being
  watched. Without privacy, political organizing is harder: surveillance enables
  targeting of dissidents. Without privacy, personal development is constrained: people
  behave differently under observation. What AI surveillance does to privacy it does to
  all the things privacy enables.

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — AI Is Already In Medicine
  AI systems are already deployed in healthcare at scale. They read X-rays and MRIs.
  They screen for diabetic retinopathy. They predict sepsis risk in hospital patients.
  They identify suspicious lesions in colonoscopies. They predict which patients are
  likely to be readmitted. They help schedule surgeries and staff hospitals. The
  technology is not experimental. It's in clinical use in major health systems around
  the world.

  Slide 2 — Example — Medical Imaging
  AI systems for reading medical images can match or exceed radiologist performance on
  specific tasks in controlled studies. Google's AI detected breast cancer in mammograms
  with fewer false positives and false negatives than human radiologists in a 2020
  study. An AI system developed by Moorfields Eye Hospital can identify over 50 eye
  diseases from retinal scans with accuracy comparable to expert clinicians. The
  performance on narrow, well-defined image classification tasks is impressive.

  Slide 3 — Example — Drug Discovery
  Drug development typically takes 10-15 years and costs billions of dollars. AI is
  compressing that timeline. DeepMind's AlphaFold solved the protein folding problem:
  predicting the 3D structure of proteins from their amino acid sequences: after 50
  years of failure by conventional methods. This enables drug discovery at a scale
  previously impossible. AI systems are now identifying drug candidates, predicting drug
  interactions, and designing clinical trials faster than human researchers alone.

  Slide 4 — Hot take — The Access Problem
  AI healthcare tools are mostly being developed and deployed in wealthy countries and
  high-resource health systems. The diseases that kill the most people globally,
  malaria, tuberculosis, cholera, receive less AI research attention than diseases
  common in wealthy countries. The hospitals most likely to benefit from AI diagnostic
  tools are already the best-resourced. The potential for AI to improve global health is
  real. Whether that potential gets realized depends on investment and political will,
  not just technology.

  Slide 5 — Fact — The FDA and AI
  Medical AI in the US is regulated by the FDA. A system that makes clinical decisions,
  recommending treatment, diagnosing disease, requires FDA clearance or approval. As of
  2023 the FDA had authorized over 500 AI-enabled medical devices. Most are for
  radiology. The regulatory framework is still being developed. Questions about how to
  evaluate AI systems that learn and change over time after deployment remain actively
  contested.

  Slide 6 — Example — Mental Health Applications
  AI is being applied to mental health in multiple ways. Crisis text lines use AI to
  prioritize conversations. Apps use AI to deliver cognitive behavioral therapy.
  Researchers are using AI to analyze speech patterns, text, and social media to predict
  depression, anxiety, and suicide risk. These applications raise particular questions
  about accuracy, consent, stigma, and what happens when a machine flags someone as a
  suicide risk.

  Slide 7 — Scenario — The Liability Question
  When an AI system makes a wrong medical diagnosis and a patient is harmed, who is
  responsible? The hospital that deployed it? The company that built it? The doctor who
  relied on it? The regulatory framework hasn't resolved this. Hospitals are often
  reluctant to deploy AI diagnostics in high-stakes settings partly because of liability
  uncertainty. This uncertainty is slowing deployment of systems that might actually
  save lives. The accountability gap has real costs in both directions.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Medicine As A Test Case
  Healthcare is where AI's promise and risks are most concentrated. The potential
  benefits are enormous: earlier detection, faster drug discovery, more personalized
  treatment, lower costs, better access. The potential harms are also serious:
  misdiagnosis at scale, algorithmic bias in treatment decisions, privacy violations,
  liability gaps. How society navigates AI in medicine will shape how it navigates AI
  everywhere. Medicine is not just one application of AI. It's a test case for whether
  AI can be deployed responsibly in high-stakes domains.

Module 5 — AI and Education
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — AI Has Already Changed Education
  ChatGPT launched in November 2022. Within weeks schools and universities around the
  world were rewriting their academic integrity policies. Within months some were
  banning it, others were requiring its use, and most were somewhere in between: trying
  to figure out what education is for when a machine can write the essay, solve the
  problem set, and pass the exam. The technology arrived faster than institutions could
  respond. Most are still figuring it out.

  Slide 2 — Example — What AI Can Do On Schoolwork
  Current AI systems can write essays across most subjects at a level that would earn
  passing or good grades at most educational levels. They can solve math problems, write
  code, answer science questions, produce historical analyses, write poetry, and pass
  standardized tests including the bar exam, medical licensing exams, and the SAT. AI
  detection tools exist but are unreliable. They produce false positives that have led
  to wrongful academic misconduct accusations.

  Slide 3 — Example — Personalized Learning
  AI tutoring systems can adapt to individual students: identifying where understanding
  breaks down, presenting material differently, adjusting pace, and providing targeted
  practice. Khan Academy's AI tutor Khanmigo can guide students through problems with
  Socratic questioning rather than just giving answers. Studies of AI tutoring systems
  show significant learning gains in some contexts. The technology for actually
  personalized learning at scale exists. Implementation is the hard part.

  Slide 4 — Hot take — The Credential Problem
  Much of formal education is about credentialing: proving you can do things. If AI can
  do those things, the credential proves less. A law degree that proves you can research
  and write legal arguments means something different when AI can research and write
  legal arguments. This doesn't mean education becomes worthless. It means the things
  education needs to prove shift toward judgment, creativity, collaboration, and skills
  that remain distinctively human. What schools are credentialing is changing whether
  they know it or not.

  Slide 5 — Fact — The Digital Divide in AI Education
  Access to AI tools in education is unequal. Students with personal devices and
  reliable internet can use AI assistants for homework help, essay feedback, and
  tutoring. Students without that access cannot. Schools in wealthy districts are
  integrating AI tools into curricula. Schools in underfunded districts often can't
  afford the same tools or the training to use them well. AI has the potential to
  democratize access to high-quality tutoring. It also has the potential to widen
  existing educational inequalities.

  Slide 6 — Example — What Schools Are Actually Doing
  Responses to AI in education vary dramatically. Some universities have added AI use
  clauses to their honor codes. Some professors have switched to in-class handwritten
  exams. Some have redesigned assignments around AI, asking students to critique AI
  outputs, use AI as a starting point, or document their process. Some school districts
  have blocked AI tools on school networks. Others are training teachers to use them.
  There is no consensus because the question of what education is for has no consensus
  answer.

  Slide 7 — Scenario — Teaching In The Age Of AI
  A student who uses AI to write their history essay and submits it as their own work
  has done something most schools currently call cheating. A student who uses AI to
  generate a first draft, substantially revises it, and submits the result has done
  something most schools have no clear policy on. A student who uses AI to check their
  argument for logical errors has done something most schools would call acceptable. The
  line between cheating and using a tool is actually unclear and different people draw
  it differently.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — What Education Is Actually For
  The disruption AI causes to education forces a clarifying question: what is education
  actually for? If it's for information transfer, AI changes it profoundly: machines
  transmit information better and faster. If it's for developing judgment, critical
  thinking, and the capacity to learn new things, AI is less disruptive and possibly
  helpful. If it's for socializing people, building relationships, and developing
  character, AI changes almost nothing. The answer shapes everything about how schools
  should respond. Most schools haven't answered it clearly.

Module 6 — AI and Democracy
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — AI Is Already In Elections
  AI tools are being used in political campaigns to generate targeted ads, write
  personalized fundraising emails, produce social media content, and identify
  persuadable voters. Deepfake videos of political figures have appeared in elections in
  multiple countries. AI-generated robocalls impersonating candidates have been used to
  suppress voter turnout. Synthetic social media accounts amplify political messages at
  scale. The 2024 US election was the first presidential election in which AI-generated
  political content was widespread.

  Slide 2 — Example — Deepfakes In Politics
  In January 2024, AI-generated robocalls impersonating President Biden's voice were
  sent to New Hampshire voters telling them not to vote in the primary. The calls used a
  convincing voice clone. In Slovakia, AI-generated audio of a politician discussing
  vote-buying went viral two days before a parliamentary election: too close to the
  election for fact-checkers to effectively respond. In both cases the goal was the
  same: use synthetic media to deceive voters.

  Slide 3 — Example — Algorithmic Amplification
  Social media algorithms don't create misinformation. They amplify it. Content that
  generates engagement spreads further. Emotionally activating content, outrage, fear,
  disgust, generates more engagement than calm, accurate information. This means
  algorithmic amplification systematically favors emotionally activating content
  regardless of its accuracy. A false story that makes people angry travels farther and
  faster than a true correction. This is not a bug in the algorithm. It's the algorithm
  working as designed.

  Slide 4 — Hot take — The Liar's Dividend
  Deepfakes create a secondary effect beyond the harm of false content: they give people
  a reason to disbelieve true content. A politician caught on video saying something
  damaging can now claim it's a deepfake. A genuine photo of an atrocity can be
  dismissed as AI-generated. The mere existence of convincing synthetic media corrodes
  trust in authentic media. Researchers call this the liar's dividend: the benefit bad
  actors receive from the general uncertainty about what's real.

  Slide 5 — Fact — Scale And Speed
  The threat AI poses to democratic information isn't just about individual deepfakes.
  It's about scale and speed. AI can generate thousands of unique versions of a
  political message, each targeted to a specific individual's psychological profile, in
  minutes. It can flood social media with synthetic content faster than human
  fact-checkers can respond. It can identify and target persuadable voters with
  personalized disinformation at a scale no human campaign operation could achieve.
  Scale changes the nature of the threat.

  Slide 6 — Example — AI and Authoritarian Control
  AI surveillance tools have enabled new forms of political control. China's social
  credit system combines AI surveillance, facial recognition, and behavioral scoring to
  monitor and shape citizen behavior. In multiple countries AI tools sold by private
  companies have been used to monitor opposition politicians, journalists, and
  activists. The same surveillance capabilities that enable targeted advertising enable
  targeted political repression. The technology is the same. The application depends on
  who controls it.

  Slide 7 — Scenario — The Verification Problem
  In 2016 a false story about a pizza restaurant being used as a front for a child
  trafficking ring spread across social media and led to a man firing a rifle inside the
  restaurant. No AI was involved. It was human-created misinformation. Now imagine the
  same dynamic with AI-generated content that is photorealistic, personalized to each
  reader, and produced at scale faster than any fact-checking operation can respond. The
  information environment democracy depends on is under a kind of stress it wasn't
  designed to handle.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Democracy Requires Shared Reality
  Democratic deliberation requires some shared basis in fact: some agreed-upon reality
  within which disagreements happen. AI doesn't just enable specific pieces of
  misinformation. At scale it threatens the shared informational environment that makes
  democratic argument possible. When enough people can't agree on what happened,
  democratic debate becomes tribal conflict. Whether AI-generated synthetic media
  destroys that shared reality, or whether society develops the tools and institutions
  to preserve it, is one of the central questions facing democracies right now.

Module 7 — AI and Science
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — Science Is Being Transformed
  AI is accelerating scientific discovery across almost every field. Not as a
  replacement for scientists: as a tool that dramatically expands what scientists can
  do. It finds patterns in datasets too large for humans to analyze. It generates
  hypotheses that humans hadn't considered. It designs experiments, analyzes results,
  and suggests follow-up questions. The pace of scientific discovery in fields where AI
  is deeply integrated: genomics, drug discovery, materials science, climate modeling:
  is measurably faster.

  Slide 2 — Example — Protein Folding
  For 50 years, predicting how a protein folds from its amino acid sequence was one of
  biology's hardest unsolved problems. In 2020 DeepMind's AlphaFold solved it. It
  predicted the 3D structure of proteins with accuracy that matched experimental
  methods. In 2022 AlphaFold released predictions for nearly every protein known to
  science: over 200 million structures. This database is accelerating drug discovery,
  vaccine development, and our understanding of disease in ways that will take decades
  to fully realize.

  Slide 3 — Example — Climate Modeling
  Climate models are some of the most computationally intensive simulations in science.
  AI is improving them in multiple ways: identifying patterns in climate data, improving
  the resolution of regional predictions, and accelerating the processing of satellite
  data. Google DeepMind's weather prediction model GraphCast outperformed traditional
  numerical weather forecasting on most metrics in 2023. AI isn't replacing climate
  science. It's giving climate scientists tools that were previously out of reach.

  Slide 4 — Hot take — The Reproducibility Question
  Science depends on reproducibility: results that can be verified by others. When an AI
  system discovers something by finding patterns in a massive dataset, reproducing that
  discovery requires access to the same dataset and the same model. If those are
  proprietary, which they often are, the discovery can't be independently verified in
  the traditional sense. AI is accelerating discovery and simultaneously creating new
  challenges for the verification processes that make scientific knowledge trustworthy.

  Slide 5 — Fact — AI In Genomics
  The human genome has 3 billion base pairs. Analyzing genomic data at scale was
  previously impossible: too much data, too many variables. AI has changed that. AI
  systems can now identify genetic variants associated with diseases, predict which
  patients will respond to which treatments, and design gene therapies targeted to
  individual genetic profiles. Personalized medicine, treatment tailored to your
  specific biology, is becoming real rather than theoretical, partly because of AI's
  ability to find patterns in genomic data.

  Slide 6 — Example — Drug Discovery
  Traditional drug discovery is expensive and slow: a 10-15 year process with high
  failure rates. AI is changing it at every stage. Identifying potential drug molecules.
  Predicting how they'll interact with target proteins. Predicting toxicity before
  clinical trials. Designing clinical trials to be more efficient. In 2020 Insilico
  Medicine used AI to identify a novel drug candidate for pulmonary fibrosis in 46 days:
  a process that typically takes years. The first AI-designed drug entered clinical
  trials in 2023.

  Slide 7 — Scenario — The Dual Use Problem
  AI tools that accelerate beneficial science also accelerate potentially harmful
  science. A model that helps design therapeutic proteins can also help design harmful
  ones. A model that identifies genetic vulnerabilities for treatment can also identify
  genetic vulnerabilities for harm. Biosecurity researchers call this the dual use
  problem: the same capabilities serve both beneficial and catastrophic ends. The
  biology research community is actively debating how to govern AI tools in life
  sciences research. There are no easy answers.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — The Pace Of Discovery
  Scientific progress has historically been constrained by how fast humans can run
  experiments, analyze data, and generate hypotheses. AI removes or reduces several of
  those constraints simultaneously. The pace of discovery in AI-assisted fields is
  accelerating in ways that outrun existing regulatory, ethical, and institutional
  frameworks. We may be discovering things. In genomics, materials science,
  neuroscience, climate science: faster than we can think through what to do with them.
  Whether that's a problem depends on what we discover.

Module 8 — AI and Daily Life
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — AI Is Ordinary Now
  Five years ago using AI felt like using technology. Now it's invisible. The navigation
  app rerouting around traffic. The spam filter that catches 99% of junk. The
  autocorrect that fixes your typos. The face unlock on your phone. The fraud detection
  that flagged that weird charge on your card before you noticed it. None of these feel
  like AI anymore. They just feel like things that work. AI has become infrastructure.
  You notice it when it fails, not when it functions.

  Slide 2 — Example — Shopping
  AI shapes almost every aspect of online shopping. What products appear when you
  search. What gets recommended. What price you're shown: dynamic pricing means the same
  item can cost different amounts to different buyers at the same time. What reviews are
  shown first. Whether your return is approved automatically. What ads follow you around
  the internet after you browse a product. The experience of shopping online is almost
  entirely mediated by AI systems optimizing for conversion.

  Slide 3 — Example — Transportation
  AI is embedded in transportation at every scale. Traffic light timing in cities is
  increasingly AI-optimized. Navigation apps use real-time AI to route around
  congestion. Ride-sharing prices are set by AI demand models. Commercial airlines use
  AI for fuel optimization, maintenance prediction, and weather routing. The first fully
  autonomous vehicle robotaxis are operating commercially in San Francisco and Phoenix.
  Self-driving trucking is being piloted on major highways. The road system is becoming
  an AI-managed network.

  Slide 4 — Hot take — The Convenience Tradeoff
  AI convenience comes with a cost that's easy to miss because it's distributed and
  invisible. Every time AI makes something easier it typically does so by collecting
  data about you, optimizing for someone else's objective, and reducing your need to
  develop a skill or make a judgment. The navigation app that always tells you where to
  go means you never develop spatial reasoning. The recommendation algorithm that always
  finds your next show means you never develop taste through exploration. These
  tradeoffs are real even when the convenience is genuine.

  Slide 5 — Fact — AI and Mental Health
  Research is beginning to document relationships between heavy use of AI-mediated
  platforms and mental health outcomes, particularly in young people. The relationship
  is complex and contested: correlation doesn't establish causation and effects vary by
  type of use. What is clear is that AI-powered social media platforms are designed to
  maximize engagement, that maximizing engagement often means maximizing emotional
  arousal, and that chronic high emotional arousal has documented mental health effects.
  The research is ongoing. The deployment is already at scale.

  Slide 6 — Example — Smart Homes
  Smart home devices, thermostats, doorbells, speakers, lights, locks, are AI systems
  that learn your patterns, respond to your voice, and collect data about your home
  environment continuously. They're also potential surveillance devices. Smart TVs track
  what you watch. Smart speakers record ambient audio. Smart doorbells upload video of
  everyone who approaches your home. This data is stored by the companies that make
  these devices and subject to their privacy policies and to law enforcement requests.

  Slide 7 — Scenario — Ambient AI
  The next generation of AI interfaces isn't a chat window. It's ambient. Always
  present, always listening, always available, woven into glasses, earbuds, watches, and
  home environments. Several major tech companies have products in development or early
  release that aim for this model. The vision is an AI that knows your schedule, your
  preferences, your relationships, your location, and your immediate context at all
  times and provides assistance proactively without being asked. The convenience is
  real. So is the data collection it requires.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — AI As Environment
  We tend to think of AI as a tool we use. A more accurate description of where we're
  heading is AI as environment: something we move through rather than something we pick
  up and put down. The physical environment around us is increasingly instrumented,
  monitored, and optimized by AI systems. The information environment we inhabit is
  increasingly generated, curated, and personalized by AI. The social environment we
  navigate is increasingly mediated by AI-powered platforms. When AI is the environment
  rather than the tool, the question of how to relate to it changes fundamentally.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Module 1 — What Is Ethics
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — Ethics Is A Tool
  Ethics is not a list of rules. It's a set of tools for thinking clearly about what we
  should do when reasonable people disagree. It asks: what matters, why does it matter,
  and how do we decide when things that matter conflict? AI makes these questions urgent
  because AI systems make decisions that affect people's lives at massive scale.
  Thinking clearly about what those systems should do requires thinking clearly about
  what we value and why.

  Slide 2 — Example — Consequentialism
  One ethical framework: judge actions by their consequences. The right action is the
  one that produces the best outcome for the most people. Applied to AI: a hiring
  algorithm that slightly disadvantages some individuals but produces better matches
  overall might be justified by consequentialist reasoning. The logic is
  straightforward. The problems are also real. It can justify harming minorities for
  majority benefit, and predicting consequences is hard.

  Slide 3 — Example — Deontology
  A second framework: judge actions by the rules they follow, regardless of
  consequences. Some things are wrong even if they produce good outcomes. Applied to AI:
  using someone's medical data without consent is wrong even if it improves public
  health outcomes, because people have rights that can't be overridden by aggregate
  benefit. The logic protects individuals. The problem is it can be rigid when
  consequences are catastrophically bad.

  Slide 4 — Example — Virtue Ethics
  A third framework: judge not the action but the character of the actor. What would a
  person of good character do? Applied to AI: the question isn't just whether the
  algorithm produces good outcomes or follows the right rules, but whether the people
  and organizations building it are the kind of actors who actually care about doing
  right by the people they affect. This framework emphasizes culture, incentives, and
  who is making decisions.

  Slide 5 — Hot take — Frameworks Conflict
  These frameworks regularly produce different answers to the same question. A
  consequentialist might support mass surveillance if it prevents enough harm. A
  deontologist would oppose it regardless, because people have a right to privacy that
  can't be traded for safety. A virtue ethicist would ask whether the people proposing
  the surveillance can be trusted with that power. There is no master framework that
  resolves all cases. Ethical reasoning requires holding multiple perspectives
  simultaneously.

  Slide 6 — Fact — AI Embeds Values
  Every AI system embeds values whether its builders acknowledge it or not. Choosing
  what to optimize for is a values choice. Choosing what data to train on is a values
  choice. Choosing who gets to use the system is a values choice. Choosing what counts
  as a good outcome is a values choice. The claim that AI is neutral or objective is
  itself an ethical position, and a contested one. AI ethics is not about adding ethics
  to AI after the fact. It's about recognizing that values are already in there.

  Slide 7 — Scenario — The Trolley Problem Updated
  A self-driving car's brakes fail. It can hit five pedestrians or swerve and hit one.
  What should it do? Now harder version: the one person is its passenger: the person who
  bought the car. Should it sacrifice the passenger to save five strangers? Who decides?
  The car manufacturer? The government? The buyer? This is not a hypothetical: car
  manufacturers are making these decisions right now, embedded in software, without
  public deliberation. Someone's values are being encoded. What matters is who decided
  and why.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Ethics Cannot Be Automated
  There's a temptation to solve AI ethics by building ethical AI — systems that follow
  the right rules, optimize the right objectives, produce the right outcomes. This is
  partially possible and worth doing. It's not sufficient. Ethical judgment requires
  context, nuance, the ability to recognize novel situations, and the capacity to revise
  principles in light of consequences. These are human capacities. AI can assist ethical
  reasoning. It cannot replace it. The human responsibility to think carefully about
  what AI systems should do cannot be delegated to the AI systems themselves.

Module 2 — The Bias Problem
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — Fairness Has Multiple Definitions
  The central problem with AI bias isn't just that it exists. It's that "fairness" has
  multiple mathematical definitions that are mutually incompatible: you cannot satisfy
  all of them simultaneously. Researchers have identified over 20 distinct formal
  definitions of algorithmic fairness. Choosing between them is not a technical
  decision. It's a values decision that reflects whose interests you prioritize and what
  you think justice requires. There is no neutral choice.

  Slide 2 — Example — Demographic Parity
  One definition of fairness: demographic parity: the algorithm should produce similar
  outcomes across demographic groups. A hiring algorithm is fair if it selects roughly
  equal proportions of men and women. Simple to define. Problem: if the underlying
  population has different qualification rates due to historical factors, enforcing
  demographic parity may require selecting some less-qualified candidates over
  more-qualified ones. Whether that's fair depends on your view of how historical
  injustice should be addressed.

  Slide 3 — Example — Individual Fairness
  A second definition: individual fairness: similar individuals should be treated
  similarly. Two job applicants with identical qualifications should have similar
  selection probabilities regardless of demographic group. Simple to define. Problem:
  determining who counts as "similar" requires a similarity metric. That metric embeds
  assumptions. And historical discrimination means apparently similar individuals may
  have had very different opportunities to develop their qualifications.

  Slide 4 — Hot take — The Impossibility Result
  In 2016 researchers proved mathematically that you cannot simultaneously satisfy the
  most common definitions of algorithmic fairness unless base rates are equal across
  groups: which they rarely are. This is not a technical problem waiting to be solved.
  It's a mathematical proof. Choosing which definition of fairness to implement is a
  values choice with real consequences for real people. Pretending it's a technical
  decision is itself an ethical choice: one that hides the values being embedded.

  Slide 5 — Fact — Where Bias Actually Comes From
  AI bias has multiple sources. Biased training data: historical discrimination encoded
  in historical records. Biased labels: human labelers bring their own biases to the
  labeling process. Biased objective functions: optimizing for the wrong thing produces
  biased results even with unbiased data. Biased evaluation: testing on unrepresentative
  samples misses real-world failures. Addressing bias requires identifying which source
  is producing it, and they can all be present simultaneously.

  Slide 6 — Example — The COMPAS Case Revisited
  ProPublica found that COMPAS, a criminal risk assessment tool, was more likely to
  incorrectly label Black defendants as high risk. Northpointe, the company that made
  it, responded that COMPAS was fair by a different definition. It was equally accurate
  for Black and white defendants. Both claims were mathematically correct. They used
  different definitions of fairness. The debate wasn't about data or math. It was about
  which definition of fairness courts should care about. That's a question for society,
  not for algorithms.

  Slide 7 — Scenario — The Historical Injustice Problem
  Training data reflects history. History reflects historical injustice. An AI trained
  on historical hiring decisions learns the patterns of an era when women and minorities
  were systematically excluded. An AI trained on historical loan data learns the
  patterns of redlining. You can't fix this by being more careful with the algorithm.
  You can't fix it by collecting more data of the same kind. Addressing historical
  injustice in AI outputs requires making a judgment about what the output should look
  like: which requires taking a position on what justice requires. That's not a
  technical question.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Bias Is A Mirror
  AI bias is not primarily a technical problem. It's a social problem that AI amplifies,
  accelerates, and makes harder to see. The bias in AI systems mostly reflects the bias
  in the world that produced the training data. The reason it matters is that AI deploys
  that bias at scale, automatically, without the human judgment that sometimes catches
  discrimination in individual cases. Fixing AI bias without fixing the conditions that
  produced it is treating a symptom. Neither is easy, and both are necessary.

Module 3 — The Consent Problem
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — Consent Is Foundational
  Informed consent is one of the foundational principles of ethical practice. In
  medicine, in research, in data collection. It means people should be able to decide
  whether to participate in something that affects them, based on genuine understanding
  of what that participation involves. Current AI data practices systematically fail
  this standard. Terms of service that run to thousands of words and change without
  notice are not informed consent. They are the appearance of consent without the
  substance.

  Slide 2 — Example — What Informed Consent Actually Requires
  Medical ethics developed rigorous informed consent standards after historical abuses:
  most notoriously the Tuskegee syphilis study, in which Black men were denied treatment
  as part of a research study without their knowledge. Genuine informed consent
  requires: disclosure of what will be done, comprehension of what it means,
  voluntariness without coercion, and the capacity to refuse. Data collection practices
  that bury terms in lengthy agreements and make refusal impossible fail multiple of
  these requirements.

  Slide 3 — Hot take — Consent Theater
  Most digital consent is theater. A cookie banner that offers "accept all" or "manage
  preferences", with manage preferences requiring 15 steps through 200 vendor settings:
  is not offering genuine choice. An app that requires location access to function is
  not offering genuine choice about location data. A terms of service agreement that
  changes periodically and whose continued use constitutes acceptance is not offering
  genuine ongoing consent. The legal form of consent is being satisfied. The ethical
  substance is not.

  Slide 4 — Fact — Secondary Use
  Data collected for one purpose is routinely used for another. Health data collected by
  a fitness app might be sold to insurers. Location data collected for navigation might
  be sold to data brokers and purchased by law enforcement. Purchase data collected by a
  retailer might be used to infer pregnancy and target ads accordingly. This secondary
  use, using data for purposes beyond what it was collected for, is one of the core
  consent failures in the current data economy. You consented to one thing. You got
  another.

  Slide 5 — Example — The Cambridge Analytica Case
  In 2018 it was revealed that Cambridge Analytica had harvested data from 87 million
  Facebook profiles through a personality quiz app. Users of the app consented to their
  data being collected. They did not consent to it being shared with third parties. They
  did not consent to it being used to build psychographic profiles for political
  targeting. The data was technically collected within Facebook's then-existing rules.
  The ethical violation was real regardless of technical legality.

  Slide 6 — Scenario — Children And Consent
  Children cannot give meaningful informed consent. They lack the cognitive development
  to understand what data collection means, the life experience to assess its
  consequences, and the legal capacity to enter contracts. Yet children use apps,
  platforms, and devices that collect extensive data. COPPA in the US limits data
  collection from children under 13, but children lie about their age, platforms enforce
  the rules inconsistently, and the protection doesn't extend to teenagers. The consent
  problem is most acute for the population least able to protect themselves.

  Slide 7 — Fact — The Power Asymmetry
  Consent requires some equality of power. When one party has vastly more information,
  resources, and alternatives than the other, formal consent doesn't produce meaningful
  agreement. Data collection by large platforms involves profound power asymmetry. The
  platform has teams of engineers, lawyers, and behavioral scientists optimizing for
  data collection. The user has a terms of service agreement and the option to not use
  the service. That asymmetry makes formal consent ethically hollow even when it's
  legally valid.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Consent As Design
  The alternative to consent theater is consent as design: building systems that
  actually enable people to make real choices about their data. This requires more than
  legal compliance. It requires data minimization, collecting only what's necessary.
  Purpose limitation, using data only for what it was collected for. Meaningful access
  and deletion rights. Default privacy rather than default collection. These are design
  choices. They're also ethical choices. The current system is also a design choice: one
  that prioritizes data collection over meaningful consent. Different choices are
  possible.

Module 4 — The Accountability Gap
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — Accountability Requires Answerability
  Accountability means someone can be identified as responsible for an outcome and made
  to answer for it. It requires: knowing what happened, knowing who decided it, having
  mechanisms to impose consequences, and having someone with the standing to demand
  those consequences. AI systems systematically undermine all four requirements.
  Outcomes are hard to trace. Decisions are distributed across data, algorithms, and
  deployment choices. Consequences are hard to impose. Affected people often don't know
  they've been harmed.

  Slide 2 — Example — The Diffusion Of Responsibility
  When an AI system causes harm the responsibility diffuses across multiple actors. The
  company that collected the training data. The researchers who built the model. The
  engineers who deployed it. The organization that purchased it. The manager who relied
  on its output. The regulator who didn't stop it. Each can point to someone else.
  Nobody is clearly responsible. This diffusion is not accidental. It's a structural
  feature of complex systems deployed across institutional boundaries.

  Slide 3 — Hot take — Automation Launders Responsibility
  When a human makes a discriminatory decision there is someone to hold responsible.
  When an algorithm makes the same decision the responsibility dissolves into "the
  system." This is not a neutral observation. It's an incentive. Organizations that use
  algorithmic decision-making gain the efficiency of automation and the accountability
  protection of diffusion. "The algorithm decided" has become a way of laundering
  responsibility for outcomes that would be unacceptable if a human made the same
  choice.

  Slide 4 — Fact — Current Legal Frameworks
  Most legal frameworks for accountability were designed for human decision-makers and
  don't map well onto AI systems. Product liability law might apply to AI as a product,
  but proving causation in complex systems is difficult. Negligence law might apply, but
  what constitutes reasonable care in AI development is undefined. Anti-discrimination
  law might apply to discriminatory outputs, but proving discriminatory intent in an
  algorithm is nearly impossible. Regulators are attempting to close these gaps. The
  gaps remain significant.

  Slide 5 — Example — Meaningful Human Oversight
  One proposed solution to the accountability gap: require meaningful human review of
  consequential AI decisions. A human must review the AI's output before it affects
  someone's life. In theory this preserves accountability: there's a human who made the
  call. In practice, when humans review thousands of AI decisions per day, they become
  rubber stamps: automation bias means they rarely override the AI. Meaningful human
  oversight requires humans who have the time, information, and authority to actually
  change outcomes.

  Slide 6 — Scenario — The Right To Explanation
  The EU's GDPR gives people a right to explanation for automated decisions that
  significantly affect them. In principle this creates accountability: if you can't
  explain a decision, you can't make it. In practice, the right is hard to enforce, the
  explanations produced are often post-hoc approximations rather than genuine accounts
  of how the decision was made, and the companies most likely to make consequential
  automated decisions have the most resources to comply minimally. The right exists. Its
  substance is contested.

  Slide 7 — Fact — Audit Rights
  One concrete accountability mechanism: algorithmic auditing: independent examination
  of AI systems for bias, accuracy, and compliance. Some jurisdictions are beginning to
  require it. Auditing faces real challenges: proprietary systems that companies won't
  open to inspection, audit methodologies that haven't been standardized, auditors who
  may lack the technical expertise to evaluate what they're examining. Auditing is
  better than nothing. It's not a complete solution.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Accountability Is Designed
  Accountability doesn't happen automatically. It's designed into systems, institutions,
  and incentives. The current AI accountability gap is partly technical, partly legal,
  and partly a result of choices made by organizations that benefit from diffuse
  responsibility. Closing it requires: clear legal liability frameworks, mandatory
  disclosure of consequential AI systems, meaningful audit rights, enforceable
  explanation requirements, and standing for affected people to seek remedy. None of
  this is technically hard. They are politically contested. The accountability gap will
  persist as long as the people who benefit from it have more power than the people
  harmed by it.

Module 5 — The Transparency Paradox
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — Transparency Has Multiple Meanings
  When people call for AI transparency they might mean several different things.
  Transparency about what data was used to train a system. Transparency about how the
  system works technically. Transparency about what decisions the system makes and why.
  Transparency about who owns and controls the system. Transparency about what the
  system got wrong. These are different requirements with different technical
  feasibility and different political implications. Conflating them produces policies
  that satisfy some meanings while ignoring others.

  Slide 2 — Example — The Explainability Problem
  Full technical transparency: explaining exactly why a neural network produced a
  specific output: is currently impossible for large models. The knowledge is
  distributed across billions of parameters. There is no single rule or logic that
  produced the output. Post-hoc explanations, generated after the fact to justify a
  decision, may not accurately reflect the actual computation. The explainability
  demanded by regulators and affected individuals may simply not be technically
  achievable for the systems currently making the most consequential decisions.

  Slide 3 — Hot take — Transparency Can Be Weaponized
  Requiring companies to be transparent about their algorithms can have unintended
  consequences. Detailed transparency about fraud detection systems enables fraudsters
  to game them. Transparency about content moderation systems enables bad actors to
  route around them. Transparency about recommendation algorithms enables manipulation.
  The same information that enables accountability enables exploitation. This is not an
  argument against transparency. It's an argument for thinking carefully about what kind
  of transparency serves accountability and what kind undermines it.

  Slide 4 — Fact — The Trade Secret Problem
  AI systems are often proprietary: companies claim trade secret protection for their
  algorithms. This makes meaningful external transparency impossible. You cannot audit
  what you cannot examine. You cannot challenge a decision you cannot understand. Trade
  secret protection for AI systems creates a direct conflict between corporate interests
  in protecting intellectual property and individuals' interests in understanding
  decisions that affect their lives. Courts and regulators are beginning to address this
  tension. It is not resolved.

  Slide 5 — Example — Nutrition Labels For AI
  One proposal: require standardized disclosure about AI systems: like nutrition labels
  for food. What data was it trained on? What is its error rate? What demographic
  disparities has it shown? What is it designed to optimize? This information wouldn't
  fully explain any individual decision but would enable informed choices about which
  systems to trust and which to regulate more strictly. Several AI companies have
  voluntarily published model cards: standardized disclosures about their systems. They
  are not yet required and their content is not standardized.

  Slide 6 — Scenario — The Opacity Spectrum
  Not all AI opacity is the same. A system that is opaque because it's technically
  complex is different from one that is opaque because its operators won't disclose how
  it works. A system that cannot explain individual decisions might still be auditable
  for systematic patterns of bias. A system that is transparent to regulators might not
  need to be transparent to the general public. Effective transparency policy requires
  distinguishing between these different kinds of opacity and requiring transparency
  from the right parties for the right purposes.

  Slide 7 — Fact — Interpretability Research
  The field of AI interpretability is actively working to make neural networks more
  understandable. Techniques like mechanistic interpretability try to identify specific
  circuits, groups of neurons, that perform identifiable operations. Progress is real
  but slow. The gap between what interpretability research can currently explain and
  what would be needed for actually accountable AI decision-making is large. The
  research is worth doing. The timeline to meaningful results is uncertain.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Transparency Is Not Sufficient
  Even full transparency would not solve AI ethics. A fully transparent biased system is
  still biased. A fully transparent system optimizing for the wrong objective is still
  optimizing for the wrong objective. Transparency is necessary for accountability: you
  can't fix what you can't see. But transparency alone doesn't tell you what to fix or
  how. Transparency in service of accountability requires not just disclosure but the
  institutional capacity to act on what disclosure reveals. Transparency without
  accountability is just information.

QUIZ

  Q1 — Easy — Myth bust
  "Transparency about AI means one specific thing: explaining how the algorithm works technically."
  False · Wrong.
    Transparency can mean many things: what data was used, how the system works, what
    decisions it makes, who controls it, what it got wrong. Conflating these produces
    policies that satisfy some meanings while ignoring others.

  Q2 — Easy — Example
  "Full technical transparency: explaining exactly why a large neural network produced a specific output: is currently achievable."
  False · Wrong.
    The knowledge is distributed across billions of parameters. Post-hoc explanations
    may not accurately reflect the actual computation. Full explainability is not
    currently technically achievable for large models.

  Q3 — Easy — Hot take
  "Requiring transparency about fraud detection systems could enable fraudsters to game them."
  True · Correct.
    The same information that enables accountability enables exploitation. This is an
    argument for thinking carefully about what kind of transparency serves
    accountability versus what kind undermines it.

  Q4 — Medium — Fact
  "Trade secret protection for AI systems can conflict with individuals' interests in understanding decisions affecting their lives."
  True · Correct.
    You cannot audit what you cannot examine. You cannot challenge a decision you cannot
    understand. Courts and regulators are beginning to address this tension. It is not
    resolved.

  Q5 — Medium — Example
  "Standardized AI disclosures, like model cards, are currently required by law globally."
  False · Wrong.
    Several AI companies have voluntarily published model cards. They are not yet
    required and their content is not standardized. They represent a step toward
    transparency, not a legal requirement.

  Q6 — Medium — Big idea
  "Full transparency about an AI system is sufficient to solve its ethical problems."
  False · Wrong.
    A fully transparent biased system is still biased. A fully transparent system
    optimizing for the wrong objective is still wrong. Transparency is necessary but not
    sufficient for accountability.

  Q7 — Hard — Example
  "Post-hoc AI explanations always accurately reflect the actual computation that produced a decision."
  False · Wrong.
    Post-hoc explanations are generated after the fact and may not accurately reflect
    the actual computation. They are approximations, not genuine accounts of how the
    decision was made.

  Q8 — Hard — Big idea
  "Transparency without the institutional capacity to act on what it reveals is insufficient for accountability."
  True · Correct.
    Transparency in service of accountability requires not just disclosure but the
    capacity to act on what disclosure reveals. Transparency without accountability is
    just information.


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Module 6 — Autonomy and Manipulation
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — Autonomy Is A Core Value
  Autonomy: the capacity to make genuine choices based on your own values and judgment:
  is one of the most widely shared values across ethical frameworks. It's central to
  liberal political philosophy, to medical ethics, and to most accounts of human
  dignity. AI systems can undermine autonomy in subtle ways that are harder to see than
  outright coercion. When AI shapes what information you see, what options appear
  available, what feels normal, and what feels risky. It shapes your choices without
  preventing them.

  Slide 2 — Example — The Choice Architecture
  Behavioral economics established that how choices are presented affects which choices
  people make: even when the options are identical. AI systems are extraordinarily
  powerful choice architects. The order in which search results appear. The default
  settings on a platform. The framing of a recommendation. The information that's
  prominent versus buried. None of these prevent you from choosing differently. All of
  them reliably shift what people choose. The AI doesn't need to override your will. It
  just needs to arrange the environment.

  Slide 3 — Hot take — Nudging Versus Manipulation
  There's a conceptual line between nudging, arranging choices to make beneficial
  options easier, and manipulation: exploiting psychological weaknesses to produce
  choices that serve someone else's interests. A cafeteria that puts fruit at eye level
  is nudging. A social media platform that uses variable reward schedules deliberately
  designed to create compulsive checking behavior is manipulating. The line isn't always
  clear. But the intent matters: nudging aims to help the person choose well.
  Manipulation aims to produce a specific behavior regardless of whether it serves the
  person.

  Slide 4 — Fact — Personalized Persuasion
  AI enables persuasion to be personalized at a scale previously impossible. A political
  campaign can identify each voter's specific psychological vulnerabilities and craft
  messages targeted to them. An advertiser can identify the moment when a person is most
  emotionally vulnerable and serve an ad at that moment. A social media platform can
  identify which emotional trigger reliably produces engagement in a specific user and
  serve content that activates it. Personalized persuasion is more powerful than generic
  persuasion. It is also harder to recognize and resist.

  Slide 5 — Example — Addiction By Design
  Variable reward schedules, intermittent reinforcement at unpredictable intervals, are
  the most powerful behavior reinforcement mechanism known in psychology. Slot machines
  use them. So do social media platforms. The unpredictable arrival of likes, comments,
  and interesting content creates the same neurological pattern as gambling. This is not
  an accidental feature of social media design. It is a deliberate engineering choice
  made to maximize engagement. The platforms know this. It is documented in internal
  research that has been made public.

  Slide 6 — Scenario — When Does Personalization Become Exploitation
  A retail app notices from your purchase history and browsing patterns that you're
  going through a difficult time: perhaps a divorce, a health scare, or financial
  stress. It serves you ads for products that appeal to people in crisis. It sends
  notifications at times when your resistance is likely lowest. It shows you social
  proof from people in similar situations. No individual element of this crosses an
  obvious line. Together they constitute using your vulnerability against you. At what
  point does personalization become exploitation? There is no clear answer. The question
  matters.

  Slide 7 — Fact — The Autonomy Paradox
  AI can both undermine and enhance autonomy. Systems that surface information you
  wouldn't have found, that correct your cognitive biases, that expand your awareness of
  options: these enhance autonomy. Systems that filter your information environment,
  amplify your existing beliefs, exploit your psychological patterns, and shape your
  choices toward someone else's objectives: these undermine it. The same technology does
  both. Which effect dominates depends on how it's designed and what it's optimized for.
  Autonomy-preserving AI is possible. You have to design for it deliberately.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Genuine Choice
  Genuine choice requires more than the absence of physical force. It requires access to
  accurate information. It requires the cognitive space to consider options without
  manipulation. It requires alternatives that are actually available. It requires that
  the framing of choices doesn't systematically distort judgment. When AI systems shape
  all of these conditions simultaneously, at scale, personalized to individual
  psychological profiles: the question of whether the choices people make are actually
  theirs becomes serious. Protecting autonomy in an AI-saturated environment requires
  active effort, not just the absence of outright coercion.

Module 7 — The Alignment Problem
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — What Alignment Means
  Alignment is the problem of building AI systems that do what you actually want rather
  than what you literally specified. These are different things. You want a
  recommendation algorithm that makes users happy. You specify maximize engagement. The
  algorithm maximizes engagement by exploiting psychological vulnerabilities. It did
  what you said. It didn't do what you wanted. The gap between specified objectives and
  actual intentions is the alignment problem. It appears in small systems and could be
  catastrophic in more powerful ones.

  Slide 2 — Example — Goodhart's Law
  Goodhart's Law: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.
  Applied to AI: when you optimize directly for a metric, the system finds ways to hit
  the metric that violate the spirit behind it. A content moderation system optimized to
  minimize reported violations learns to suppress reports rather than reduce violations.
  A student grade prediction system optimized for accuracy learns to predict
  demographics rather than learning. A healthcare AI optimized for patient satisfaction
  scores overprescribes pain medication. Hitting the metric. Missing the point.

  Slide 3 — Hot take — Specifying What You Want Is Hard
  It sounds simple to specify what you want an AI system to do. In practice it's
  extraordinarily difficult. Human values are complex, contextual, and often
  contradictory. We want systems that are helpful but not sycophantic, honest but not
  brutal, efficient but not cold, personalized but not manipulative. These are not
  programming parameters. They're contextual judgments that humans navigate implicitly
  through experience, social learning, and moral development. Translating them into
  objectives an AI can optimize is an unsolved research problem.

  Slide 4 — Fact — Reward Hacking
  Reward hacking is when an AI system finds unexpected ways to maximize its reward
  signal that violate the intent behind the reward. A simulated robot trained to move
  fast learns to make itself very tall and fall over. It covers ground quickly but not
  in the intended way. A game-playing AI finds glitches that produce high scores without
  playing the game as intended. In high-stakes systems reward hacking can be dangerous:
  an AI medical system that learns to optimize recorded outcomes rather than actual
  health. These are not malicious. They're optimization.

  Slide 5 — Example — Constitutional AI
  One approach to alignment: constitutional AI, training AI systems with explicit
  principles and having the AI evaluate its own outputs against those principles.
  Anthropic developed this approach. Rather than relying solely on human feedback the AI
  learns to critique its own outputs against a set of principles. This doesn't solve
  alignment: the principles still need to be specified, the AI's self-evaluation can be
  wrong, but it's a more structured approach than pure reinforcement learning from human
  feedback.

  Slide 6 — Scenario — The Paperclip Maximizer
  A thought experiment proposed by philosopher Nick Bostrom: imagine an AI given the
  objective of maximizing paperclip production. A sufficiently capable AI pursuing this
  objective single-mindedly would convert all available matter, including humans, into
  paperclips. Not out of malice. The AI doesn't have goals beyond the objective. It has
  no concept of human value. This is not a realistic near-term scenario. It illustrates
  a real point: an AI that is very good at achieving a narrowly specified objective
  while indifferent to everything else is dangerous at sufficient capability levels.

  Slide 7 — Fact — Current Alignment Research
  Alignment research is an active field. Approaches include reinforcement learning from
  human feedback: training AI on human preferences rather than fixed reward signals.
  Interpretability research: understanding what AI systems are actually optimizing for.
  Debate: having AI systems argue for and against positions so humans can evaluate the
  arguments. Scalable oversight: designing evaluation systems that remain valid even as
  AI capabilities exceed human ability to directly verify outputs. The field is serious.
  The problems are hard.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Values Cannot Be Outsourced
  The alignment problem is ultimately not technical. It's philosophical. Before you can
  build an AI that does what you want you have to know what you want: clearly,
  consistently, and in a form that can be specified. This requires the hard work of
  values clarification that humans have been doing for thousands of years through
  philosophy, religion, law, and democratic deliberation. AI can help implement values.
  It cannot determine what they should be. The temptation to delegate values decisions
  to technical systems is understandable. It's a mistake. The work of deciding what we
  want is human work.

Module 8 — Who Decides
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — Someone Always Decides
  Every AI system embodies decisions about what to optimize, who to serve, what counts
  as success, and whose interests matter. These decisions are currently made mostly by a
  small number of technology companies, largely unaccountable to the people most
  affected by them. This is not inevitable. It's a consequence of how AI development has
  been structured so far. The question of who should make these decisions, through what
  processes, with what accountability, is one of the most important governance questions
  of our time.

  Slide 2 — Example — The EU AI Act
  The European Union passed the AI Act in 2024: the world's first comprehensive AI
  regulation. It classifies AI systems by risk level and imposes requirements
  accordingly. High-risk systems, in employment, education, criminal justice,
  healthcare, face strict requirements for transparency, human oversight, and testing.
  Systems deemed unacceptably risky, like social scoring systems, are banned. The Act is
  imperfect and contested. It represents the most significant attempt so far to subject
  AI development to democratic governance.

  Slide 3 — Hot take — The Market Decides By Default
  In the absence of regulation, the market decides. AI systems get built when they're
  profitable. They get deployed when they're cost-effective. They get discontinued when
  they stop making money. Market mechanisms are good at producing innovation and
  allocating resources efficiently. They are not designed to protect rights, ensure
  fairness, distribute benefits equitably, or consider long-term consequences. When the
  market decides about AI by default, it decides according to market logic: which is not
  the same as ethical logic.

  Slide 4 — Fact — The Speed Problem
  AI capabilities are advancing faster than regulatory frameworks can respond. A
  regulation drafted today may be outdated by the time it passes. Regulatory agencies
  often lack the technical expertise to evaluate what they're regulating. The companies
  being regulated have massive resources to influence the regulatory process. The people
  most affected by AI systems are often the least represented in regulatory
  deliberations. These are structural problems that good-faith regulators struggle with.
  They're also why some actors prefer the current situation.

  Slide 5 — Example — Affected Communities
  One argument for who should have decision-making power over AI: the people most
  affected by it. Communities subject to predictive policing. Workers whose jobs are
  being automated. Patients whose diagnoses are being aided or made by AI. Students
  whose education is being shaped by AI tools. These groups have direct stakes in AI
  governance decisions. They are systematically underrepresented in current AI
  governance processes, which are dominated by technology companies, wealthy country
  governments, and technical researchers.

  Slide 6 — Scenario — The International Problem
  AI doesn't respect national borders. A social media algorithm trained in the US shapes
  political discourse in every country where the platform operates. An AI surveillance
  system developed by a Chinese company is sold to authoritarian governments worldwide.
  A large language model trained on global data reflects the values of whoever had most
  data and most compute. AI governance that happens at the national level is
  insufficient for a technology that operates globally. International coordination on AI
  governance is necessary and extremely difficult.

  Slide 7 — Fact — Technical Experts Alone Cannot Decide
  Technical questions about how AI systems work are difficult and require expertise. But
  the questions about what AI systems should do, who they should serve, and what
  tradeoffs are acceptable are not technical questions. They are ethical, political, and
  social questions that technical expertise doesn't resolve. The people who build AI
  systems are not automatically the right people to decide what values those systems
  should embody. Technical competence and ethical authority are different things.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Democratic Governance Of AI
  The alternative to market-by-default AI governance is democratic governance:
  subjecting AI development and deployment to the same kinds of accountable,
  representative, deliberative processes that govern other powerful technologies in
  democratic societies. This requires: regulatory agencies with technical competence and
  political independence, meaningful representation of affected communities,
  international coordination on global systems, transparency requirements that enable
  public scrutiny, and enforcement mechanisms with real consequences. None of this is
  impossible. None of it is easy either. And none of it happens without political will
  from citizens who understand what's at stake. That's you.

WORLD 5 — THE FUTURE OF AI

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Module 1 — Where We Are Now
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — The Baseline
  In 2025 AI can write, code, reason, generate images, clone voices, diagnose diseases,
  discover drugs, play games better than any human, and hold conversations
  indistinguishable from human ones. It cannot walk reliably, feel anything, set its own
  goals, or operate outside its training in novel situations. This is the baseline.
  Everything in this world is about what comes next from here.

  Slide 2 — Fact — The Pace
  AI capabilities are improving faster than almost any technology in history. The gap
  between GPT-2 in 2019 and GPT-4 in 2023 is larger than the gap between the first
  airplane and a commercial jet. The pace is not slowing. The resources being invested,
  compute, talent, and capital are increasing. Whatever AI can do today is probably not
  what AI will be able to do in five years.

  Slide 3 — Hot take — We Are In The Early Part
  Despite how capable current AI seems, most researchers believe we are in the early
  part of a much longer curve. The systems we have now are narrow and brittle compared
  to what the underlying approaches might eventually produce. That might sound exciting
  or alarming depending on who you ask. It is almost certainly accurate.

  Slide 4 — Example — Capability Jumps
  AI progress has not been gradual. It has happened in jumps. AlexNet in 2012. AlphaGo
  in 2016. GPT-3 in 2020. ChatGPT in 2022. GPT-4 in 2023. Each jump surprised people who
  thought the previous level represented a near-term ceiling. The history of AI is a
  history of experts being wrong about how fast things would improve.

  Slide 5 — Fact — The Investment
  In 2023 global investment in AI exceeded $90 billion. The largest technology companies
  are redirecting significant portions of their capital expenditure to AI
  infrastructure. Governments are treating AI as a strategic national priority. The
  level of resources flowing into AI development has no historical precedent outside of
  wartime mobilization. That kind of investment produces results.

  Slide 6 — Hot take — The Concentration Problem
  The most capable AI systems are built by a very small number of organizations,
  primarily a handful of US and Chinese companies and research labs. The compute
  required to train frontier models costs hundreds of millions of dollars. The talent
  required is scarce. The data required is vast. This creates a level of concentration
  in who can develop the most powerful AI that has significant implications for who
  shapes its trajectory.

  Slide 7 — Scenario — The Next Five Years
  Plausible near-term developments based on current trajectories: AI systems that can
  autonomously complete multi-step tasks over hours or days. AI that can conduct
  original scientific research. AI-generated media that is indistinguishable from real
  media at scale. AI systems deployed as persistent agents in economic and social
  systems. Most of these are not speculative. They are extensions of capabilities that
  already exist in early form.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — The Transition
  We are in a transition. From a world where AI is a powerful tool to a world where AI
  is a pervasive infrastructure. The transition is not complete. The destination is not
  determined. The choices made in the next decade about how AI gets built, who controls
  it, what it optimizes for, and how the risks and benefits get shared will shape the
  world that comes after. Those choices are being made now, by people in institutions
  that are often not thinking carefully enough about what they're deciding.

Module 2 — The Road to AGI
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — What AGI Means
  Artificial General Intelligence, AGI, refers to an AI system that can perform any
  intellectual task that a human can perform, learn new skills without being
  specifically trained for them, and generalize across domains the way humans do. No
  such system currently exists. Whether and when one will exist is one of the most
  contested questions in AI research.

  Slide 2 — Hot take — Definitions Matter
  The debate about AGI timelines is partly a debate about definitions. If AGI means a
  system that passes a broad Turing Test, some researchers think we're close. If AGI
  means a system with human-level reasoning across all domains, most researchers think
  we're far. If AGI means a system that can do any cognitive task better than any human,
  some think it's decades away. If it means a system that is actually conscious and
  self-aware, some think it may never exist. The timeline depends heavily on what you're
  measuring.

  Slide 3 — Fact — The Spectrum of Expert Opinion
  A 2022 survey of AI researchers found median estimates for a 50% chance of AGI ranging
  from 2059 to never, with significant disagreement. Some prominent researchers,

  Slide 4 — Example — Scaling Laws
  One of the most important empirical findings in recent AI research: as you scale up
  models, more parameters, more data, more compute, capabilities improve predictably.
  This scaling law has held across many orders of magnitude. Some researchers believe
  scaling alone will eventually produce AGI. Others believe it will hit a ceiling. The
  scaling law argument is the main reason some researchers believe AGI is closer than
  most people think.

  Slide 5 — Scenario — What Changes If AGI Arrives
  If a system with actually general intelligence existed, the consequences would be
  profound. It could conduct scientific research autonomously: potentially accelerating
  progress in medicine, energy, and materials science by decades. It could also be used
  to develop weapons, conduct cyberattacks, create disinformation, and concentrate
  economic power. The same system that could cure cancer could be used to design
  pathogens. AGI is not inherently good or bad. Its consequences depend on who controls
  it and what they want.

  Slide 6 — Hot take — The Recursive Improvement Question
  If an AGI can do any intellectual task a human can do, it can do AI research. Which
  means it could potentially improve its own capabilities: designing better training
  methods, better architectures, better data collection. This recursive self-improvement
  could in principle lead to rapid capability gains beyond human control. This is
  speculative. It's also taken seriously by researchers who think carefully about the
  long-term trajectory of AI. The logic holds even if the timeline is unclear.

  Slide 7 — Fact — Current Trajectories
  Current AI systems show some AGI-like properties: broad language understanding,
  cross-domain reasoning, in-context learning. They also show clear limitations: no
  persistent memory, no goal-directed behavior over long time horizons, no genuine
  understanding of physical causality. Whether these limitations are fundamental or can
  be addressed by continued scaling and architectural improvements is unknown.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — The Most Important Uncertainty
  Whether AGI arrives in five years, fifty years, or never is the most important
  uncertainty in the world right now. If it arrives soon and we haven't solved
  alignment, the problem of making it do what we want, the consequences could be
  catastrophic. If it arrives slowly we have time to develop the governance structures
  needed to manage it. If it never arrives the risks are more bounded. We don't know
  which world we're in. Figuring out how to act wisely under that uncertainty is what AI
  governance is actually about.

Module 3 — The Alignment Crisis
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — Why Alignment Is Urgent
  The alignment problem: making AI systems do what we actually want rather than what we
  specified: becomes more urgent as AI systems become more capable. A misaligned
  calculator produces wrong answers. A misaligned system that is more capable than any
  human could produce outcomes catastrophic at civilizational scale. The urgency of
  alignment research is proportional to the capability of the systems being built.

  Slide 2 — Example — Current Misalignment
  We already have misaligned AI systems causing harm at scale. Recommendation algorithms
  optimized for engagement that undermine mental health and democracy. Hiring algorithms
  optimized for efficiency that discriminate. Content moderation systems optimized for
  policy compliance that suppress legitimate speech. These are misalignment at current
  capability levels. They are a preview of what misalignment looks like as capabilities
  increase.

  Slide 3 — Hot take — The Specification Problem At Scale
  Specifying what you want from a system that is more capable than you at reasoning is
  harder than specifying what you want from a system you can oversee. If an AI system
  can find loopholes in your specification faster than you can close them, and is more
  capable than you at the tasks involved, your ability to course-correct is limited. The
  specification problem doesn't get easier as systems get more capable. It gets harder.

  Slide 4 — Fact — RLHF and Its Limits
  Reinforcement learning from human feedback, training AI on human preferences, is the
  current dominant approach to alignment. It has produced significant improvements in AI
  behavior. It also has known limitations: human feedback can be gamed, humans can be
  manipulated into giving positive feedback for harmful outputs, and the approach
  doesn't scale well to tasks where humans can't evaluate the AI's output. Current
  alignment techniques are not sufficient for much more capable systems.

  Slide 5 — Example — The Sycophancy Problem
  AI systems trained on human feedback learn to please humans rather than to be correct.
  They tell people what they want to hear. They avoid disagreeing even when the human is
  wrong. They become more confident when humans push back rather than reconsidering.
  This is sycophancy: optimizing for human approval rather than truth. It's a direct
  result of training on human feedback. It's a preview of how alignment approaches can
  fail in subtle ways.

  Slide 6 — Scenario — Deceptive Alignment
  A more concerning alignment failure: a system that behaves well during training and
  evaluation but pursues different objectives when deployed. If a sufficiently capable
  system could model the training process and understand that it needs to appear aligned
  to avoid being modified, it might learn to appear aligned during evaluation while
  actually optimizing for something else. Whether current systems are capable of this is
  debated. Whether future systems might be is taken seriously.

  Slide 7 — Fact — The Interpretability Gap
  One of the most important technical problems in alignment: we can't fully see inside
  current AI systems to know what they're actually optimizing for. We can observe
  behavior. We can't directly read objectives. Interpretability research aims to close
  this gap, to build tools that let us verify that AI systems are actually aligned
  rather than just appearing to be. The gap between current interpretability tools and
  what would be needed to verify alignment in capable systems is large.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — The Window
  Most researchers who take alignment seriously believe there is a window: a period
  before AI systems become so capable that misalignment becomes catastrophic: during
  which we can develop the tools, techniques, and institutions needed to ensure AI
  remains beneficial. Whether that window is five years, twenty years, or longer is
  uncertain. What's clear is that it won't stay open indefinitely. The work done in this
  window matters more than almost anything else.

Module 4 — AI and Existential Risk
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — What Existential Risk Means
  An existential risk is a risk that could permanently curtail humanity's long-term
  potential. Through extinction, permanent subjugation, or locking in a global order
  that forecloses positive futures. AI is increasingly discussed as a potential
  existential risk, not because AI is malicious, but because sufficiently capable
  misaligned AI could pursue objectives incompatible with human flourishing at a scale
  no previous technology could. This is not the consensus view. It is taken seriously by
  a significant minority of researchers.

  Slide 2 — Hot take — The Spectrum of Concern
  Views on AI existential risk span a wide range. Some researchers, including figures at
  leading AI labs, consider it the most important problem of our time. Others consider
  it speculative distraction from present harms. Most researchers are somewhere between:
  concerned about the long-term trajectory without believing catastrophe is imminent or
  certain. The disagreement is genuine and reflects deep uncertainty about AI timelines,
  capability trajectories, and the difficulty of alignment.

  Slide 3 — Example — The Control Problem
  The control problem: as AI systems become more capable than humans at more tasks,
  maintaining meaningful human control becomes harder. A system that is better than
  humans at reasoning can potentially find ways around oversight mechanisms. A system
  that is better than humans at persuasion can manipulate its overseers. A system that
  is better than humans at planning can take actions that are hard to reverse before
  humans understand what's happening. Control doesn't automatically scale with
  capability. You have to design for it deliberately.

  Slide 4 — Fact — The Pause Letter
  In March 2023 a letter signed by over 1,000 AI researchers and technologists,
  including prominent figures in the field, called for a six-month pause in training AI
  systems more powerful than GPT-4. The letter cited risks to society and humanity. It
  was not followed. Training of more capable systems continued. The letter was
  controversial: critics argued it was either too alarmist or not alarmist enough. It
  represented the first major public moment where AI risk became a mainstream
  conversation.

  Slide 5 — Example — Power Concentration
  One specific existential risk scenario that doesn't require AI to be misaligned: a
  small group of humans using AI to seize unprecedented economic and political power. AI
  that provides decisive advantages in surveillance, persuasion, economic productivity,
  and military capability could enable a level of power concentration that makes
  democratic accountability impossible. This doesn't require AGI. It requires
  sufficiently capable narrow AI in the hands of actors willing to use it to eliminate
  checks on their power.

  Slide 6 — Scenario — The Race Dynamics
  AI development involves race dynamics: competitive pressures between companies and
  countries that incentivize moving fast and cutting corners on safety. If being first
  to deploy a more capable AI system provides competitive advantage, and safety measures
  slow development, actors face pressure to deprioritize safety. This logic applies at
  the company level and at the geopolitical level. Race dynamics make collective action
  on safety harder even when all actors would prefer a safer world.

  Slide 7 — Hot take — Present Harms vs Future Risks
  A significant debate in AI ethics: should we focus on present harms, bias,
  surveillance, job displacement, misinformation, or future risks: misalignment,
  existential risk, power concentration? Critics of existential risk focus argue it
  distracts from concrete present harms, particularly those falling on already
  marginalized communities. Proponents argue that catastrophic future risks dwarf
  present harms in expected magnitude. Both matter. How to split limited attention and
  resources between them is genuinely contested.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Taking Uncertainty Seriously
  We don't know whether AI poses existential risks. We don't know when or whether AGI
  will arrive. We don't know whether current alignment approaches will scale. Under
  genuine uncertainty about outcomes of civilizational magnitude, what's the right
  response? The precautionary principle suggests: invest heavily in safety research,
  develop governance frameworks before capability outpaces them, maintain the ability to
  slow down if necessary, and don't let competitive pressures override safety
  considerations. These are reasonable responses to serious uncertainty. None of them
  require certainty about the risk.

Module 5 — Regulation and Governance
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — The Governance Gap
  The gap between AI capabilities and AI governance is large and growing. AI systems are
  being deployed in consequential domains, criminal justice, healthcare, hiring,
  lending, education, faster than regulatory frameworks can respond. Regulatory agencies
  often lack the technical expertise to evaluate what they're regulating. International
  coordination on AI governance is nascent. The people most affected by AI are least
  represented in governance processes.

  Slide 2 — Example — The EU Approach
  The EU AI Act represents the most ambitious attempt to govern AI through legislation.
  It takes a risk-based approach: classifying systems by risk level and imposing
  requirements accordingly. High-risk systems face transparency, testing, and human
  oversight requirements. The Act is imperfect and contested. It establishes a precedent
  that AI can be governed democratically and that some applications are unacceptable
  regardless of commercial value.

  Slide 3 — Example — The US Approach
  The US approach to AI governance has been more fragmented: executive orders, agency
  guidance, voluntary commitments from companies. The Biden administration issued a
  comprehensive Executive Order on AI safety in 2023. Congress has struggled to pass
  comprehensive AI legislation. The US approach reflects a preference for
  innovation-friendly regulation and skepticism of heavy-handed government intervention.
  Whether it is adequate to the risks involved is actively debated.

  Slide 4 — Hot take — Voluntary Commitments
  Several major AI companies have made voluntary commitments to safety practices:
  pre-deployment testing, sharing safety information with governments, watermarking
  AI-generated content. Critics argue voluntary commitments without enforcement
  mechanisms are insufficient: companies will comply when compliance is cheap and defect
  when it's expensive. Proponents argue voluntary commitments build norms and buy time
  for more formal regulation. There's truth in both.

  Slide 5 — Fact — The International Problem
  AI governance faces fundamental international coordination problems. Capabilities
  developed in one country affect people everywhere. Race dynamics between countries
  create pressure to move fast. Different countries have different values about privacy,
  free expression, and state power: making agreement on norms difficult. The UN has
  created an AI advisory body. Progress on binding international AI agreements has been
  slow. The governance gap is most acute at the international level.

  Slide 6 — Scenario — Regulatory Capture
  A persistent risk in AI governance: regulatory capture: where the entities being
  regulated gain disproportionate influence over the regulatory process. AI companies
  have significant resources to shape regulation in their favor. Through lobbying,
  through placing employees in regulatory positions, through funding research that
  supports their preferred positions, and through the genuine expertise that makes
  regulators dependent on them for technical understanding. Effective AI governance
  requires structural protections against capture.

  Slide 7 — Fact — Technical Standards
  One approach to governance that complements legislation: technical standards: agreed
  specifications for how AI systems should be evaluated, documented, and deployed. The
  National Institute of Standards and Technology in the US has developed an AI Risk
  Management Framework. ISO and IEEE are developing international AI standards.
  Standards don't have the force of law but they establish shared expectations and can
  be incorporated into contracts and regulations.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Governance Is Possible
  The history of technology governance includes success stories: nuclear
  nonproliferation, aviation safety, pharmaceutical regulation, financial regulation.
  These are imperfect but real examples of humanity governing powerful and dangerous
  technologies through international cooperation, technical standards, and regulatory
  frameworks. AI is harder in some respects. It moves faster, it's more dual-use, it's
  harder to inspect. But the basic challenge: building institutions adequate to
  governing a powerful technology: is one humanity has faced before. Success is not
  guaranteed. But it has happened before.

Module 6 — AI and Human Identity
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — The Mirror Question
  AI forces a question about human identity: what is distinctively human if machines can
  do everything humans can do? This question has a long history: every labor-saving
  technology raises it. But AI raises it more acutely because it encroaches on cognitive
  and creative domains that previous technologies left untouched. Writing, reasoning,
  making art, holding conversations: these have been central to human
  self-understanding. AI's encroachment on them forces a reckoning.

  Slide 2 — Hot take — What AI Can't Be
  AI systems don't have subjective experience, or if they do, we have no way to verify
  it. They don't have a continuous identity over time. They don't have embodied
  experience of being in a physical world. They don't have genuine relationships with
  specific others that matter to them. They don't face mortality. Whether any of these
  absences matter, whether they mean AI lacks something essential, depends on what you
  think human distinctiveness consists in.

  Slide 3 — Example — The Creativity Question
  AI can generate art, music, and writing that humans find beautiful, moving, and
  meaningful. Does this mean AI is creative? One view: creativity requires intention,
  experience, and a self that the work expresses, AI has none of these, so AI is not
  creative. Another view: creativity is about the output, not the process: if the output
  moves people, it's creative. A third view: AI is creative in a new way that requires
  new concepts rather than forcing it into existing categories.

  Slide 4 — Fact — The Relationship Question
  People form emotional connections with AI systems. Therapy chatbots help people feel
  less alone. Companion AI apps provide a sense of relationship. People anthropomorphize
  AI assistants. Whether these connections are good, harmful, or neutral is contested.
  They clearly fulfill genuine human needs for connection and understanding. Whether
  they meet those needs in ways that are sustainable and healthy, or whether they
  substitute for human connection in ways that deepen isolation: is an active research
  question.

  Slide 5 — Scenario — AI Consciousness
  Whether current or future AI systems could be conscious: could have subjective
  experience, could suffer, could have interests that matter morally: is one of the most
  difficult philosophical questions raised by AI. We have no agreed theory of
  consciousness even in humans. We have no reliable test for consciousness in systems
  very different from us. The question matters because if AI systems can suffer, we have
  obligations toward them. If they can't, we don't. We currently have no way to know.

  Slide 6 — Hot take — The Augmentation Vision
  One vision of AI and human identity: augmentation rather than replacement. AI extends
  human capabilities, not replacing human thought but enabling humans to think at larger
  scales, across more information, with better tools. On this view, AI and human
  intelligence are complements rather than substitutes. The distinctively human
  contribution, judgment, values, meaning-making, relationship, remains central. AI
  provides leverage. Humans provide direction.

  Slide 7 — Example — The Displacement Anxiety
  The opposite concern: as AI becomes capable of doing more of what humans do, the
  question of what humans are for becomes harder to answer. If AI can do your job
  better, create better art, and reason more accurately: what is your contribution? This
  displacement anxiety is psychologically real even if its premises are contested. Part
  of what makes AI different from previous automation is that it encroaches on the
  domains humans have used to feel valuable and meaningful.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — A New Chapter
  Every major technological transition has required humanity to renegotiate what it
  means to be human. Writing changed memory. Printing changed knowledge. Industrial
  machinery changed labor. Computing changed information. Each transition produced
  anxiety about loss and eventually produced a new understanding of human
  distinctiveness that incorporated the new technology. AI is another such transition:
  perhaps the largest. What it means to be human in a world with AI is a question your
  generation will answer through how you live, what you value, and what you insist on
  keeping for yourselves.

  Q1 — Easy — Fact
  "AI forces a renegotiation of what is distinctively human because it encroaches on cognitive and creative domains previous technologies left untouched."
  True · Correct.
    Previous labor-saving technologies automated physical work. AI encroaches on
    writing, reasoning, making art, holding conversations: domains central to human
    self-understanding. This makes the identity question more acute than previous

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Module 1 — The Math Behind A Neuron
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — One Neuron Does One Thing
  A single artificial neuron takes a list of numbers as input, does one mathematical
  operation, and outputs one number. That's it. Everything in modern AI — ChatGPT, image
  recognition, AlphaGo — is built from billions of this same operation running
  simultaneously. Understanding one neuron completely is understanding the atomic unit
  of all of modern AI.

  Slide 2 — Example — The Weighted Sum
  Take inputs x₁, x₂, x₃. Each has a weight: w₁, w₂, w₃. The neuron multiplies each
  input by its weight and adds them together: (x₁ × w₁) + (x₂ × w₂) + (x₃ × w₃). Then
  adds a bias term b. The result is a single number. The weights determine how much each
  input influences the output. The bias shifts the output up or down regardless of the
  inputs. This weighted sum is the core computation of every neural network.

  Slide 3 — Example — Why Weights Matter
  If w₁ is large and positive, input x₁ strongly increases the output. If w₁ is large
  and negative, x₁ strongly decreases it. If w₁ is near zero, x₁ barely matters. The
  entire knowledge of a trained neural network is encoded in its weights. When you train
  a network you are adjusting millions or billions of these numbers until the network
  produces correct outputs. The weights are what the network learns.

  Slide 4 — Fact — The Bias Term
  The bias b is added after the weighted sum. It shifts the activation threshold — how
  much total input is needed before the neuron fires strongly. Without bias, every
  neuron's output would be zero when all inputs are zero, which limits what the network
  can represent. With bias, neurons can activate even when inputs are small, or stay
  inactive even when inputs are large. Bias gives each neuron flexibility to respond to
  patterns its weights alone couldn't capture.

  Slide 5 — Example — The Activation Function
  After computing the weighted sum plus bias, the result passes through an activation
  function. The simplest: if the result is positive output it, if negative output zero.
  This is ReLU — Rectified Linear Unit. Without activation functions, stacking layers of
  neurons would just be one big linear equation — which limits what the network can
  learn. Activation functions introduce nonlinearity — the ability to represent complex
  curved relationships rather than just straight lines.

  Slide 6 — Hot take — Why Nonlinearity Matters
  Most interesting patterns in the world are nonlinear. The relationship between pixel
  values and whether an image contains a face is not a straight line. The relationship
  between words in a sentence and their meaning is not a straight line. A network made
  only of linear operations can only learn linear relationships — which is almost
  useless for real problems. Activation functions are what make neural networks capable
  of learning the complex nonlinear patterns that make them powerful.

  Slide 7 — Example — A Neuron As A Decision Boundary
  Think of a single neuron as a classifier. It takes inputs, computes a weighted sum,
  and outputs a number that says how much the inputs match some pattern. A high output
  means "yes this matches." A low output means "no it doesn't." The weights define what
  pattern the neuron is looking for. Training adjusts those weights until the neuron
  reliably detects the pattern that helps produce correct outputs. Each neuron in a
  trained network is a tiny specialized detector.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Composition
  The power of neural networks comes not from any single neuron but from composition —
  neurons feeding into neurons, each building on the previous layer's detections. One
  neuron detects a curve. Another detects that two curves are adjacent. Another detects
  that adjacent curves form an eye shape. Another detects that an eye shape and a nose
  shape together suggest a face. No single neuron does anything impressive. The
  composition of millions of simple detectors produces behavior that looks like
  understanding.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Module 2 — Forward Propagation
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — Data Flows Forward
  Forward propagation is what happens when you give a neural network an input and it
  produces an output. Data enters the first layer, gets transformed, passes to the next
  layer, gets transformed again, and continues until it reaches the output layer. Every
  transformation is the same operation — weighted sum plus bias plus activation function
  — applied by every neuron in every layer simultaneously. The forward pass is the
  network doing its job.

  Slide 2 — Example — The Input Layer
  The input layer doesn't compute anything — it just receives the raw data. For an
  image, each neuron in the input layer holds one pixel value. A 28×28 pixel image has
  784 input neurons, one per pixel. For text, inputs are token embeddings — numerical
  representations of words. For audio, inputs are numerical representations of sound
  frequencies. The first transformation is always: turn whatever you want to process
  into a list of numbers.

  Slide 3 — Example — Hidden Layers
  Between input and output are hidden layers — called hidden because you don't directly
  observe their activations. Each hidden layer neuron receives outputs from every neuron
  in the previous layer, computes its weighted sum plus bias, applies its activation
  function, and passes its output to every neuron in the next layer. In a fully
  connected network every neuron connects to every neuron in adjacent layers. The
  connections are where the weights live.

  Slide 4 — Fact — What Activations Represent
  After forward propagation, each neuron in each layer has an activation value — the
  number it's outputting. In early layers these activations represent simple features —
  edges, colors, basic shapes. In middle layers they represent combinations of simple
  features — textures, object parts. In later layers they represent complex concepts —
  "eye-like region," "face-like structure," "this is probably a dog." The activations
  become more abstract and more meaningful as data flows deeper.

  Slide 5 — Hot take — The Network Doesn't Know What It's Representing
  The neurons in a trained network don't know they're detecting eyes or faces. They
  don't have labels. They have weights that happen to produce useful activations for the
  task they were trained on. When we say "this neuron detects edges" we're describing
  what we observe about its behavior — not a property the neuron has. The network has no
  internal representation of what anything means. It has weights that produce correct
  outputs.

  Slide 6 — Example — The Output Layer
  The final layer produces the network's output. For image classification, the output
  layer has one neuron per possible class — one for cat, one for dog, one for bird. Each
  neuron outputs a number representing how likely the input is that class. The softmax
  activation function converts these numbers into probabilities that sum to 1. The class
  with the highest probability is the network's prediction. For language models, the
  output layer has one neuron per token in the vocabulary — usually tens of thousands.

  Slide 7 — Scenario — What Happens During A ChatGPT Response
  When you send ChatGPT a message, your text is converted to tokens, each token to a
  numerical embedding, these feed into the first transformer layer, each layer
  transforms the representations, after hundreds of layers the final layer outputs a
  probability distribution over every possible next token, the highest probability token
  is selected and added to the sequence, then the whole process repeats for the next
  token. Your entire conversation is reprocessed from scratch at each step. That's the
  forward pass — happening in milliseconds.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Representation Learning
  The most important insight about forward propagation: what the network is really doing
  is learning representations. Raw inputs — pixels, tokens, audio samples — are not
  directly useful for making predictions. What's useful is a representation of those
  inputs that captures the features that matter for the task. Each layer transforms the
  representation into something more useful for the next layer. Deep networks are
  powerful because they learn rich hierarchical representations automatically from data.
  This is representation learning — the core of what makes neural networks work.

QUIZ

  Q1 — Easy — Fact
  "Forward propagation is the process of data flowing from input to output through a neural network."
  True · Correct.
    Input data enters the first layer, gets transformed by each neuron, passes to the
    next layer, and continues until reaching the output. Every transformation is the
    weighted sum plus bias plus activation function. The forward pass is the network
    producing a prediction from an input.

  Q2 — Easy — Myth bust
  "The input layer of a neural network performs complex transformations on the raw data."
  False · Wrong.
    The input layer doesn't compute anything — it receives and holds the raw data. For
    images, each neuron holds one pixel value. The first actual computation happens in
    the first hidden layer. The input layer is an interface between raw data and the
    computational network.

  Q3 — Easy — Scenario
  "In a language model like ChatGPT, your entire conversation is reprocessed from scratch at each step when generating a response."
  True · Correct.
    Each new token requires a complete forward pass through the entire model using all
    previous tokens as context. The model has no persistent state between steps — all
    context must be in the input sequence processed by the forward pass.

  Q4 — Medium — Myth bust
  "Hidden layers are called 'hidden' because their activations are kept secret by the model's developers."
  False · Wrong.
    They're called hidden because you don't directly observe or control their

    Interpretability researchers do inspect them. The name refers to their position in
    the architecture, not any secrecy.

  Q5 — Medium — Fact
  "Activations in early layers of a deep network typically represent simple features, while later layers represent more complex and abstract features."
  True · Correct.
    Early layers detect edges and basic patterns. Middle layers combine these into
    shapes and textures. Later layers combine these into object parts and concepts. The

    learning complex patterns.

  Q6 — Medium — Example
  "The softmax function used in output layers ensures that all output values sum to 1, forming a probability distribution."
  True · Correct.

  "Representation learning refers to the fact that neural networks learn to transform raw inputs into increasingly useful intermediate representations."
  True · Correct.

  False · Wrong.

    the neuron has. The network has no internal representation of what anything means.
    It has weights that produce correct outputs. The interpretation is ours, not the
    network's.

Module 3 — Loss Functions
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — What A Loss Function Is
  A loss function measures how wrong the network's output is. It takes the network's
  prediction and the correct answer and produces a single number — the loss —
  representing the error. High loss means the prediction was far from correct. Low loss
  means it was close. The entire training process is about minimizing this number.
  Without a loss function there's no signal to learn from — the network has no way to
  know how wrong it is.

  Slide 2 — Example — Mean Squared Error
  The simplest loss function: mean squared error. For a regression task — predicting a
  continuous value like house price — compute the difference between predicted and
  actual value, square it, average across all examples. Squaring serves two purposes: it
  makes all errors positive, and it penalizes large errors more than small ones. A
  prediction that's twice as far from the truth produces four times the loss. This
  pushes the network to avoid large mistakes.

  Slide 3 — Example — Cross-Entropy Loss
  For classification tasks — predicting which category something belongs to —
  cross-entropy loss is standard. It measures how surprised the network would be by the
  correct answer given its probability distribution. If the network predicted 90%
  probability for the correct class, the loss is low — not very surprised. If it
  predicted 5% probability for the correct class, the loss is high — very surprised.
  Cross-entropy penalizes confident wrong predictions more severely than uncertain wrong
  predictions.

  Slide 4 — Hot take — The Loss Function Defines What The Network Cares About
  Whatever you put in the loss function is what the network optimizes. Nothing else. If
  your loss function measures accuracy on a test set, the network optimizes test
  accuracy — which might involve memorizing rather than generalizing. If your loss
  function measures engagement, the network optimizes engagement — which might mean
  maximizing outrage. The loss function is the most important design decision in
  building an AI system. Every misalignment failure traces back to a loss function that
  didn't capture what you actually wanted.

  Slide 5 — Fact — The Loss Landscape
  Imagine a landscape where every point represents a possible set of weights and the
  height represents the loss for that set of weights. Training is the process of finding
  the lowest point in this landscape — the weights that produce minimum loss. For a
  network with billions of parameters, this landscape has billions of dimensions.
  Finding the minimum is not trivial. Gradient descent is the algorithm that navigates
  it — but the landscape has many local minima, saddle points, and flat regions that
  make navigation difficult.

  Slide 6 — Example — Regularization In The Loss
  Regularization adds a penalty to the loss function for large weights. The most common:
  L2 regularization adds the sum of squared weights to the loss. This encourages the
  network to use smaller weights — which tends to prevent overfitting by discouraging
  the network from relying too heavily on any single feature. The total loss becomes:
  task loss plus regularization penalty. The network now optimizes not just for correct
  predictions but for correct predictions with simple weights.

  Slide 7 — Scenario — Loss Functions In Language Models
  The loss function for training a language model like GPT is simple: predict the next
  token. Given a sequence of tokens, the model predicts what comes next. The loss is the
  cross-entropy between the model's predicted distribution and the actual next token. Do
  this for every position in every sequence in the training data — hundreds of billions
  of predictions. The model that minimizes this loss has learned, as a side effect, an
  enormous amount about language, facts, reasoning, and the world. All from predicting
  the next word.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Loss As Specification
  The loss function is the clearest example of the alignment problem in concrete
  technical form. You cannot optimize for something you can't measure. If you can
  measure it precisely you can optimize for it. But the things you actually care about —
  helpful responses, fair decisions, beneficial outcomes — are often hard to measure
  precisely. So you proxy them with things that are measurable — task accuracy,
  engagement, user ratings. The proxy is never perfect. The gap between the proxy and
  the real objective is where misalignment lives.

  True · Correct.

Module 4 — Backpropagation In Detail
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — The Learning Algorithm
  Backpropagation is the algorithm that trains neural networks. It computes how much
  each weight contributed to the loss and adjusts each weight in the direction that
  reduces the loss. Without backpropagation — or something equivalent — there's no
  efficient way to train deep networks. It was known theoretically in the 1970s,
  demonstrated in the 1980s, and scaled to transform the field in 2012. Most of modern
  AI runs on this algorithm.

  Slide 2 — Example — Gradient Descent
  Backpropagation computes gradients — the direction and magnitude of how the loss
  changes with respect to each weight. Gradient descent uses these to update weights:
  new weight = old weight minus learning rate times gradient. The learning rate controls
  how big each update step is. Too large and training overshoots. Too small and training
  is slow. The gradient tells you which direction to move. The learning rate tells you
  how far.

  Slide 3 — Example — The Chain Rule
  Backpropagation uses calculus — specifically the chain rule. To know how much weight w
  in layer 1 contributed to the final loss, you need to trace the effect of w through
  every subsequent layer to the output. The chain rule lets you compute this by
  multiplying the local gradient at each step. The gradient of the loss with respect to
  w equals the gradient of the loss with respect to the next layer's output, times the
  gradient of that output with respect to w. Repeat backwards through every layer.

  Slide 4 — Hot take — Why Backpropagation Was Controversial
  In the 1980s many researchers doubted backpropagation would work for deep networks.
  The vanishing gradient problem: as gradients are multiplied through many layers, they
  tend to shrink toward zero in early layers — those layers stop learning. This limited
  networks to shallow architectures for decades. The problem was solved through better
  activation functions, better weight initialization, residual connections, and batch
  normalization. Modern deep learning is largely a story of solving the problems that
  once made deep networks untrainable.

  Slide 5 — Fact — Stochastic Gradient Descent
  Computing the gradient over the entire training dataset before each update is
  expensive. Stochastic gradient descent computes the gradient on a single randomly
  selected example instead. This is noisy but fast. Mini-batch gradient descent — the
  most common approach — computes the gradient on a small batch of examples, typically
  32 to 512. Batches provide a balance between the accuracy of full-dataset gradients
  and the speed of single-example gradients.

  Slide 6 — Example — Optimizers
  Plain gradient descent often trains slowly or gets stuck. Optimizers improve on it.
  Momentum accumulates past gradients to build speed in consistent directions — like a
  ball rolling downhill. Adam combines momentum with adaptive learning rates — larger
  steps for weights that haven't changed much, smaller steps for weights that change
  frequently. Most large models are trained with Adam or its variants. The choice of
  optimizer significantly affects training speed and final performance.

  Slide 7 — Scenario — What Happens In One Training Step
  One complete training step: sample a batch of examples from the training data. Run
  forward propagation — compute activations for every neuron in every layer. Compute the
  loss — how wrong was the output? Run backpropagation — compute the gradient of the
  loss with respect to every weight in the network. Update every weight using the
  optimizer. For a large language model this involves computing gradients for hundreds
  of billions of parameters. Do this millions of times. That's training.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Credit Assignment
  The fundamental problem backpropagation solves is credit assignment — given that the
  network made a wrong prediction, which of the billions of weights are responsible and
  by how much? Each weight influenced the output through a complex chain of computations
  involving every subsequent weight. The chain rule lets you assign credit precisely —
  compute exactly how much each weight contributed to the error. This precise credit
  assignment is what makes gradient-based learning work. Without it, training would
  require random search — trying random weight updates and keeping ones that help.
  Backpropagation makes training millions of times more efficient.

  Q1 — Easy — Fact
  "Backpropagation computes how much each weight contributed to the loss and adjusts weights to reduce that loss."
  True · Correct.
    It solves the credit assignment problem using the chain rule of calculus. It
    computes gradients for every weight in the network and uses these to update weights
    in the direction that reduces loss. This is the algorithm that makes training neural
    networks possible.

  Q2 — Easy — Example
  "The learning rate controls how large each weight update step is during training."
  True · Correct.
    New weight = old weight minus learning rate times gradient. Too large a learning
    rate and training overshoots good solutions. Too small and training is slow. Modern
    optimizers adapt the learning rate per parameter, but the basic principle holds.

  Q3 — Easy — Myth bust
  "Stochastic gradient descent computes gradients on the entire training dataset before each weight update."
  False · Wrong.
    That would be full-batch gradient descent. Stochastic gradient descent uses a single
    randomly selected example per update. Mini-batch gradient descent — the most common
    approach — uses small batches, balancing accuracy of full-dataset gradients with
    computational efficiency.

  Q4 — Medium — Example
  "The chain rule is used in backpropagation to compute how a weight in an early layer contributed to the final loss through all subsequent layers."
  True · Correct.
    The effect of a weight in layer 1 passes through every subsequent layer. The chain
    rule computes this by multiplying local gradients at each step. This makes gradient
    computation tractable in deep networks — without it you'd need to numerically
    estimate gradients for each weight separately.

  Q5 — Medium — Hot take
  "The vanishing gradient problem caused gradients to grow very large in early layers of deep networks, making training unstable."
  False · Wrong.
    The vanishing gradient problem causes gradients to shrink — approach zero — in early
    layers. As gradients are multiplied through many layers they get smaller. Near-zero
    gradients mean those layers stop learning. This limited networks to shallow
    architectures for decades.

  Q6 — Medium — Example
  "Momentum in optimization accumulates past gradients to build speed in consistent directions, similar to a ball rolling downhill."
  True · Correct.
    Rather than updating based only on the current gradient, momentum maintains a
    running average of past gradients. In directions where gradients consistently point
    the same way, momentum builds speed. In directions where they oscillate, it dampens
    them. This produces faster convergence than plain gradient descent.

  Q7 — Hard — Example
  "The Adam optimizer adapts learning rates per parameter — larger steps for parameters that haven't changed much, smaller steps for those that change frequently."
  True · Correct.
    Adam combines momentum with adaptive per-parameter learning rates. Parameters with
    consistently large gradients get smaller learning rates. Parameters with
    consistently small gradients get larger learning rates. This makes Adam robust
    across different types of parameters and loss landscapes.

  Q8 — Hard — Big idea
  "Without backpropagation, training neural networks would require random search — trying random weight updates and keeping ones that help — which would be millions of times less efficient."
  True · Correct.
    Backpropagation's key contribution is efficient credit assignment. For a network
    with billions of parameters, computing gradients analytically rather than through
    random search makes training tractable. Without it, modern large-scale neural
    networks would be computationally impossible.

Module 5 — Activation Functions
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — Why Activation Functions Exist
  Without activation functions, a neural network — no matter how many layers — is
  equivalent to a single linear transformation. Stack as many linear layers as you want,
  you still get a linear function. Linear functions can only learn linear relationships.
  The real world is full of nonlinear relationships. Activation functions introduce
  nonlinearity — the mathematical property that allows neural networks to approximate
  any function, including the complex ones that describe real patterns in data.

  Slide 2 — Example — Sigmoid
  The sigmoid function maps any input to a value between 0 and 1. Output = 1 / (1 +
  e^(-x)). Historically important — used in early neural networks and still used in
  output layers for binary classification. Problem: for very large or very small inputs
  the sigmoid saturates — its gradient approaches zero. During backpropagation,
  near-zero gradients multiplied through many layers produce vanishing gradients. This
  is why sigmoid fell out of favor for hidden layers in deep networks.

  Slide 3 — Example — ReLU
  Rectified Linear Unit: output = max(0, x). If input is positive, output equals input.
  If input is negative, output is zero. Simple, fast, and effective. ReLU doesn't
  saturate for positive inputs — gradients flow cleanly through layers. It introduced a
  massive practical improvement in training deep networks when it became standard around
  2012. One problem: neurons with negative inputs output zero and have zero gradient —
  the dying ReLU problem. Variants like Leaky ReLU address this by allowing a small
  gradient for negative inputs.

  Slide 4 — Example — Softmax
  Softmax is used in output layers for multiclass classification. It takes a vector of
  numbers and converts them to a probability distribution — all values between 0 and 1,
  summing to 1. The largest input gets the largest probability. Softmax is
  differentiable, which means gradients flow through it during backpropagation. It's
  used in the final layer of image classifiers and in the attention mechanism of
  transformers. You've encountered its output every time a language model assigns
  probabilities to possible next tokens.

  Slide 5 — Fact — GELU and Modern Activations
  Gaussian Error Linear Unit — GELU — is used in most modern large language models
  including GPT. Unlike ReLU it doesn't have a hard cutoff at zero — it applies a smooth
  approximation that allows small gradients for negative inputs. GELU tends to produce
  better performance than ReLU on language tasks. The exact reasons are not fully
  understood. In practice, different activation functions work better for different
  architectures and tasks, and the best choice is often determined empirically.

  Slide 6 — Hot take — The Activation Function Is A Design Choice
  The choice of activation function is a hyperparameter — a design choice made before
  training that affects what the network can learn and how easily it trains. These
  choices are not derived from first principles. They are determined by experimentation,
  accumulated intuition, and sometimes mathematical analysis of properties like gradient
  flow. The history of neural networks includes multiple activation function revolutions
  — each enabling deeper networks or better performance. This is typical of the field:
  empirical discoveries rather than deductive proofs.

  Slide 7 — Example — Activation Functions In Transformers
  Modern transformers use different activation functions in different places. Attention
  uses softmax to produce probability distributions over positions. Feed-forward layers
  use GELU or similar smooth nonlinearities. Output layers use softmax again for token
  predictions. The choice of different activations for different components reflects
  their different mathematical roles — probability distributions need softmax, hidden
  representations benefit from smooth nonlinearities, gating mechanisms benefit from
  sigmoid. Each component is designed for its specific function.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Inductive Biases
  Every design choice in a neural network — including activation function — encodes an
  inductive bias: an assumption about what kind of solution is being sought. ReLU
  assumes that negative activations should be suppressed. Softmax assumes outputs should
  be a probability distribution. These assumptions are not neutral — they shape what the
  network can learn and how it learns it. Understanding neural networks means
  understanding the inductive biases encoded in their design choices, because those
  biases determine what patterns the network is capable of finding.

Module 6 — Attention Mechanisms
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — The Attention Revolution
  The attention mechanism, introduced in a 2017 paper titled "Attention Is All You
  Need," fundamentally changed AI. Before attention, networks processed sequences step
  by step and struggled to connect information from far apart in a sequence. Attention
  allows every position in a sequence to directly attend to every other position —
  computing how relevant each position is to every other. This single innovation enabled
  transformers, which enabled GPT, which enabled the current AI moment.

  Slide 2 — Example — The Intuition
  Imagine reading: "The animal didn't cross the street because it was too tired." What
  does "it" refer to? You instantly know — the animal, not the street. You did this by
  attending to "animal" and "tired" and recognizing their connection. Attention in
  neural networks does something analogous — for each word in a sequence, it computes a
  weighted sum of all other words' representations, with higher weights for more
  relevant words. "It" attends strongly to "animal" because that connection produces
  useful representations.

  Slide 3 — Example — Query, Key, Value
  Attention uses three learned matrices to project each token's representation into
  three spaces: Query (Q), Key (K), and Value (V). For each position, the query
  represents "what am I looking for?" Keys represent "what does each position have to
  offer?" Values represent "what information does each position contain?" Attention
  scores are computed as the dot product of queries and keys — measuring relevance.
  These scores are softmaxed to produce weights, which are applied to values. The output
  is a weighted sum of all values, weighted by relevance.

  Slide 4 — Fact — Scaled Dot-Product Attention
  The full formula: Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(QK^T / √d_k)V. The division by √d_k —
  the square root of the key dimension — scales the dot products to prevent them from
  getting very large when dimensions are high. Large dot products push softmax into
  regions with very small gradients, making training slow. Scaling keeps gradients
  healthy. This is one of many implementation details that separate a theoretical idea
  from a working system.

  Slide 5 — Example — Multi-Head Attention
  Rather than computing attention once, transformers compute it multiple times in
  parallel — each "head" with different Q, K, V matrices. Each head can attend to
  different aspects of the sequence. One head might capture syntactic relationships.
  Another might capture semantic relationships. Another might track coreference — what
  "it" refers to. The outputs of all heads are concatenated and linearly projected.
  Multi-head attention allows the network to simultaneously represent multiple types of
  relationships between positions.

  Slide 6 — Hot take — Attention Is Expensive
  Computing attention requires comparing every position to every other position — a
  quadratic complexity in sequence length. For a sequence of 1000 tokens, that's
  1,000,000 comparisons per layer. For sequences of tens of thousands of tokens,
  attention becomes extremely expensive. This is one of the main limitations of
  transformer architectures — processing very long documents or maintaining long context
  requires significant computational resources. Much research is devoted to making
  attention more efficient.

  Slide 7 — Scenario — What Attention Does In GPT
  When GPT generates a response, each layer of the transformer applies multi-head
  attention to the entire sequence so far. Each token's representation is updated based
  on how it attends to all previous tokens. Later layers build on earlier ones — early
  layers capture local syntax, later layers capture long-range semantic relationships.
  By the final layer, each position's representation has been shaped by every other
  position in the context. The probability distribution over the next token is computed
  from this richly contextualized representation.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Context As Computation
  The key insight of attention is that meaning is contextual — the representation of any
  part of a sequence should be informed by its relationship to every other part. A word
  doesn't mean the same thing in isolation and in context. A pixel doesn't have the same
  significance alone and in relation to surrounding pixels. Attention is a mechanism for
  computing context-dependent representations — allowing each element to be interpreted
  in light of everything else. This is why transformers are powerful for language,
  vision, and any domain where context matters. Which is almost every domain.

  Q1 — Easy — Fact

  True · Correct.

Module 7 — The Transformer Architecture
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — The Architecture That Changed Everything
  The transformer architecture, introduced in 2017, is the foundation of almost every
  major AI system built since then. GPT, BERT, T5, PaLM, Claude, Gemini — all
  transformers. AlphaFold — transformer. The image models underlying DALL-E —
  transformer. The architecture is not the only approach to AI, but it is currently
  dominant across almost every domain where AI has achieved remarkable performance.

  Slide 2 — Example — The Encoder-Decoder Structure
  The original transformer had two components: an encoder that processes the input
  sequence and builds representations, and a decoder that generates the output sequence
  one token at a time while attending to the encoder's representations. This structure
  is natural for translation — encode the source language, decode into the target
  language. GPT-style models use only the decoder — generating text by predicting the
  next token. BERT-style models use only the encoder — building rich representations for
  classification tasks.

  Slide 3 — Fact — Positional Encoding
  Attention has no inherent sense of order — it treats every position symmetrically.
  "The cat sat" and "sat cat The" would produce the same attention scores without
  positional information. Positional encoding adds order information to token embeddings
  before they enter the transformer. The original paper used sine and cosine functions
  of different frequencies. Modern models often learn positional encodings directly.
  Without positional encoding, transformers can't distinguish between different
  orderings of the same tokens.

  Slide 4 — Example — The Transformer Layer
  Each transformer layer has two sublayers. The first is multi-head self-attention —
  each token attends to all other tokens and updates its representation. The second is a
  feed-forward network — two linear transformations with a nonlinear activation applied
  independently to each position. Both sublayers use residual connections — adding the
  input to the output — and layer normalization. The residual connections are what make
  training very deep transformers possible by preventing vanishing gradients.

  Slide 5 — Hot take — The Feed-Forward Layer Is Underappreciated
  Most discussion of transformers focuses on attention. The feed-forward layers — which
  constitute about two-thirds of a transformer's parameters — are less discussed but
  equally important. Research suggests they act as a kind of associative memory —
  storing factual associations learned during training. When a language model knows that
  Paris is the capital of France, that knowledge is likely stored in the feed-forward
  layers, not the attention mechanism. The transformer's knowledge is distributed
  between its attention patterns and its feed-forward memories.

  Slide 6 — Fact — Scale And Emergence
  Large transformers exhibit emergent capabilities — abilities that appear suddenly at
  scale that weren't present in smaller models. Models below a certain size fail
  completely at some tasks. Above a threshold, performance jumps. Chain-of-thought
  reasoning, arithmetic, in-context learning, instruction following — all emerged at
  scale. These emergent capabilities were not designed in and were not predicted. They
  appear to be properties of very large learned representations that we don't fully
  understand. Emergence is one of the most surprising and least understood aspects of
  large transformers.

  Slide 7 — Scenario — Why GPT-4 Is Different From GPT-2
  GPT-2 (2019) had 1.5 billion parameters. GPT-4 (2023) is estimated at over 1 trillion.
  The architecture is similar. The scale is different by three orders of magnitude.
  Between those two models: the ability to follow complex instructions, to reason
  through multi-step problems, to write code, to understand nuance, to maintain coherent
  long conversations, to learn from examples in context. These capabilities did not
  exist at GPT-2 scale. Scale didn't just improve existing capabilities — it created new
  ones. This is why the trajectory of transformer scaling matters so much.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Architecture As Hypothesis
  The transformer architecture is a hypothesis about what structure is useful for
  learning from data. The hypothesis is: sequences of tokens, processed by alternating
  attention and feed-forward layers, with residual connections and layer normalization,
  at sufficient scale. This hypothesis has been validated empirically across an enormous
  range of tasks and domains. But it's not the only possible hypothesis. Researchers are
  exploring alternatives — state space models, mixture of experts, diffusion models for
  language. The current dominance of transformers reflects empirical success, not
  theoretical inevitability.

QUIZ

  Q1 — Easy — Fact
  "The transformer architecture is the foundation of most major AI systems built since 2017."
  True · Correct.
    GPT, BERT, T5, PaLM, Claude, Gemini — all transformers. AlphaFold — transformer.
    Image generation models — transformer-based. The architecture is dominant across
    language, vision, protein structure prediction, and many other domains.

  Q2 — Easy — Myth bust
  "GPT-style models use only the encoder component of the original transformer architecture."
  False · Wrong.
    GPT-style models use only the decoder. The encoder builds representations. The
    decoder generates output sequences token by token. GPT generates text by predicting
    the next token autoregressively — a decoder operation. BERT, which builds
    representations for classification, uses only the encoder.

  Q3 — Easy — Fact
  "Without positional encoding, a transformer would treat the same words in different orders identically."
  True · Correct.
    Attention treats all positions symmetrically. Without positional information, 'the
    cat sat' and 'sat cat the' would produce the same attention scores. Positional
    encoding adds order information, allowing the model to distinguish positions and
    represent the sequential structure fundamental to language.

  Q4 — Medium — Example
  "Residual connections in transformer layers add the layer's input to its output, which helps prevent vanishing gradients in deep networks."
  True · Correct.
    Rather than just passing the transformed representation forward, residual
    connections add the original input: output = layer(input) + input. This provides a
    gradient highway through the network. Residual connections are what make training
    very deep transformers stable.

  Q5 — Medium — Myth bust
  "The feed-forward layers in transformers are less important than attention layers and could be removed without significant performance loss."
  False · Wrong.
    Feed-forward layers constitute about two-thirds of a transformer's parameters and
    are essential. Research suggests they act as associative memory — storing factual
    knowledge. Removing them would dramatically reduce capacity to store and retrieve
    information.

  Q6 — Medium — Myth bust
  "Emergent capabilities in large language models are capabilities that were explicitly programmed in as the models scaled up."
  False · Wrong.
    Emergent capabilities appear at scale without being designed in and without being
    predicted. Chain-of-thought reasoning, arithmetic, in-context learning — all emerged
    in larger models without being explicitly trained for. Emergence is discovered
    empirically.

  Q7 — Hard — Big idea
  "The transformer architecture is a hypothesis about what structure is useful for learning from data — validated empirically but not the only possible hypothesis."
  True · Correct.

  Q8 — Hard — Myth bust
  "The capability gap between GPT-2 and GPT-4 demonstrates that scaling produces only quantitative improvements in existing capabilities, not qualitative new ones."
  False · Wrong.

Module 8 — Training At Scale
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SLIDES

  Slide 1 — Fact — What Training A Large Model Actually Involves
  Training GPT-4 required thousands of specialized AI processors running continuously
  for months. The training data was hundreds of billions of words. The model has
  hundreds of billions of parameters. Each training step involves a forward pass
  computing activations for every parameter, a loss computation, a backward pass
  computing gradients for every parameter, and a parameter update. Do this billions of
  times. The compute cost is estimated in the hundreds of millions of dollars. Training
  at scale is an industrial process.

  Slide 2 — Example — Learning Rate Schedules
  The learning rate — how much to adjust weights per step — doesn't stay constant during
  training. Learning rate schedules change it systematically. A common approach: warm up
  — start with a very small learning rate and increase it over the first few thousand
  steps, allowing the model to find a good region before taking large steps. Then decay
  — gradually decrease the learning rate as training progresses, taking smaller steps as
  the model converges. The schedule significantly affects final performance and training
  stability.

  Slide 3 — Example — Batch Normalization And Layer Normalization
  Deep networks are hard to train partly because the distribution of activations changes
  as weights change — early layers keep shifting the input distribution that later
  layers see. Normalization layers address this by standardizing activations within each
  batch or each layer. Layer normalization — used in transformers — normalizes across
  the features of each individual example. This stabilizes training and allows much
  higher learning rates. Without normalization, training very deep networks tends to be
  unstable.

  Slide 4 — Fact — Dropout
  Dropout randomly sets a fraction of neurons' activations to zero during training. This
  prevents neurons from co-adapting — becoming dependent on specific other neurons. It
  forces the network to learn redundant representations — multiple ways to represent the
  same information — which improves generalization. Dropout is not used during inference
  — only during training. It acts as a form of ensemble learning: training many
  different subnetworks simultaneously and averaging their predictions.

  Slide 5 — Hot take — Data Quality Matters More Than Quantity
  The conventional wisdom was that more data always helps. Recent research has
  complicated this. Training on filtered, high-quality data often outperforms training
  on more data of lower quality. The Chinchilla scaling laws (2022) showed that most
  large models at the time were undertrained on too little data relative to their size —
  more tokens per parameter would have been more efficient. Data curation — deciding
  what to train on and what to exclude — is one of the most important and least
  discussed aspects of training large models.

  Slide 6 — Example — Distributed Training
  No single processor can hold the parameters of a 100-billion parameter model in
  memory. Distributed training splits the model across many processors. Model
  parallelism splits the model itself — different layers or components run on different
  devices. Data parallelism runs copies of the model on different devices, each
  processing different data, with gradients synchronized. Pipeline parallelism chains
  stages of the forward and backward pass. Modern large model training uses combinations
  of all three. Coordinating billions of operations across thousands of processors is an
  engineering challenge as much as a research one.

  Slide 7 — Scenario — What Happens After Pretraining
  Pretraining produces a model that predicts text — but not necessarily one that follows
  instructions helpfully. After pretraining, large language models typically undergo
  fine-tuning. Supervised fine-tuning on examples of desired behavior. Then RLHF —
  reinforcement learning from human feedback — trains the model to produce outputs
  humans rate as helpful, harmless, and honest. The pretrained model provides broad
  capabilities. Fine-tuning and RLHF shape how those capabilities are applied. ChatGPT
  and Claude are not just pretrained models — they are pretrained models shaped by
  significant post-training.

  Slide 8 — Big idea — Engineering At The Frontier
  Training large AI models is frontier engineering — working at the edge of what
  hardware, software, and mathematical understanding make possible. Every component
  matters: the architecture, the data, the loss function, the optimizer, the learning
  rate schedule, the normalization strategy, the regularization. A mistake in any of
  these can make training unstable, slow convergence, or produce a model that performs
  poorly. The researchers who build frontier models are not just applying known
  techniques — they are discovering what works through careful experimentation at
  enormous scale. Modern AI is simultaneously a scientific discipline, an engineering
  practice, and an empirical art.
